# Internet Studies and Sociocybernetics

## From "Social Media" to Post-Social Formations

A clear arc runs through these papers: the conceptual scaffolding built in the 2000s and 2010s for studying "social media" is being dismantled, both by empirical drift in the platforms themselves and by the second-order recognition that our analytic categories were always provisional. [[Boyd2026-op]] makes the terminological case most directly, arguing that the "social" in social media has become a misnomer — reciprocal posting has given way to scrolling, and influencer-mediated parasociality has displaced peer exchange. [[Tornberg2025-ir]] generalizes this diagnosis into a programmatic call for a "post-social media studies," identifying three concurrent dissolutions: the move from social-graph to interest-based recommendation, the substitution of generative AI for user-generated content, and the retreat into semi-private spheres. [[Baym2026-tr]] reads the same period through political economy, finding that the structural threats identified a decade ago — wealth concentration, opaque algorithms, precarious creative labor, data extraction — have not merely persisted but compounded into a "broligarchy."

Together these three pieces stage a shared retrospective: the field's foundational vocabulary (networked publics, participatory culture, user-generated content) was contingent on a platform configuration that no longer obtains. Where boyd and Baym write as self-aware participants revisiting their own coinages, Törnberg and Rogers push furthest, claiming the emergent formations — algorithmic broadcasting, micro-communities, AI-mediated communication without publics — require not just new vocabulary but new methods and ethics.

## Sociocybernetic Resources for a Moving Target

The methodological anxiety running through these essays is, at root, an epistemological one, and this is where the older sociocybernetic tradition becomes relevant. [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] explicitly mobilizes second-order cybernetics (von Foerster, Bateson) and Luhmann's systems theory against the creator-centric framing of "fake news," reorienting analysis toward propagators and the recursive judgments by which information cycles take shape. The move treats information as observer-dependent and emergent — a stance that anticipates exactly the problem Törnberg and Rogers describe when AI-mediated communication produces no shared text and no public trace to analyze. If communication is increasingly individualized and generative, then a propagation-centric, multi-level model becomes more rather than less necessary.

## Political Communication as Test Case

[[F2020-6278a4aa]] occupies an intermediate position, treating political communication as the empirical domain where these abstract transformations bite. The three vectors it identifies — blurred organizational boundaries, micro-targeted advertising, and strategic amplification — operationalize the broader claims of [[Baym2026-tr]] and [[Tornberg2025-ir]] in a concrete regulatory and methodological register. The argument that traditional self-report and source-list methodologies fail in high-choice, incidentally exposed environments echoes Giglietto's earlier insistence that propagation cycles, not creators, are the proper object — and it dovetails with boyd's worry that calling something "social media" prejudges how we study it.

## Points of Convergence and Tension

Across the five papers, a convergence is visible: passive consumption, algorithmic curation, and the erosion of shared epistemic infrastructure are now treated as defining rather than peripheral. Yet tensions remain. [[Boyd2026-op]] proposes a relabeling ("parasocial media") that retains a single unifying category, while [[Tornberg2025-ir]] insists the field fragment into three distinct formations each demanding its own apparatus. [[Baym2026-tr]] is skeptical that scholarly relabeling matters at all without cross-sector intervention. And [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] suggests that the deeper problem is not naming but the unwarranted expectation of societal consensus — a Luhmannian point that cuts against any program presuming a recoverable shared public. The structure-note question this topic poses is thus whether sociocybernetic, observer-centric epistemology can supply the theoretical backbone that the post-social-media program currently lacks.
