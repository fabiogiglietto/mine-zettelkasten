# Online Radicalization, Extremism, and the Far Right on Social Media

## From content to infrastructure: the shifting object of online radicalization research

The three papers gathered here mark a maturing field that is moving away from asking *whether* the internet radicalizes and toward more granular questions about *how* extremist content is produced, circulated, and rendered ordinary within specific platform environments. [[Rothut2026-wt]] provides the macro-view: a 25-year scoping review of 382 publications showing that the field has undergone an "empirical turn" since 2018, that Islamist and far-right extremism dominate the corpus, and that the once-tidy distinction between online and offline radicalization is no longer tenable. [[Nangle2026-yo]] and [[Karo2026-dn]] can be read as exemplars of exactly the kind of phenomenologically differentiated, platform-specific, computational-qualitative work that [[Rothut2026-wt]] identifies as the field's growth edge.

## Affective normalization and "everyday extremism"

A striking convergence between [[Nangle2026-yo]] and [[Karo2026-dn]] is that contemporary extremist communication on visual, algorithmically curated platforms does not look like terrorist spectacle. On Instagram, [[Nangle2026-yo]] shows far-right actors mobilising positive affect, humour, and memes to soften and mainstream ideology within an "affective-discursive ecology." On TikTok, [[Karo2026-dn]] documents how jihadist supporters embed propaganda in Roblox memes, trending sounds, Pride Month clips, and football highlights — a "collapse of spectacle and ritual" into platform vernacular that the authors call *everyday extremism*. Both papers thereby reinforce [[Rothut2026-wt]]'s meso-level observation that emotional convergence and community vernaculars, rather than overt hostility, are central to how radical content travels. They also broaden the canonical communicator/content/recipient model: meaning is co-produced with platform aesthetics and audience competencies.

## Algorithms, affordances, and the limits of moderation

Both empirical studies foreground the *infrastructural* layer that [[Rothut2026-wt]] flags as understudied at the macro level. [[Nangle2026-yo]] argues that Instagram's recommendation logics produce "algorithmic proximity" between mainstream and far-right adjacent content, supporting mobilisation through visibility rather than through dedicated extremist enclaves. [[Karo2026-dn]] catalogues five concrete evasion strategies — audio camouflage, meme infiltration, blurred intent, emoji codes, and bait-and-switch — that exploit basic gaps in TikTok's moderation stack (e.g., "1S1 S" bypassing keyword filters). Crucially, [[Karo2026-dn]] notes that these adversarial creative strategies are not ideology-specific, recurring across far-right, anti-vaccine, and jihadist communities. This dovetails with [[Rothut2026-wt]]'s argument that the field is increasingly transcending traditional left–right typologies in favour of cross-cutting mechanisms.

## Methodological frontiers and open gaps

Methodologically, the two empirical pieces illustrate the rise of mixed and computational approaches that [[Rothut2026-wt]] tracks: [[Nangle2026-yo]] combines algorithmic ethnography with multimodal discourse analysis across 2,603 Reels, while [[Karo2026-dn]] uses algorithmically assisted snowball sampling from a dedicated research account paired with grounded-theory coding. Yet both remain cross-sectional and observational — precisely the limitation [[Rothut2026-wt]] singles out as the field's most persistent: longitudinal and causal evidence on radicalization outcomes is still scarce, and recipient-level effects depend heavily on prior dispositions and protective factors that platform-content studies cannot directly measure.

## Toward an infrastructural research and policy agenda

Read together, the three papers sketch a coherent normative arc. If radicalization is hybrid, multi-level, and increasingly mediated by affect and algorithmic curation, then content-removal regimes are structurally inadequate. [[Karo2026-dn]] makes this explicit, calling for a shift from content moderation to infrastructural critique and culturally informed, coordinated public–private governance; [[Nangle2026-yo]] implies a similar move by treating platform logics, ideology, and emotion as mutually constitutive rather than separable. [[Rothut2026-wt]] generalises the point: prevention requires multi-faceted approaches that engage macro-level platform architectures, meso-level community dynamics, and micro-level vulnerabilities simultaneously — and a research infrastructure capable of studying alternative platforms, gaming environments, and longitudinal trajectories that current scholarship still largely neglects.
