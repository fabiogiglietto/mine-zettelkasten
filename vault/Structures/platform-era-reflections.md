# Twenty-Year Reflections on Social Media's Trajectory

## From Social to Parasocial: Twenty Years of Loss and Diagnosis

The anniversary essays gathered here share a structure of feeling: a sense that something once promising about social media has curdled, and that this curdling is neither accidental nor inevitable. Across the set, the contributors converge on a diagnosis — the political economy of contemporary platforms has hollowed out reciprocal sociality — while diverging in what they name as the dominant pathology of the present: parasociality [[Boyd2026-op]], scams [[Swartz2026-zb]], oligarchic capture [[Baym2026-tr]], or the foreclosure of intimate community [[Marwick2026-ss]] [[Marwick2026-qd]].

### Mourning the Old Internet

The most elegiac thread runs through Marwick's twin essays, which read as autoethnographic fieldnotes from LiveJournal, IRC, and university BBSes [[Marwick2026-ss]] [[Marwick2026-qd]]. For Marwick, what has been lost is concrete: granular privacy filters, sustained reciprocal writing, subcultural smallness, and the affective labor of long comment threads that seeded lifelong friendships and partnerships. Her wager is that this loss is contingent — a product of advertising, surveillance, and consolidation — and that any plausible future for online community lies not in cloning Twitter (as Bluesky does) but in reimagining infrastructures of care at small scale. boyd's contribution sharpens this lament into a terminological intervention: the very word "social" has become a misnomer, and scholars should deprecate "social media" in favor of "parasocial media" to mark the shift from peer-to-peer exchange to scrolling, influencers, and one-sided emotional attachment to professionalized content [[Boyd2026-op]].

### From Critique to Crisis

Where Marwick and boyd narrate transformation through personal and conceptual registers, Baym tallies the damage in political-economic terms [[Baym2026-tr]]. Returning to her 2015 manifesto, she finds each of her four threats — wealth inequality, algorithmic opacity, precarious labor, data extraction — measurably worse. Zuckerberg's wealth has quintupled; Musk has crossed $700 billion; generative AI launders user-generated content into deepfakes and synthetic slop. Baym's essay thus serves as the political-economic backbone for the others' affective complaints: the "broligarchy" she names is precisely the force that produced the parasocial turn boyd describes and foreclosed the LiveJournal-style sociality Marwick mourns. Crucially, Baym pivots from diagnosis to self-critique, asking what scholarly critique has actually accomplished and calling for interdisciplinary, cross-sector engagement rather than the comfortable repetition of already-shared verdicts.

### Scams as the Constitutive Logic

Swartz pushes the diagnosis in a different direction, arguing that scams are not a symptom of platform decay but its organizing logic [[Swartz2026-zb]]. Where boyd sees parasocial extraction and Baym sees oligarchic accumulation, Swartz sees a "scam age" in which MLMs, crypto pitches, hustle courses, sextortion, and creator-economy grift run on and through the same infrastructures that once promised connection. Her argument productively complicates the elegiac frame: the withering of institutions that Marwick and Baym lament is precisely what fuels scammy substitutes for community, agency, and futurity. Anti-fraud infrastructures, meanwhile, become governance regimes that authorize the very surveillance Marwick identifies as alienating. Scams, in this reading, are the economic correlate of parasociality — both name a condition in which one-sided, asymmetric, extractive relations have displaced reciprocal ones.

### Points of Convergence and Tension

Read together, the essays articulate a shared counter-history: the present arrangement of platforms is the contingent outcome of financialization, algorithmic optimization, and corporate consolidation, not a technological fate [[Marwick2026-ss]] [[Boyd2026-op]] [[Baym2026-tr]]. They also share a common gesture toward alternatives — Mastodon, Bluesky, mutual aid networks, encrypted chats, small-scale stewardship — while remaining ambivalent about whether such alternatives can scale or whether scale is itself the problem. A productive tension emerges between the nostalgic register of [[Marwick2026-ss]] and [[Marwick2026-qd]], which locates the good object in a subcultural past, and the forward-looking interventions of [[Baym2026-tr]] and [[Swartz2026-zb]], which insist that researchers must build new analytical tools — collaborative, cross-sectoral, attentive to scams and oligarchy — adequate to a media environment that no longer resembles what the field was founded to study. boyd's call to rename the object [[Boyd2026-op]] is, in this sense, both an ending and a beginning: an admission that the twenty-year-old vocabulary of "social media" has outlived its referent.
