# Polarization in Hybrid Media Systems

## The Hybrid Media System as Analytical Frame

The papers in this cluster share a common departure point: the empirical and conceptual inadequacy of treating "social media" and "legacy media" as separable spheres. Chadwick's hybrid media system framework recurs as the explicit backbone of [[Rodarte2026-dk]], [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]], [[Unknown2023-9137f448]], and [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]], with each extending it in a different direction. [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] reorients the study of false information away from creator intent toward propagator chains, treating circulation itself as the analytical object. [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] shows how covid-skeptic narratives gained traction not by bypassing legacy media but by being remediated through them — talk-show quotes and newspaper interviews of "Intellectual Dark Web" figures were what gave alternative influence networks their reach. [[Rodarte2026-dk]] inverts the classical sourcing relation: Brazilian parliamentarians become media producers themselves, mimicking journalistic conventions or attacking them. The thread running through these works is that polarization cannot be located in a single venue; it lives in the traffic between them.

## Mechanisms of Mainstreaming and Radicalization

A second strand turns from systems to mechanisms, asking *how* fringe content moves into broader publics. [[Rothut2026-or]] introduces "protest-facilitated mainstreaming" with the Querdenken case, distinguishing content mechanisms (anti-elite framings that bridge moderate and extreme publics) from connective mechanisms (Telegram infrastructures that route attention toward far-right hubs). [[Knupfer2025-vt]] develops a complementary "logic of connective faction," showing how CRT-adopting Republicans cluster with hyper-partisan media and are rewarded with engagement, while [[Bennett2025-xs]] generalizes this into a broader theory of "digital surrogate organizations" that erode party gatekeeping. [[Copland2025-em]] traces a transnational variant: Sky News Australia inserting itself into the US right-wing propaganda pipeline. [[Askanius2026-de]] adds the Swedish AIN with its fusion of microcelebrity self-branding and political extremism. Together these papers describe radicalization as an organizational and infrastructural achievement, not a cognitive one — a corrective Bennett and Livingston explicitly press against the "technocentric" misinformation paradigm.

## Echo Chambers Revisited: Algorithm vs. User Choice

A more contested literature concerns whether platforms themselves segregate audiences. [[Bakshy2015-rn]] set the early benchmark by showing that on Facebook, user choice suppressed cross-cutting exposure more than algorithmic ranking did. [[Brown2026-br]] reaches a similar verdict for YouTube: no radicalization pathways, only mild echo chambers (driven by users), but strong "rabbit hole" effects and a platform-wide moderately conservative tilt. [[Hartmann2025-px]]'s systematic review argues that much of the field's apparent disagreement is a measurement artifact, while [[Green2025-ap]] reframes the problem entirely: domain-level audience scores mask substantial story-level partisan heterogeneity, so "curation bubbles" form through networked sharing even when sources appear moderate. [[Dehghan2026-sy]] pushes furthest in this revisionist direction, arguing that polarization on Reddit is "sedimented" — a precondition rather than an outcome — produced by an entanglement of platform affordances, moderation, and discourse that cannot be reduced to any one. [[Mosleh2024-op]] complicates the picture cross-platform, and [[Kristensen2025-ni]] asks whether alternative platforms specifically drive discursive polarization. The cumulative effect is to dissolve the simple echo-chamber thesis without exonerating platforms.

## Platform Governance and Its Side Effects

Several papers turn the lens onto the platforms as active political agents. [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] shows that Facebook's amplification of partisan content is not stable but fluctuates sharply with governance interventions — the "break the glass" measures around the 2020 election produced measurable partisan reach penalties and journalistic-quality rewards. [[Ventura2026-yc]] documents the opposite kind of intervention's consequences: Brazil's X ban produced a durable "sorting ratchet," with conservatives circumventing and liberals departing, leaving the post-ban platform more right-leaning than before. [[Brady2026-ln]] demonstrates experimentally on Bluesky that engagement-based ranking amplifies moralized, toxic content and distorts perceived norms of political dialogue, while a "diversified extremity" algorithm can mitigate these effects without harming enjoyment. Read together, these works converge on a view of platforms as consequential political actors whose calibrations — whether moderation, bans, or ranking choices — reshape the information environment in ways that often run counter to their stated intentions.

## Narrative, Frames, and the Weaponization of Epistemic Categories

A further set of papers attends to the discursive substance of polarized communication. [[Elfes2026-jb]] introduces "narrative polarisation," using Greimas's actantial model to show that YouTube video transcripts on Israel-Palestine encode strongly opposed actantial roles even where comment-level discourse appears convergent. [[Yoo2026-ev]] traces how "fake news" itself has been weaponized as a delegitimizing label by hyperpartisan outlets on both left and right, reshaping Hallin's spheres of legitimate controversy. [[Ghezzi2023-8bebc91f]] documents the politicization of science sourcing, with UK newspapers selectively citing COVID-19 scientists by ideological orientation. [[Sarmiento2025-as]] offers unsupervised methods for surfacing emergent frames, and [[Gaisbauer2025-by]] argues for multi-level "political cartography" of news sharing that captures story, outlet, and content dimensions simultaneously. These contributions suggest that polarization is not only structural but rhetorical — built through the differential assignment of roles, blame, and epistemic legitimacy.

## Insularity, Populism, and Diverging Engagement Patterns

[[Giglietto2019-882f1900]] anchors a related line of work showing that populist supporters in Italy did not simply self-segregate; rather, the M5S electorate uniquely exploited the attention economy by amplifying favorable coverage and contesting unfavorable coverage. [[Rodarte2026-dk]]'s Brazilian findings echo this asymmetry between opposition and government-aligned media strategies, while [[Knupfer2025-vt]] and [[Bennett2025-xs]] document parallel asymmetries in the US. The recurring pattern: right-wing or populist networks more effectively integrate digital surrogates, hyper-partisan media, and elite signaling into self-reinforcing factional structures, while opposition or liberal counterparts more often go silent (as in [[Ventura2026-yc]]) or remain dispersed.

## Normative Stakes and the Limits of Scholarly Prescription

Finally, several papers reflect on what is to be done. [[Esau2025-tf]] theorizes "destructive polarization" and proposes deliberative reciprocity and inclusive listening as antidotes. [[F2020-6278a4aa]] catalogues methodological challenges — fuzzy organizational boundaries, opaque ad targeting, unreliable self-report — that complicate any prescriptive program. Most pointedly, [[van-Eck2026-xg]]'s systematic review shows that depolarization recommendations in the literature are largely disconnected from the empirical evidence the same studies generate, resting instead on implicit deliberative-democratic ideals. This is a sobering coda: even as the cluster develops increasingly sophisticated accounts of how polarization is produced through hybrid-media dynamics, the field's normative prescriptions remain weakly tethered to its empirical findings — a gap that defines the open frontier.
