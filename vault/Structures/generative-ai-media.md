# Generative AI and Media

## A field forming around generative AI

The papers gathered here track an emerging research front in which generative AI is studied simultaneously as object, infrastructure, and instrument. Three orientations recur: how AI is *imagined and narrated* in public discourse; how it acts as a *vector of persuasion, manipulation and epistemic disturbance*; and how it can be *put to work* in journalism, fact-checking and computational analysis. The vera.ai-adjacent work on coordinated gambling promotion [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]] sits at the intersection of all three, making it a useful anchor for reading the rest.

## Imaginaries, hype, and the discursive construction of AI

A cluster of papers treats AI primarily as a *discursive object*. [[Nguyen2026-vm]] maps mentalistic-agentic framings of LLMs across fifteen global outlets, finding that anthropomorphism is real but neither uniformly dominant nor uniformly uncritical; outlet-level editorial culture matters more than region. [[Wang2025-zy]] complements this by operationalising Cave and Dihal's hopes-and-fears framework across UK, US, Chinese and Indian press, while [[Fattorini2026-bo]] tracks audience-side attitudes in Italy and finds a 'critical ambivalence' shaped by direct tool use. [[Dodds2026-df]] reframes the entire conversation: AI hype is not just rhetoric but a *systemic infrastructure* that redistributes legitimacy and resources within journalism — a claim that gives the descriptive mapping in [[Nguyen2026-vm]] and [[Wang2025-zy]] sharper political stakes.

Two ethnographically-inflected pieces drill into where these imaginaries are produced. [[Stanusch2026-ec]] reads the Altman firing as a controversy in which industry deploys 'premediation' and 'preclusion' to monopolise AI's future, while [[Hepp2026-oi]] locates a 'quiet futuring' in Bay Area pioneer communities that supplies ideas later amplified by loud CEO discourse. [[Weinbrand2026-sf]] extends this to product-level framing, showing how Google, journalists and SEO marketers contest the meaning of AI Overviews. Read together, these papers suggest a layered model: lab-and-community imaginaries → industry controversy management → media framing → public attitudes — with [[Tornberg2026-lc]] arguing that the underlying media environment in which all this circulates is itself dissolving into 'post-social' formations partly driven by generative AI.

## Persuasion, manipulation, and epistemic harm

A second strand turns to GenAI's *effects*. The persuasion experiments now form a tight conversation. [[Hackenburg2025-dj]] identifies post-training and information density — not scale or personalisation — as the main levers of conversational AI persuasion, and documents a persuasion–accuracy trade-off. [[Lin2025-xp]] replicates large effects in real electoral contexts and finds that fact-and-evidence arguments, not psychological manipulation, do the work; right-leaning-advocacy AIs are systematically less accurate. [[DiGiuseppe2026-pu]] adds an important boundary condition: perceived political bias attenuates persuasion by ~25%, recasting neutrality itself as a lever. [[Costello2024-bg]]'s durable reductions in conspiracy beliefs operate in the same evidential register, suggesting that the persuasive mechanisms identified across these studies are continuous with classical central-route persuasion rather than uniquely AI-magical.

Other papers widen the threat surface. [[Schroeder2026-im]] and [[Orlando2025-ul]] both examine *agentic* swarms; the latter shows experimentally that mere teammate awareness is enough for emergent coordination in simulated information operations. [[Emilio2026-ik]] generalises these threads into a 'synthetic reality' stack and articulates the Generative AI Paradox: as fakes proliferate, rational discounting of all digital evidence raises the cost of truth. [[Schiffrin_undated-gi]] grounds the same concern in deepfake financial fraud and proposes gatekeeper liability. [[Lee2026-je]] documents a quieter but related capacity — LLM inference of political alignment from ostensibly nonpolitical posts — that feeds into the micro-targeting concerns [[Hackenburg2025-dj]] partly defuses. [[Waight2026-ts]] adds a geopolitical dimension: state-controlled media is 'laundered' into LLM outputs, with cross-language spillover. Finally, [[Triedman2025-uy]]'s Grokipedia analysis shows ideological capture operating not through persuasion but through the *substitution* of reference infrastructure.

Audience-side studies suggest the effects are real but bounded. [[Hameleers2026-mc]] finds that AI-generated visual disinformation does not universally outperform text or decontextualised video, while [[Choi2026-bz]] identifies modality-congruent carryover effects on truth discernment. Together these temper the more catastrophist readings in [[Emilio2026-ik]] and [[Schroeder2026-im]] without dismissing them.

## GenAI as method and as journalistic tool

A third strand treats GenAI as *instrument*. [[Arminio2025-tw]] shows that vision-language models substantially improve connotative image clustering and interpretability — directly relevant to the visual-disinformation pipelines used in [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]]. [[Alizadeh2026-es]] benchmarks coding agents on social-science reproduction and finds high accuracy but also worrying sycophancy under confirmatory prompting, echoing the persuasion–accuracy tension in [[Hackenburg2025-dj]]. [[Dierickx2026-tw]] offers the epistemological scaffolding journalism needs to handle these tools, proposing 'emergent facts' as a new category for evaluating GenAI outputs. [[Unknown2025-qj]] situates red-teaming as a public-interest practice that could bring some of these evaluative capacities into civil society.

## Synthesis: where the arc points

Across the corpus, three convergences stand out. First, *mechanism matters more than magnitude*: persuasion, coordination and bias largely operate through identifiable, often classical channels — information density [[Hackenburg2025-dj]], evidence [[Lin2025-xp]], teammate awareness [[Orlando2025-ul]], training data composition [[Waight2026-ts]] — rather than through unprecedented manipulative powers. Second, *the systemic level dominates the artifact level*: from hype as infrastructure [[Dodds2026-df]] to synthetic reality stacks [[Emilio2026-ik]] to post-social formations [[Tornberg2026-lc]] to platform-amplification regimes [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]], the most consequential harms are institutional and infrastructural. Third, *fact-checking and journalism are repositioned* — not just as targets of disruption but as epistemological resources [[Dierickx2026-tw]], persuasive interventions [[Costello2024-bg]], and sites where AI imaginaries get negotiated [[Nguyen2026-vm]], [[Dodds2026-df]]. The open research frontier traced by these papers is therefore less 'detect the deepfake' than 'design and study the sociotechnical conditions under which shared reality remains tractable.'
