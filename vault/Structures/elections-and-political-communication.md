# Elections and Political Communication

## The empirical terrain: from coverage to platforms to data access

Work filed under this topic shares a recurring question: when elections are mediated through fragmented, platformised information environments, how do attention, partisanship and influence actually flow? The papers approach this from three overlapping angles — coverage and visibility, platform dynamics and manipulation, and the infrastructural conditions under which any of this can be studied. Brazil, Italy, Germany and EU-level elections recur as the central terrains, but the arguments increasingly converge.

## Coverage, visibility and the media's role

A first cluster examines how news and platform algorithms allocate attention to political actors. [[Iris2026-pg]] finds that radical right parties received disproportionate visibility in online news across five EU countries during the 2024 EP elections — a structural pattern unrelated to outlet orientation, intensifying in the campaign's final weeks. [[Giglietto2019-882f1900]] complements this by showing that in 2018 Italy, sources favoured by populist supporters were more *insular*, and that the Five Star Movement uniquely orchestrated sharing and commenting to amplify positive coverage and contest negative coverage. [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] traces the older roots of these dynamics in TV–Twitter hybridity during the 2013 Italian campaign, where second-screen practices remained narrow and shaped by broadcast logic. Together these papers suggest that media visibility is neither neutral nor a simple reflection of electoral weight, and that populist actors have been particularly adept at exploiting hybrid attention economies.

Other coverage-side work shifts to the supply side of political communication itself. [[Rodarte2026-dk]] and the near-identical [[Li2026-wq]] document how Brazilian parliamentarians during the Manaus COVID crisis split into competing modes of *epistemic authority* — derivative reporting, direct state-aligned claims, and proximity-based brokerage — illustrating how politicians increasingly produce rather than merely supply news. [[Knupfer2025-vt]] develops a parallel argument for the US Republican Party, where a "logic of connective faction" ties ideologically extreme legislators to hyper-partisan media, with engagement and visibility rewards reinforcing radicalisation. [[Larsson2026-ro]] tracks the same negative-affect dynamic longitudinally in Norway's Progress Party, where negative posts drive shares and comments while positive posts drive likes.

## Affect, framing and narrative construction

Several papers zoom in on *how* electoral discourse is constructed affectively and narratively. [[Kalsnes2025-zb]] uses Facebook Reactions across three Scandinavian elections to show that "Angry" reactions consistently drive sharing while "Love" reactions suppress it, and [[Anwar2024-34dba628]] synthesises this finding across 64 studies, framing Reactions as paralinguistic affordances that systematically reward negative political content. [[Bouchafra2026-ts]] extends the affective story visually, showing how the Sweden Democrats tailored securitising memes to TikTok's young audience. [[Starbird2025-jj]] and [[Prochaska2025-ef]] complicate the misinformation literature by arguing that election rumours are produced through *framing* of often-true evidence rather than outright falsehoods, with [[Prochaska2025-ef]] showing that the 2020 "deep story" of fraud was reactivated in 2022 with little explicit narration needed. [[Rossini2026-jn]] connects these narrative dynamics to attitudinal consequences, finding that belief in electoral misinformation in Brazil 2022 predicted increased political intolerance, mediated by messaging-app news use.

## Platform dynamics, manipulation and influence

A second large cluster asks what platforms themselves *do* to electoral communication. The most direct interventions are documented in [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]] and its companion [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]], which show that Meta's political content reduction policy cut Italian MPs' reach by 72%, began ten months before its announced global rollout, and asymmetrically advantaged extremist accounts that responded by ramping up posting volume. [[Ventura2026-yc]] documents the converse effect: Brazil's 2024 X ban produced a durable rightward "sorting ratchet" as conservatives circumvented the ban while liberals went silent. Jurg2025-ro (via [[Jurg2025-ur]]) audits YouTube's 2024 EP-election moderation, finding inconsistent publisher labelling and opaque removal communication, while [[Simeone2025-vo]] shows that targeted deplatforming of Arizona election-audit accounts produced disproportionate, network-wide effects that visibility-reduction approaches likely cannot replicate.

A related strand studies coordinated and inauthentic behaviour. [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]] proposes Coordinated Link Sharing Behaviour as an action-based alternative to content-veracity detection in the Italian 2018 and 2019 elections, and [[Giglietto2023-fa71a001]] extends this to an iterative workflow for keeping coordinated-account lists current. [[Kansaon2025-id]] adapts these tools to encrypted WhatsApp groups around Brazil's 2022 election, linking coordinated messaging to real-world events including the January 8 attacks. [[Oprea2025-lf]] documents hyperactive sharing on Romanian parties' Facebook pages, and [[Gaw2025-ru]] reconceptualises Philippine influence operations as political brokerage embedded in political-economic infrastructures. Yet skepticism runs through this literature too: [[Di-Marco2025-aa]] formally shows that observed coordinated accounts on X exert influence close to random placement, far below strategic optima; [[Appel2026-qr]] finds that Meta's deceptive networks in the 2020 US election reached millions but produced no robust attitudinal effects after controlling for pre-exposure characteristics; and [[Arceneaux2026-xk]] suggests bots influence campaigns more than press or public discourse. [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]] similarly finds misinformation distinctively viral but concentrated.

The advertising literature mirrors this ambivalence. [[Kim2026-wg]] documents geo-racially targeted voter suppression ads in 2016 with substantial heterogeneous turnout effects on the targeted segment, while [[Allcott2025-jb]] finds no detectable effects from removing political ads in 2020. [[Votta2025-xz]] highlights ad delivery algorithms as an under-examined layer of platform power, and [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]] illustrates volunteer-based auditing as one workaround. [[Lin2025-xp]] introduces a newer concern — that human-AI dialogues can shift voter preferences more than traditional ads — with right-leaning AI advocacy producing systematically more inaccurate claims.

## Perceptions and downstream attitudinal effects

Some papers move from exposure to perception. [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]] documents a "hostile misinformation effect" across three 2024 EP-election countries: voters systematically believe their own side is more targeted by misinformation than their opponents, with downstream losses in media trust and electoral integrity. [[Gattermann2025-yx]] complements this by tying disinformation concern to far-right electoral performance. The bot–public–press analysis in [[Arceneaux2026-xk]] and the misinformation-tolerance link in [[Rossini2026-jn]] sit alongside these as evidence that the *perception* of a polluted information environment matters even where direct effects are elusive.

## Research infrastructure as a first-order problem

A striking development across the topic is that infrastructure has become a substantive — not merely methodological — concern. [[Munger2025-cz]] argues that the Meta2020 partnership exposes the structural limits of reactive empirical social science: platforms change too fast for replication, and proactive regulation requiring platforms to characterise their own effects is needed. [[Lukito2026-nb]] develops this into a systematic mapping of 72 data-access tools, naming "price, proficiency, or permission" as the operative barrier and warning that platform-permission access is inherently ephemeral. [[Philipp2026-tl]] surveys the new DSA-mandated APIs and portals, [[Schulte2026-df]] (whose argument is duplicated under [[Hepp2026-oi]]) treats multi-platform observability as a reusable-infrastructure problem using the 2025 German federal election, and the Meta content-reduction work in [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]] explicitly demonstrates the value — and limits — of DSA-enabled tools like the Meta Content Library. [[Ventura2025-sw]] turns to messaging deactivation as one of few causal designs reaching beyond feed-based platforms.

Methodological contributions sit in this same register. [[Achmann-Denkler2026-lx]] validates multimodal LLMs for visual analysis of the 2021 German election, [[Marino2024-2fbc690f]] articulates validation protocols for LLMs-in-the-loop pipelines on Italian elections, and [[Giglietto2024-cbeb3f70]] benchmarks embedding models for clustering Italian political news. [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]] extends Guess et al.'s US analysis to France, Germany and Italy using Meta's URL Shares dataset.

## A line of argument emerging across the corpus

Read together, these papers trace an arc from documenting *what* circulates around elections — coverage imbalances, affective engagement, populist amplification — to interrogating *what platforms do* through moderation, algorithmic delivery and bans, and finally to confronting *what researchers can know* under contemporary access regimes. [[F2020-6278a4aa]] anticipated this trajectory by naming networked publics, micro-targeting and attention competition as the three vectors reshaping political communication; the empirical work since has both confirmed those vectors and revealed that their study now depends on regulatory infrastructure as much as on theory. The MINE programme's terrain is therefore neither purely about elections nor purely about platforms, but about the conditions under which democratic communication remains legible to empirical inquiry at all.
