# Coordinated Inauthentic Behaviour

## From content to coordination: the analytical pivot

The papers gathered here share a common conviction: that studying online manipulation through the veracity of individual posts or the badness of individual actors is insufficient, and that the analytically productive unit is *coordinated behaviour* itself. [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]] articulates this most programmatically, proposing an "ecological, action-based" reframing that grounds Facebook's notion of coordinated inauthentic behaviour (CIB) in scholarship on collective action and operationalises it as Coordinated Link Sharing Behaviour (CLSB). That move seeded the CooRnet lineage extended by [[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]] into pandemic-era fact-checking, by [[Giglietto2023-fa71a001]] into a continuously updating workflow for the 2022 Italian election, and by [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]] into the analysis of generative-AI-fuelled gambling promotion. Across these works, the methodological wager is the same — content is too fast, too varied, and too easily laundered to monitor directly, so one tracks the patterned co-actions of accounts instead.

## Conceptual contestation: what counts as coordinated, what counts as inauthentic

The very terms of this research program are increasingly contested. [[Thiele2025-ol]] argues that "coordinated inauthentic behaviour" conflates inauthenticity with manipulative intent and is too tailored to real-name platforms, proposing instead a rational-choice framework around principals, scale, elaborateness and disguise. [[Hurcombe2025-cs]] turns the critical lens onto Meta itself, showing how its Newsroom strategically deploys "CIB," "authenticity" and "coordination" as floating signifiers that displace responsibility onto outside actors while obscuring the role of platform affordances and hyperpartisan elites. [[Graham2026-fb]] pushes further still, arguing through the Reddit "Anus_Fungi" case that the human/bot binary is analytically unstable and that authenticity is a culturally performed norm rather than a technical property. [[Jovanovic-Harrington2026-ze]] complements these critiques empirically: Serbia's SNS-linked networks mimic grassroots authenticity precisely *because* detection regimes are calibrated to centralised bot farms. Together these papers destabilise the field's vocabulary even as others continue to refine its methods.

## The CooRnet lineage and the CLSB tradition

A through-line of the cluster is the CLSB programme. [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]] establishes the core finding that coordinated entities disproportionately amplify problematic domains in Italian elections, with structural typologies (centralised vs. clustered networks) cutting across openly political and deceptively "non-political" actors. [[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]] extends this to the COVID-19 infodemic, documenting how Italian covid-skeptic networks emerged as offshoots of pre-existing political clusters and adapted through tactics like first-comment link placement, "link laundering" via reputable outlets, and image-macros. [[Giglietto2023-fa71a001]] addresses the inherent decay of static actor lists by building a workflow for continuous detection, surfacing ideologically, economically, and religiously motivated operations side by side. [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]] then shows the same infrastructure capturing a post-ChatGPT explosion in coordinated gambling promotion, foregrounding algorithmic amplification rather than moderation as the regulatory pressure point. [[Song2025-yh]] adapts CooRnet for a comparative UK/US analysis of pro- and anti-vaccine CLSB, making explicit a claim latent in the earlier work: coordination itself is ideologically agnostic, used for both misinformation and credible public-health communication.

## Methodological maturation: from single signals to multimodal, temporal, and structural detection

A parallel arc concerns the technical sophistication of detection. Early CLSB work relied largely on near-simultaneous URL co-shares; subsequent papers progressively complicate this. [[Yang2025-iv]] critiques timing-based detection as easily evaded and proposes signals based on statistical regularities in sharing speed and frequency. [[Mannocci2025-ig]] systematically compares operationalisations of *multimodal* coordination (across retweets, replies, mentions, hashtags, URLs), showing that naive flattening discards structurally important nodes and that multiplex community detection offers the best trade-off. [[Iannucci2025-eg]] integrates this multiplex logic with temporal decay kernels, benchmarking against the field's standard methods. [[Luceri2025-tr]] extends detection beyond text into TikTok's video-first ecosystem, while [[Rodriguez_Farres2025-sg]] tackles real-time detection on Bluesky. [[Minici2024-tf]] proposes IOHunter, a graph foundation model combining language and structural signals for cross-campaign generalisation, and [[Gerard2025-br]] develops CANE/t-CANE to reconstruct user networks via shared latent narrative clusters when API access and interaction signals are scarce — a direct response to the post-Twitter-API research environment.

## Sceptical and limiting voices

Yet several papers urge caution about how much explanatory weight coordination can bear. [[Di-Marco2025-aa]] models the post-hoc influence of coordinated accounts in retweet cascades and finds that observed CIB placement looks statistically closer to random than to any influence-maximising strategy, suggesting coordinated accounts may be less pivotal than commonly assumed. [[Appel2026-qr]], working with Meta-shared exposure data across 49 deceptive networks in the 2020 US election, finds that exposure is highly concentrated, largely driven by reshares from non-network accounts, and that apparent associations with attitudes mostly vanish under entropy balancing. [[Pante2025-pq]] reassesses claims of *inter-state* coordination and finds that they often do not survive more rigorous detection methods with proper controls. These works do not abandon the coordination paradigm but discipline its causal pretensions.

## Beyond detection: organisations, brokers, and political economy

A further set of contributions argues that detection alone misses the organisational and political-economic scaffolding of manipulation. [[Poliakoff2026-fa]] uses OSINT analysis of 350 CVs to reconstruct the Internet Research Agency as a media-labour organisation with a five-level hierarchy, embedded in Russia's promotional culture rather than an isolated state operation. [[Gaw2025-ru]] reframes Philippine election influence operations as a contemporary form of political brokerage. [[Kansaon2025-id]] tracks coordinated WhatsApp activity into the streets of Brasília on 8 January 2023, linking detection to real-world mobilisation. [[Kuznetsova2025-nu]] and [[Kulichkina2026-zk]] extend the toolkit into authoritarian contexts (Russian/Belarusian Telegram; Chinese COVID protests), where coordinated infrastructure operates as both protest enabler and repressive instrument. [[Oprea2025-lf]] develops an "Authenticity Matrix" for Romanian election shares, finding hyperactive users responsible for up to 45% of shares on individual posts, while [[Graham2025-gp]] shows how the #IStandWithPutin campaign parasitised the very infrastructures designed to support credible information.

## Discursive ecologies and the limits of "inauthenticity"

A recurring finding is that coordination cannot be cleanly separated from the broader information ecosystem it inhabits. [[Efstratiou2026-ij]] traces how a small, predominantly contrarian coordinated retweet network amplifies credentialed anti-consensus voices, with media coverage temporally *following* superspreader activity — a pathway from coordinated amplification into mainstream news. [[Zhao2025-ny]] examines hashtag hijacking as a strategic contest combining attention, narrative framing, and bot activity. [[FitzGerald2025-nv]] argues that manipulation campaigns persist by appropriating emerging events. [[Gerard2025-br]] documents how a sliver of "bridge users" structurally positioned between Truth Social and X seeds roughly 70% of cross-platform narrative migrations. The picture across these papers is of coordination as one node in a larger ecology of attention, narrative migration and elite amplification, rather than a self-contained pathology.

## The generative-AI horizon

Finally, several papers register a regime shift driven by generative AI. [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]] empirically documents a structural break in coordinated gambling promotion after ChatGPT's release. [[Orlando2025-ul]] simulates LLM agents in information-operation scenarios and finds that mere *teammate awareness* produces coordination dynamics — synchronised amplification, narrative convergence, hashtag diffusion — nearly equivalent to explicit collective deliberation. [[Schroeder2026-im]] generalises this into a policy argument about "malicious AI swarms," combining persistent identity, real-time adaptation, and cross-platform deployment in ways that strain synchrony-based detectors and threaten to poison future training data through "LLM grooming." Read alongside the conceptual critiques of [[Thiele2025-ol]], [[Graham2026-fb]] and [[Hurcombe2025-cs]], these papers suggest that the next phase of the coordination research programme will need detection methods, governance frameworks, and a conceptual vocabulary that can accommodate manipulation which is at once more authentic-seeming, more adaptive, and harder to attribute than the centralised troll farms that originally gave the field its name.
