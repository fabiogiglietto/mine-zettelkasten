# Platform Affordances and the Transformation of Social Media Publics

## From Networked Publics to Algorithmic Clusters

The throughline running across these essays is a diagnosis: the social media of the 2010s — built around explicit interpersonal ties, the social graph, and an open web of buttons and embeds — is giving way to something else. [[Gerbaudo2026-fo]] gives this transition its sharpest theoretical formulation, arguing that TikTok inaugurates a "second generation" of platforms whose publics are no longer networked through friending and following but *clustered* through statistical inference over implicit behavioral signals. The social form itself has changed: from people-centric to item-centric, from visible affiliation to opaque assignment, from self-selected community to algorithmic silosociality.

[[Helmond2026-ll]] supplies the infrastructural counterpart to this account. The retirement of Facebook's Like button — once the emblematic device of platformization's outward push across the open web — marks Meta's abandonment of the social graph paradigm in favor of in-app behavioral capture and AI-driven inference. Where Gerbaudo theorizes the morphology of clustered publics, Helmond traces the political economy that makes them possible: a Big Tech reorientation toward "Big AI," compute-hungry to the point of pushing data centers into orbit. Together the two essays describe the same pivot from different vantage points — affordance and architecture, interface and infrastructure.

## The Political Economy Beneath the Affordances

The collection insists that none of this was inevitable. [[Marwick2026-ss]] and its companion piece [[Marwick2026-qd]] press hardest on this point through autoethnographic fieldnotes from LiveJournal, IRC, and the early web. The intimacy, reciprocity, and granular privacy of those spaces are mobilized not as nostalgia but as evidence: alternative design and governance choices once produced — and could again produce — small-scale, care-oriented sociality. The current condition of alienation, surveillance, and hostility to vulnerability is the contingent outcome of advertising-driven business models and scale-seeking optimization, not a natural endpoint of networked communication.

[[Swartz2026-zb]] sharpens the political-economic critique by reframing it through the figure of the scam. If Marwick's essays describe what platforms have foreclosed, Swartz describes what they have proliferated: a "scam age" in which fraud is not peripheral noise but a central organizing logic of platform economies, hustle culture, and even the anti-fraud infrastructures that legitimate expansive surveillance. The boundary work performed by the word *scam* becomes itself a site of governance — defining who counts as a legitimate economic citizen online.

## Points of Convergence and Tension

Read together, the essays converge on a shared object — the transformation of social media publics under algorithmic, AI-centered, and extractive logics — while approaching it through distinct registers: theoretical (Gerbaudo), infrastructural (Helmond), economic-cultural (Swartz), and autobiographical-historical (Marwick). They agree that the *social* in social media has been substantially hollowed out: replaced by behavioral inference in [[Gerbaudo2026-fo]], by AI-mediated content recommendation in [[Helmond2026-ll]], by scammy substitutes for failing institutions in [[Swartz2026-zb]], and by alienated scrolling in [[Marwick2026-ss]].

They diverge, productively, on what to do with this diagnosis. Gerbaudo and Helmond remain analytically situated within the platforms as they currently exist, refining concepts (clustered publics, transcoding) to keep pace with what platforms are becoming. Marwick gestures outward and backward, toward small-scale infrastructures of care that resist the scale logic itself. Swartz occupies a third position: rather than reform or exit, she calls for taking the scam seriously as the dominant mode of economic life online, treating fraud and its governance as a privileged window onto the platform present.

## An Emerging Research Agenda

A coherent agenda emerges across these texts. First, the unit of analysis for "publics" must be rebuilt for an era in which visibility is detached from following and inferred from watch time — a project [[Gerbaudo2026-fo]] begins but explicitly invites others to extend. Second, the analytic of platformization must be re-specified for the AI phase, distinguishing it from generic digitization, as [[Helmond2026-ll]] proposes via transcoding. Third, the economic substrate of platform life — its scams, hustles, and anti-fraud regimes — demands the kind of sustained attention [[Swartz2026-zb]] models. Fourth, the historical archive of the pre-platform internet, as recovered in [[Marwick2026-ss]] and [[Marwick2026-qd]], is itself a methodological resource: a reminder that the present platform order is one configuration among possible others, and that any future digital public sphere will be built — or refused — through deliberate design choices.
