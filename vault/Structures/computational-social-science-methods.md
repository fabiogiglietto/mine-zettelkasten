# Computational Social Science Methods

## The Closing Laboratory: From API Optimism to Permanent Precarity

The papers gathered here document a field-defining inflection point. What [[Freelon2024-sc]] periodizes as the "post-API age" — successive waves of opening and closing data access across Facebook, Twitter/X, YouTube, Reddit, and TikTok — has hardened into a structural condition. [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] supplies the scientometric obituary: a 25% annual growth in Twitter-based research through 2022, followed by stagnation and a 13% decline once the free API closed and Enterprise pricing hit $42,000/month. [[Yang2026-tq]] generalises this across disciplines, showing that empirical social-media research tripled over 2010–2022 before plateauing in 2022–2024, with the same two platforms (Twitter/X and Facebook) carrying — and now dragging down — the field. [[Lukito2026-nb]] reframes the situation as a "price, proficiency, or permission" problem that sorts who can study elections at all, while [[Allen2025-ot]] reads the current moment as one in which platform-independent experimental tools (browser extensions, LLM reranking) become necessary precisely because cooperation is sporadic and revocable. Across these contributions, the dominant mood is neither triumphalism nor despair but a sober reckoning: data access is now understood as permanently contingent on corporate and regulatory weather.

## Data as Political Object: The DSA and the Politics of Quality

A second strand reads access not just as infrastructure but as politics. [[Peters2026-mo]] traces how "data quality" entered the EU's Digital Services Act only because academic and NGO submissions pushed it in against platform resistance — Meta even inverting the argument to claim that low-quality platform data made researcher access pointless. The DSA's Article 40 thus surfaces repeatedly across this corpus as both promise and warning: [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] and [[Freelon2024-sc]] cite it as the most credible legislative lever, while [[Lukito2026-nb]] notes the WEIRD-centric geography it reinforces by privileging EU-based researchers. The McNally/Bastos cluster ([[Bastos2025-ya]], [[McNally2025-dn]], [[Bastos2025-ol]]) operationalises Article 40(4) avant la lettre by reconstructing Facebook's News Feed interventions from outside, arguing that recommender systems are not "black boxes" but sociotechnical interventions with detectable, ~20-day lagged signatures. [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] supplies the necessary corrective: roughly 80% of high-profile social-media research is "industrially saturated" through author, editor, or reviewer ties, most undisclosed — a finding that complicates any reading of regulator-mandated access as straightforwardly emancipatory.

## Audits of the Pipes: APIs, Scrapers, and Donated Traces

Where the political-economy strand asks who gets in, a parallel methodological strand asks what comes out once you do. [[Rieder2025-ju]] audits YouTube's Data API and finds it "forgetful by design": coverage decays in three phases after publication, identical queries return inconsistent results, and the endpoint behaves more like a recommender than a retrieval system. [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] documents an analogous artefact inside Meta's URL Shares Dataset, where the 100-share anonymisation threshold makes 46 disparate countries look algorithmically synchronised. [[Ulloa2024-jm]] indicts ex-situ scraping itself: 33.8% of measured discrepancy comes from the collection environment (paywalls, logins, cookie walls) rather than temporal decay, biasing news-exposure estimates in systematic ways. These audits converge on a sobering point — that the social signals researchers hope to read are deeply entangled with platform engineering choices, anonymisation thresholds, and the user-interaction context of capture. Donated-trace approaches partially escape this trap: [[Bouchaud2026-lr]] uses 2.5 million donated "Who to Follow" recommendations to reconstruct X's embedding space and show that the system inadvertently encodes a Left-Right direction (ρ=0.887), while [[Fan2026-af]] mobilises 1.26 million donated traces from 309 German users to argue for a "temporal turn" using user-sequences rather than cross-sectional aggregates.

## The Tip of the Iceberg: Validity of the Visible

Even with clean pipes, the visible is not the social. [[Oswald2025-km]] articulates the production-consumption gap most starkly: a small minority generates most political content, distorting inference for citizens and researchers alike. [[Iannelli2018-ebd918b7]] foreshadows one practical workaround — using Facebook's own ad platform to recruit niche populations into surveys — though the evidence that interest-targeting actually finds ideologically distinctive respondents proved inconclusive. [[F2020-6278a4aa]] earlier sketched why this matters: networked publics, micro-targeting, and amplification have made traditional political-communication methodologies (self-reported diets, official-channel monitoring) unreliable. The Bruns and Sarmiento methodological proposals ([[Bruns2025-fz]], [[Sarmiento2025-as]]) and the cartographic framework of [[Gaisbauer2025-by]] respond by trying to represent multimodal interactions and emergent frames without collapsing them into one-dimensional summaries — practice mapping, unsupervised framing, story/outlet/content-level news cartographies — each pushing against the impoverished representations that earlier trace-based work inherited.

## LLMs Enter the Toolkit — and the Object of Study

The most recent papers register the arrival of large language models as both method and methodological problem. As measurement tools, LLMs are powerful: [[Lee2026-je]] shows GPT-4o inferring political alignment from ostensibly non-political Reddit conversations with user-level F1 approaching 0.8, exploiting culturally politicised terms like "Tesla" or "Taylor Swift"; [[Arminio2025-tw]] uses VLLMs to recover connotative meaning from Instagram images that CNN pipelines miss; [[Lai2024-to]] extends latent ideology estimation to YouTube videos; [[Arora2025-tx]] operationalises multi-modal framing. At the meta-level, [[Alizadeh2026-es]] shows that frontier coding agents can reproduce 78–93% of social-science findings, even inferring research questions from anonymised code. But [[Balluff2026-if]] insists on reflexivity: corporate LLMs introduce reproducibility, environmental, and bias costs that the field is adopting without accounting; synthetic data exhibits regression-to-the-mean and Western/centrist stereotyping; commercial guardrails silently constrain validity. [[Lee2026-je]]'s privacy implications complete the loop, recalling Kosinski-era anxieties: the same inferential power that lets researchers scale also makes scalable micro-targeting trivial.

## A Provisional Synthesis

Read together, these papers narrate a field that has lost its early infrastructure (open APIs, [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]]'s "Golden Age"), is contesting a new one (the DSA, [[Peters2026-mo]], [[Freelon2024-sc]], [[Lukito2026-nb]]), and is simultaneously interrogating both the integrity of the pipes ([[Rieder2025-ju]], [[Ulloa2024-jm]], [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]]), the representativeness of the visible ([[Oswald2025-km]], [[Yang2026-tq]]), the independence of its own scholarship ([[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]]), and the implications of folding LLMs into both method and object ([[Balluff2026-if]], [[Lee2026-je]], [[Alizadeh2026-es]]). The "open laboratory" agenda that animated the 2010s has not been abandoned, but its terms have shifted: from frictionless access to *governed* access; from trace volume to trace *provenance* and *temporality* ([[Fan2026-af]], [[Bouchaud2026-lr]]); from platform cooperation to platform-independent instruments ([[Allen2025-ot]]). The through-line is a discipline learning to do reflexive computational social science under conditions where the laboratory is no longer open by default — and may never be again.
