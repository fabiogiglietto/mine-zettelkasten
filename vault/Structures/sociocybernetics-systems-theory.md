# Sociocybernetics & Social Systems Theory

## From Creator to Propagator: A Cybernetic Reframing of Information Disorder

The single paper currently filed here, [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]], stakes out the theoretical posture that this hub is meant to consolidate: a Luhmannian and second-order cybernetic vantage on contemporary communication problems. Rather than treating "fake news" as an object whose essence is fixed at the moment of creation, the authors redescribe false-information circulation as a recursive system of observations — each propagator a self-referential observer making a judgment that becomes input for the next. This is Bateson's "difference that makes a difference" applied to the hybrid news ecology: information has no truth-value independent of the observers who process it.

### Second-order observation as analytical move

The decisive theoretical gesture is the shift from first-order to second-order observation. First-order accounts ask *what* a piece of content is (satire, hoax, error, propaganda); second-order accounts ask *how observers distinguish* it as such, and how those distinctions chain together. [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] operationalises this through a micro/meso/macro architecture in which the meso level — a matrix of injector/propagator judgments — is precisely where systemic emergence becomes visible. The four resulting propagation typologies are not categories of content but configurations of observation, which is what makes the framework genuinely systems-theoretical rather than merely taxonomic.

### Hybridity and the limits of consensus

A second strand worth tracking forward in this folder is the claim that stable societal consensus on truthfulness is structurally unlikely — coexisting opposing beliefs are the normal operating condition of a functionally differentiated, hybrid media system. This reads as a direct extension of Luhmann's reality-of-the-mass-media argument: the system produces and reproduces its own distinctions, and "fake news" is best understood as a self-description that the system uses to mark its own boundaries. The paper's reflexive worry — that the very term *fake news* positions its user as a truth-claimer — is itself a second-order observation about scholarly discourse.

### Open threads for this hub

As further papers are filed here, two arcs are worth sustaining. First, the methodological payoff of multi-level cybernetic models for empirical internet studies: how does one actually observe cascades as emergent without collapsing back into creator-centric narratives? Second, the diagnostic ambivalence toward key terms — "fake news," "disinformation," "misinformation" — which [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] treats as observer-dependent labels rather than natural kinds. Both threads keep the Zettelkasten anchored in the Luhmannian commitment that animates the PI's framing work: systems do not contain information; they produce it through recursive observation.
