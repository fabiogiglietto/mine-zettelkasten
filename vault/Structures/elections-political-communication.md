# Elections and Political Communication on Social Media

## Studying Elections on Platforms: A Field Defined by Its Infrastructure

What unites the work filed here is less a single research question than a shared predicament: how to study electoral communication when platforms, parties, and publics co-constitute one another, and when the infrastructure for observing them is itself politically contested. The papers collectively trace an arc from cataloguing campaign behaviour to interrogating the algorithmic, regulatory, and methodological scaffolding that determines what can be known at all.

### The Infrastructure Turn

A striking convergence runs through the most recent work: researchers increasingly treat *data access itself* as the primary object of analysis. [[Lukito2026-nb]] frames this as a tripartite problem of "price, proficiency, or permission," cataloguing 72 access tools and documenting the structural exclusion of non-Western researchers. [[Schulte2026-df]] makes a parallel case from the German 2025 election, arguing that multi-platform observability should be reconceived as reusable research infrastructure rather than ad-hoc projects. [[Philipp2026-tl]] surveys how the EU Digital Services Act has reshaped this landscape, while [[Jurg2025-ur]] audits YouTube's moderation through the lens of "observability" rather than transparency. The two Giglietto working papers ([[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]], [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]]) demonstrate the payoff: the DSA-enabled Meta Content Library makes it possible to detect a 72% reduction in Italian MPs' Facebook reach that Meta never disclosed. Together with [[F2020-6278a4aa]]'s earlier diagnosis of methodological strain in the big-data era, these papers argue that the politics of platform research has become inseparable from the politics of elections themselves.

### Platform Effects: From Bold Claims to Calibrated Nulls

A second arc runs through experimental and quasi-experimental studies of platform effects. The Meta2020 collaboration's headline result — [[Allcott2025-jb]] finds no detectable effect of removing political ads — sits alongside [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]]'s diffusion analysis of misinformation reach. [[Munger2025-cz]] then turns metascientific critic, arguing the entire enterprise suffers from low "temporal validity" and inadequate power. Against this backdrop of nulls, more recent work locates effects through narrower mechanisms: [[Gauthier2026-iq]] shows X's algorithm shifts attitudes rightward via persistent changes in *who users follow*, [[Kim2026-wg]] recovers large suppression effects only by examining heterogeneous treatment on geo-racially targeted subpopulations, and [[Lin2025-xp]] documents substantial persuasion effects from LLM dialogues. [[Appel2026-qr]] complements these by showing that deceptive networks reached tens of millions but that exposure correlations largely vanish once pre-treatment characteristics are controlled. The lesson is that platform effects exist but require methodological precision about *whom*, *how*, and *for how long*.

### Coordinated, Inauthentic, and Strategic Behaviour

A third cluster examines actors who exploit platform affordances at scale. [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]] formalises "coordinated link sharing behaviour" as a unit of analysis in Italian elections, an approach extended into encrypted spaces by [[Kansaon2025-id]] for Brazilian WhatsApp groups and into Romanian campaigns by [[Oprea2025-lf]]. [[Gaw2025-ru]] reframes such operations as political *brokerage* embedded in Philippine political economy. Yet skepticism is also growing: [[Di-Marco2025-aa]] shows that observed coordinated accounts in UK election cascades behave essentially randomly rather than strategically, achieving roughly a tenth of their theoretical influence, while [[Arceneaux2026-xk]] finds bots influence campaigns (especially negative sentiment) more than they influence press or public agendas. The picture is one of genuine but bounded manipulation, often dependent on resharing by ordinary users — a pattern [[Appel2026-qr]] documents quantitatively.

### Advertising, Algorithms, and Visibility

Several papers focus on the political economy of paid and algorithmic visibility. [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]] pioneers crowdsourced auditing of Brazilian Facebook ads; [[Kim2026-wg]] documents geo-racial voter-suppression targeting; [[Votta2025-xz]] turns attention from advertiser intent to delivery algorithms. [[Balluff2026-bv]] traces the Austrian "Inseratenaffäre" to show how government advertising can capture even democratic-corporatist media systems. [[Iris2026-pg]] finds the Radical Right structurally overrepresented across European online news in the 2024 EP campaign — a visibility imbalance that the Giglietto working papers ([[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]], [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]]) show is partially produced by *Meta's own* algorithmic choices, which suppressed mainstream politicians while extremists compensated through posting volume.

### Misinformation as Identity Work

Misinformation papers in this collection increasingly resist the "false facts" framing. [[Starbird2025-jj]] develops an evidence-frame approach showing that election rumours typically combine accurate evidence with distorting frames, while [[Prochaska2025-ef]] extends this to "deep stories" co-performed by elites, influencers, and audiences across the 2020–2022 cycle. [[Rossini2026-jn]] traces how belief in electoral misinformation in Brazil cultivates political intolerance, and [[Ventura2025-sw]] uses WhatsApp deactivation to isolate messaging-app effects. The elite-supply side is treated by [[Tornberg2025-ir]], who shows that neither populism nor right-wing ideology alone predicts misinformation sharing — only their combination, *radical-right populism*, does — and by [[Tai2026-qk]] on institutional checks among US officials. [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]] adds a perceptual layer: a "hostile misinformation effect" by which partisans believe their own side is disproportionately targeted, paralleling the classical hostile media effect. [[Gattermann2025-yx]] links such perceptions back to electoral context.

### Parties, Platforms, and the Adaptation of Campaigning

A final strand examines how parties craft messages for specific platform logics. [[Bouchafra2026-ts]] shows the Sweden Democrats migrating securitising discourse from text-heavy Facebook/X posts to TikTok memes aimed at younger voters. [[Larsson2026-ro]] documents a decade-long shift toward negative posting by Norway's Progress Party, with negativity rewarded by shares and comments but not likes — a finding consonant with [[Kalsnes2025-zb]]'s Scandinavian analysis of "Angry" reactions as mobilisation drivers, and synthesised in [[Anwar2024-34dba628]]'s review of Facebook reactions research. [[Achmann-Denkler2026-lx]] extends visual analysis to candidate-versus-party Instagram strategies in Germany using multimodal LLMs. [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] provides an earlier baseline from the 2013 Italian elections, situating these adaptations within a hybrid TV–Twitter ecology that anticipated today's cross-platform concerns.

### Narrative Authority and the Hybrid Media System

Cutting across these strands is a recurring theme: politicians no longer merely use platforms but compete with journalism for epistemic authority. [[Rodarte2026-dk]] shows three distinct strategies by which Brazilian parliamentarians pursued narrative control during the Manaus crisis — derivative reporting, direct state authority, and local brokerage. [[Knupfer2025-vt]] develops a "logic of connective faction" linking radicalised Republican legislators to hyper-partisan media surrogates. [[Marino2024-2fbc690f]] addresses the methodological side, proposing LLM-in-the-loop pipelines to study such discourse at scale. The collective implication, echoing [[F2020-6278a4aa]], is that the elections-and-platforms research programme is no longer about measuring exposure to discrete messages but about understanding how authority, attention, and visibility are jointly produced — by parties, algorithms, regulators, and the researchers who can or cannot see them.
