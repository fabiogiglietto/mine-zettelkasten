# Elections & Political Communication on Social Media

## Tracing the line of argument

The papers gathered here trace a broad arc from descriptive accounts of how campaigns and citizens *use* social platforms during elections, through increasingly sceptical work on what platforms *do* to political discourse, into a frontier preoccupied with infrastructure, regulation, and methodological reflexivity. Italian, German, Brazilian, Scandinavian and European Parliament cases recur, but the underlying questions are shared: how do partisanship, polarisation and campaign dynamics emerge from the entanglement of party strategy, audience response, platform affordance, and algorithmic mediation?

### Campaigns as supply: parties, leaders, and platform-specific styles

A first cluster reads campaigns from the supply side. Early Italian work links broadcast and Twitter into a hybrid system, showing that second-screen politics during the 2013 talk-show season was narrow, TV-led and dominated by opinion rather than agenda-shaping [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]]. By 2018, [[Giglietto2019-882f1900]] shows populist-aligned news ecologies (M5S, League) becoming distinctively *insular* on Twitter while M5S supporters strategically amplify favourable Facebook stories and contest unfavourable ones. Cross-platform German work on the 2021 and 2025 elections extends this picture: parties differentiate visual strategies between candidate and party Instagram accounts and between stories and posts [[Achmann-Denkler2026-lx]], and target X and TikTok in starkly different ways, with TikTok favouring a multimodal "politainment" style [[Schulte2026-df]][[Hepp2026-oi]]. The Scandinavian case adds a temporal dimension: a decade of Norwegian Progress Party Facebook posts grows steadily more negative, especially under new leadership, while the election-year rhythm fades into permanent campaigning [[Larsson2026-ro]]. The Sweden Democrats' 2024 EU campaign shows the same platform-tailoring logic taken to its securitising edge, with TikTok memes mobilising foreign footage to manufacture domestic crisis for younger audiences [[Bouchafra2026-ts]].

### Demand-side dynamics: emotion, engagement, and partisan asymmetry

A second cluster turns to the audience. Facebook Reactions become a workhorse instrument: a systematic review confirms that sociopolitical content draws broader, more negative affect and that anger reliably drives engagement [[Anwar2024-34dba628]]. Scandinavian elections operationalise this directly, showing 'Angry' reactions predict sharing while 'Love' suppresses it, with populist actors disproportionately mobilising the former [[Kalsnes2025-zb]]. Anger and negativity also drive engagement for the Norwegian Progress Party over a decade [[Larsson2026-ro]]. Cross-national 2024 EP coverage shows the Radical Right enjoying *disproportionate* media visibility relative to polls and outcomes, intensifying late in the campaign [[Iris2026-pg]] — a structural rather than outlet-level asymmetry that complements the claim, from the US, that connective ties between hyper-partisan media and ideologically extreme legislators reward factional behaviour with attention and engagement [[Knupfer2025-vt]].

### Misinformation, coordination, and influence operations

The Italian Coordinated Link Sharing Behavior programme reframes disinformation as a problem of *behaviour* rather than content, showing coordinated networks disproportionately spread problematic domains across the 2018 and 2019 elections [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]], and develops dynamic detection workflows for the 2022 snap election that surface ideologically, commercially and religiously motivated operations [[Giglietto2023-fa71a001]]. This logic travels: Romanian EP-election analysis documents hyperactive users amplifying party content under Meta's nose [[Oprea2025-lf]], Brazilian WhatsApp groups host coordinated misinformation that materially shapes street mobilisation including the January 8 events [[Kansaon2025-id]], and Philippine campaigns are reread as brokerage enterprises whose manipulative capacity rests on political-economic infrastructure [[Gaw2025-ru]]. US studies push further: deceptive networks in 2020 reached tens of millions but mostly via *reshares by non-network accounts*, with three operations driving most reach and effects largely vanishing after controls [[Appel2026-qr]], while elected officials' own misinformation sharing is conditioned by institutional and ideological position [[Tai2026-qk]]. Targeted digital voter suppression ads, by contrast, show effects that average-treatment designs missed: heterogeneous turnout depression concentrated on geo-racially targeted subgroups [[Kim2026-wg]].

### Polarisation, sorting, and contested epistemic authority

A further line of work moves from misinformation to its attitudinal consequences. Belief in electoral misinformation during Brazil's 2022 election predicts rising intolerance over a three-wave panel, with WhatsApp news use operating indirectly through misinformation belief [[Rossini2026-jn]]. The hostile-misinformation effect generalises this: across Germany, the Netherlands and Poland in 2024, partisans believe their *own* side is the target, especially when they are interested, identified, extreme, or right-wing [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]]. Brazilian parliamentarians' Twitter contests over the Manaus collapse [[Rodarte2026-dk]][[Li2026-wq]] show elites pursuing epistemic authority through three distinct strategies — derivative reporting, direct state-aligned authority, and proximity-based brokerage — competing directly with journalistic gatekeeping. Reddit work pushes back on naive public-sphere readings, showing minimal cross-ideological overlap even between discursively similar subreddits and arguing for polarisation as a sedimented entanglement of platform, moderation and discourse [[Dehghan2026-sy]]; partisan curation bubbles likewise operate at the *story* rather than domain level on Twitter and Facebook Green2025-rq [[Green2025-ap]]. Brazil's 2024 X ban shows that even regulatory shocks can produce durable partisan "sorting ratchets," with conservatives circumventing and liberals exiting, leaving the residual environment more right-leaning [[Ventura2026-yc]].

### Algorithms, ads, and the limits of platform effects research

Algorithmic mediation is the explicit object of a fifth cluster. Bakshy's foundational Facebook study argued individual choice mattered more than the News Feed for cross-cutting exposure [[Bakshy2015-rn]], but more recent work complicates that settlement: González-Bailón shows misinformation's distinctive diffusion dynamics during the 2020 US election [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]]; Allcott et al. find no detectable effects of removing political ads on Facebook/Instagram, though underpowered for turnout [[Allcott2025-jb]]; and a 2023 X experiment shows the algorithmic feed *causally* shifts attitudes rightward and induces persistent conservative follow patterns even after deactivation, helping reconcile prior Meta nulls [[Gauthier2026-iq]]. A Bluesky Registered Report goes further still, showing engagement-based feeds amplify intergroup-moralised-emotional content and distort perceived social norms, while a "diversified extremity" algorithm mitigates these effects without hurting enjoyment [[Brady2026-ln]]. Bots act as agenda-builders especially on attribute (sentiment) salience in the 2022 Ohio midterms [[Arceneaux2026-xk]], and ad-delivery algorithms themselves shape who sees what at what cost [[Votta2025-xz]]. Meta's own under-disclosed political-content reduction policy substantially cut Italian MPs' Facebook reach, while extremist accounts compensated through posting volume [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]]. Munger's metascientific critique of the Meta2020 partnership pulls these threads together: large field experiments may produce precise numerical estimates with vanishing temporal and poetic validity, motivating proactive regulation over reactive science [[Munger2025-cz]]. The asymmetric persuasive power of LLM dialogues across US, Canadian and Polish elections extends the algorithmic-influence question into generative AI [[Lin2025-xp]].

### Rumour, narrative, and collective sensemaking

A more interpretive strand reads electoral discourse as participatory storytelling. Around US 2022, evidence-frame analysis shows that election-integrity rumours are produced through framing operations on largely accurate evidence, often as "call and response" between influencers and audiences [[Starbird2025-jj]]; by 2022, audiences increasingly interpret novel artefacts through the established 2020 "deep story" of fraud, with influencers merely cueing rather than explaining [[Prochaska2025-ef]]. Brazilian narrative contests during Manaus exhibit a parallel logic at the elite level [[Rodarte2026-dk]][[Li2026-wq]].

### Methods, infrastructure, and the regulated data environment

Finally, an increasingly self-conscious methodological literature situates all of the above within a constrained, regulated data environment. The DSA reopens partial access but unevenly across platforms and researcher communities [[Philipp2026-tl]]; YouTube content moderation audits during the 2024 EP elections expose inconsistent publisher labels and opaque removal communications [[Jurg2025-ur]]; volunteer browser plugins audit Brazilian Facebook ads where platforms will not [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]]; and German multi-platform analyses argue that observability itself should be treated as reusable research infrastructure [[Schulte2026-df]][[Hepp2026-oi]]. Multimodal LLMs are validated for visual political communication [[Achmann-Denkler2026-lx]], LLMs-in-the-loop pipelines for Italian elections require new validation regimes [[Marino2024-2fbc690f]], and large-N news-circulation cartographies push beyond outlet-level partisanship measures [[Gaisbauer2025-by]][[Kristensen2025-ni]]. WhatsApp deactivation experiments extend the causal toolkit into encrypted spaces [[Ventura2025-sw]], while cross-national survey work shows that far-right performance itself shapes disinformation concerns [[Gattermann2025-yx]]. Giglietto's earlier programmatic essay anticipates much of this, naming networked publics, micro-targeting and attention competition as the three vectors reshaping political communication scholarship [[F2020-6278a4aa]].

### Where the arc points

Read together, the corpus moves from "do parties post differently?" through "does this content polarise audiences?" to "what does it even mean to measure platform effects when the platforms, the ads, the algorithms, the regulations and the researchers' own access are all changing simultaneously?" Populist and radical-right actors recur across cases as the most adept exploiters of platform asymmetries — in visibility [[Iris2026-pg]], affect [[Kalsnes2025-zb]], visuality [[Bouchafra2026-ts]], coordination [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]] and factional connectivity [[Knupfer2025-vt]]. Algorithms appear capable of both amplifying and constraining these dynamics [[Gauthier2026-iq]][[Brady2026-ln]][[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]]. And the field increasingly recognises, with Munger, that its own knowledge claims depend on infrastructures of access that are themselves political [[Munger2025-cz]][[Philipp2026-tl]][[Schulte2026-df]].
