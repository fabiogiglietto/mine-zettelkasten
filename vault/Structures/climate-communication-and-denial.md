# Climate Communication, Visualization, and Misinformation

## The communicative arc: from images to messages to denial

The papers in this folder trace a single, increasingly granular question: how does climate meaning get *made*, *moved*, and *resisted* in public communication? [[Bravo2026-ue]] sets the widest frame, mapping two decades of research on climate visualization in the media and diagnosing the field's blind spots — an over-reliance on traditional Western news outlets and a corresponding neglect of social-media imagery and computational methods. That diagnosis effectively names the terrain the other three papers occupy: persuasion experiments on textual messages, multimodal denial on Instagram, and content-vs-logic taxonomies of misinformation.

## Persuasion and its limits

[[Voelkel2026-lc]] takes the persuasion question head-on with a megastudy of the ten most-cited climate messaging strategies. The result is a sobering correction to a literature long built on single-study claims: most effects are small (1–4 percentage points), several canonical strategies fail to replicate, and — strikingly — almost none of the partisan-targeted framings outperform generic ones. Attitudes shift; donations do not. This finding crystallizes a classic attitude–behaviour gap and, methodologically, advances megastudies as a corrective to the underpowered, paradigm-siloed designs that produced the original "most-cited" claims. Read against [[Bravo2026-ue]], it also suggests that the substantive payoff of better *visual* communication research may be similarly modest unless paired with comparable methodological rigor.

## Misinformation: content over logic

[[Lieu2025-nl]] pivots from pro-climate persuasion to its mirror image, testing whether the rhetorical *form* (FLICC fallacies) or the rhetorical *content* (CARDS categories) of denial drives its uptake. Content wins decisively: fallacy type has no detectable effect, but attacks on climate *solutions* are perceived as most credible and produce the sharpest ideological polarization. This complicates the "logic-checking" program by suggesting that what a denial claim is *about* matters more than how it is logically structured — a finding that dovetails with [[Voelkel2026-lc]]'s observation that effective pro-climate messages shift multiple mediators simultaneously, making mechanism isolation difficult on both sides of the persuasion ledger.

## The visual turn in denial

[[Gardam2025-er]] returns us to the gap [[Bravo2026-ue]] identified, but from the denialist side: climate conspiracy discourse on Instagram is irreducibly multimodal, and text-first methods systematically underrepresent its rhetorical force. Where [[Lieu2025-nl]] holds platform aesthetics constant (fake Facebook posts) to isolate content category, [[Gardam2025-er]] argues the aesthetics *are* the message on image-first platforms. The two papers thus stake out complementary methodological positions on how to study denial: experimental-textual versus interpretive-visual.

## Open lines

A productive tension runs across the set. [[Voelkel2026-lc]] and [[Lieu2025-nl]] share a quantitative, taxonomy-driven sensibility — comparing message paradigms on common dependent variables — while [[Bravo2026-ue]] and [[Gardam2025-er]] argue that the visual register, where most public climate communication now lives, demands different (and largely undeveloped) tools. A natural next move for the kasten is to ask whether megastudy logic could be ported to visual stimuli, and whether CARDS-style content taxonomies travel into the image-first denial ecologies that [[Gardam2025-er]] describes.
