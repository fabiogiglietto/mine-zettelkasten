# Platform Governance and Data Access

## From open APIs to regulated access: an arc of contraction

The papers gathered here trace a roughly twenty-year arc in which social media moved from a relatively open empirical object to an increasingly closed, contested, and politically charged research environment. [[Freelon2024-sc]] periodizes this history across five phases — from API prehistory through the current regime of "limited options" and "academic walled gardens" — while [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] supplies the bibliometric receipt: a "Golden Age" of Twitter-based research yielding 33,306 studies and 610,738 citations, followed by stagnation in 2023 and a 13% decline in 2024 after the $42,000/month Enterprise pricing. [[Yang2026-tq]] generalizes the pattern across disciplines, showing that the share of social-science papers using social media data tripled before plateauing in 2022–2024 in lockstep with API closures. [[Bruns2026-yv]] narrates the same arc as a story of Twitter's "death," and [[Tornberg2026-lc]], [[Boyd2026-op]], [[Helmond2026-ll]], and [[Baym2026-tr]] argue, in companion anniversary essays, that the very object of study — "social media" — is dissolving into algorithmic broadcasting, parasocial consumption, and AI-mediated infrastructures, eroding the analytical adequacy of methods built for a networked-publics era.

## The DSA as inflection point: promise, friction, and asymmetry

Several papers treat Article 40 of the EU Digital Services Act as the central regulatory event reshaping this landscape. [[Ohme2026-nv]], [[Pierri2025-hm]], and [[de-Vreese2026-zx]] frame it as a genuine break with "independence by permission," while urging realism about implementation: opaque procedures, under-resourced Digital Services Coordinators, narrow platform interpretations, and a "circular resource problem" disadvantaging early-career researchers. [[Peters2026-mo]] documents how data *quality* was almost absent from official DSA texts and had to be inserted by academic and NGO advocacy during consultation — with Meta strategically inverting the argument to claim platform data was too poor to share. [[Philipp2026-tl]] and [[Lukito2026-nb]] survey the resulting patchwork of new APIs and portals, finding stark variation across platforms and persistent inequities of "price, proficiency, or permission" that fall hardest on non-WEIRD researchers. Audits of specific endpoints intensify the skepticism: [[Entrena-Serrano2025-gw]] on TikTok's Research API and [[Rieder2025-ju]] on YouTube's "forgetful by design" search endpoint show that compliance gestures often fail to deliver the substantive observability the DSA envisions. [[Schulte2026-df]] and [[Hepp2026-oi]] reframe the problem as infrastructural — calling for platform observability to be treated as reusable research infrastructure rather than a series of one-off scrapes.

## Industry influence and the politics of platform-cooperative research

A second cluster turns the critical lens onto platform-academic *collaborations* themselves. [[Bak-Coleman2025-pm]] and [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] argue that tech companies exert influence comparable to tobacco, pharma, and fossil fuels, but amplified by exclusive control over data: roughly half of high-profile social media papers have disclosable industry ties, the vast majority undisclosed, and "industrial saturation" of the field approaches 80% once editors and reviewers are included. [[Heiss2026-qv]] develops the parallel argument for European research ecosystems. [[Munger2025-cz]] offers a sympathetic but devastating meta-scientific critique of the Meta2020 partnership, contending that even the field's most ambitious collaboration produces estimates of unclear external validity on platforms that change faster than science can replicate. [[Allen2025-ot]] picks up the methodological response: as APIs close and collaborations remain sporadic, browser-extension and LLM-based "platform-independent" experiments become essential. [[Gauthier2026-iq]] exemplifies this turn with a 4,965-user X feed experiment finding conservative-leaning attitudinal effects that the FIES null findings did not detect, while [[Brady2026-ln]] runs a parallel intervention on Bluesky, showing engagement-based feeds amplify intergroup-moralized-emotional content and distort norm perceptions. [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]] illustrates earlier crowdsourced auditing in the Brazilian context.

## Platform discourse, governance theater, and the politics of "change"

A third strand reads platforms as discursive and political actors. [[Hurcombe2025-cs]] shows how Meta's Newsroom strategically frames misinformation as the work of "inauthentic" outsiders, exporting terms like "coordinated inauthentic behaviour" into regulatory vocabularies. [[De2026-ld]] generalizes this into a theory of "changecraft" — the discursive flexibility by which Meta, TikTok, YouTube, and X aestheticize infrastructure shifts, reframe ideological pivots as continuity, and patchwork incremental change. [[Weinbrand2026-sf]] extends the analysis to Google's AI Overviews and the platformization of knowledge itself. [[Donovan2025-ws]] historicizes "misinformation-at-scale" as a structural feature of engagement-driven business models, while [[Moran2025-qn]] documents the retrenchment of the trust-and-safety profession amid political backlash. [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] and [[Farkas2026-lr]] (with its near-duplicate [[Shi2026-ko]]) examine the rhetorical and material consequences of Meta's January 2025 abandonment of third-party fact-checking, finding that fact-checkers defend their platform ties through transcendence and differentiation while rejecting Zuckerberg's "censorship" framing. [[Mahl2026-hc]] aggregates expert judgment across these debates: platform governance and journalism interventions are rated more important than individual-level fixes, and there is "no silver bullet."

## Algorithmic effects, visibility, and the limits of moderation

A fourth cluster empirically interrogates what platforms actually *do* to content. [[Bouchaud2026-lr]] reverse-engineers X's recommender to show it inadvertently encodes users' Left-Right ideology with ρ=0.887. [[Efstratiou2025-gs]] finds that post-Musk algorithmic amplification of right-leaning accounts is largely explained by agitating content and proximity to Musk himself. [[Bastos2025-ya]], [[McNally2025-dn]], and [[Bastos2025-ol]] (overlapping analyses) argue that Facebook's News Feed is not a black box: documented ranking updates produce measurable, lagged effects on hard-news engagement. [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] extends this longitudinally, showing partisan reach penalties and journalistic-quality rewards that fluctuate with Facebook's "break the glass" interventions, while [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]] and [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]] document Meta's political content reduction policy producing a 72% reach decline for Italian MPs — implemented ten months before Meta's announced rollout and never disclosed in DSA compliance reports. [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] earlier showed how even the 100-shares threshold in Meta's URL dataset injects platform-wide algorithmic signals into ostensibly social data. On moderation's limits: [[Karo2026-dn]] catalogs jihadist evasion tactics on TikTok, [[Rieder2026-pp]] maps the "Tate-space" persisting on YouTube despite deplatforming, [[Jurg2025-ur]] audits YouTube's authoritative-source promotion during EU elections, [[Tonneau2025-bv]] documents stark language disparities in moderator allocation, and [[Simeone2025-vo]] shows targeted deplatforming can produce outsized network ripple effects. [[Renault2025-uh]] finds that even crowdsourced Community Notes flag Republican posts 2.3 times more often, undermining Musk's and Zuckerberg's rationale for replacing professional fact-checkers. [[Ventura2026-yc]] and [[Kristensen2025-ni]] document the partisan and polarization consequences of platform bans and alternative-platform migration. [[Ahuja2025-ku]] and [[Votta2025-xz]] examine, respectively, dark-pattern enforcement under DSA Article 25 and ad-delivery algorithm effects on campaign reach.

## What the platforms have become — and what research must become

Across these threads, a shared diagnosis emerges. The "social" of social media has receded into parasocial consumption ([[Boyd2026-op]]), algorithmic broadcasting ([[Tornberg2026-lc]]), and AI infrastructure ([[Helmond2026-ll]], [[Weinbrand2026-sf]]); platforms have become discursive, political, and infrastructural powers whose self-narration ([[Hurcombe2025-cs]], [[De2026-ld]]) and self-knowledge ([[Munger2025-cz]]) are themselves objects of contestation; scams, fraud, and AI-enabled harm have moved from periphery to core ([[Swartz2026-zb]], [[Schiffrin_undated-gi]], [[Vincent_undated-re]]); and democratic backsliding has become entangled with platform dynamics ([[Lewandowsky2026-ob]]). The methodological response is plural and uneasy: defend and operationalize DSA Article 40 ([[Ohme2026-nv]], [[de-Vreese2026-zx]], [[Pierri2025-hm]]), build independent experimental and donation infrastructures ([[Allen2025-ot]], [[Gauthier2026-iq]], [[Brady2026-ln]], [[Bouchaud2026-lr]]), treat observability as a shared research infrastructure ([[Schulte2026-df]], [[Lukito2026-nb]]), and insist on transparency requirements that catch undisclosed policy shifts like Meta's political-content reduction ([[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]]). The unfinished entry [[Unknown2025-ed60bc90]] would presumably take its place among these state-of-the-field assessments. What unites them is a recognition that data access is not a technical convenience but a question of informational power — and that the next decade of platform research will be defined by whether independent scholarship can keep its footing on terrain that platforms, regulators, and AI infrastructures are actively reshaping beneath it.
