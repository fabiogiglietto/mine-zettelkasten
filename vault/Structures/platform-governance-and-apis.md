# Platform Governance, APIs and Data Access

## From Open APIs to Walled Gardens: A Periodization

The natural anchor for this collection is Freelon's updated periodization of the "post-API age" [[Freelon2024-sc]], which traces five eras from API prehistory through today's fragmented regime of laissez-faire, academic, walled-garden, and pay-to-play access. Murtfeldt et al.'s eulogy for the Twitter API quantifies what was lost: 33,306 studies, 610,738 citations, and a 13% decline in Twitter-based publications in 2024 alone [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]]. Yang et al. extend this bibliometrically across disciplines, showing the proportion of social-media-based social science tripled before plateauing in 2022–2024 precisely as APIs closed [[Yang2026-tq]]. Bruns' essay on Twitter's "death" supplies the qualitative counterpart — the loss not only of data but of a distinctive infrastructure for public debate [[Bruns2026-yv]] — while Törnberg argues the underlying object of study has itself dissolved, displaced by algorithmic broadcasting, semi-private spheres, and AI-mediated communication [[Tornberg2026-lc]].

## The DSA Moment: Promise and Implementation

Article 40 DSA is the regulatory pivot around which much of this literature now turns. Ohme et al. frame it as a substantive change in research conditions [[Ohme2026-nv]], and Pierri et al., drawing on their ERC-supported pilot with DG-CONNECT, document the misaligned incentives, resource asymmetries, and the "circular resource problem" that disadvantage early-career researchers under the new regime [[Pierri2025-hm]]. Peters et al. show that data *quality* — as opposed to mere access — was almost absent from official EU documents, raised mainly by academics and NGOs during consultation, and only partially smuggled into the final Delegated Regulation [[Peters2026-mo]]. Philipp et al. and Lukito et al. survey what this means in practice for election research, cataloguing platforms' inconsistent API implementations and the "price, proficiency, or permission" barriers that still gate access [[Philipp2026-tl]] [[Lukito2026-nb]]. A complementary descriptive overview of the DSA-era tooling landscape is offered by [[Unknown2025-ed60bc90]].

## Auditing the New Access Regimes

Several papers take the DSA-mandated tools themselves as their object of critical scrutiny. Rieder et al.'s audit of YouTube's Data API documents systematic "forgetfulness" — a temporal decay that renders the endpoint inadequate for systemic risk research [[Rieder2025-ju]] — while Jurg et al.'s audit of YouTube moderation during the 2024 EU elections finds inconsistent application of publisher labels and opaque removal statements [[Jurg2025-ur]]. Entrena-Serrano et al. report parallel inadequacies in TikTok's Research API [[Entrena-Serrano2025-gw]], and Tonneau et al. use DSA transparency disclosures themselves to expose stark cross-lingual inequalities in moderator allocation [[Tonneau2025-bv]]. Giglietto's earlier work on Meta's URL Shares Dataset already demonstrated how a single design choice — the 100-share threshold — could distort cross-national research [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]]. Schulte et al. and the related paper indexed under Hepp synthesize these challenges into a programmatic call to treat platform observability as reusable *research infrastructure* rather than a series of one-off projects [[Schulte2026-df]] [[Hepp2026-oi]].

## Industry Influence and the Limits of Collaboration

A parallel strand interrogates what platform-mediated research can ever produce. Bak-Coleman and colleagues' two papers offer the sharpest critique: roughly half of high-profile social media research has disclosable industry ties (mostly undisclosed), industry-tied papers receive double the citations and policy mentions, and topical biases steer the field toward user-focused rather than platform-level questions [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] [[Bak-Coleman2025-pm]]. Heiss et al. extend the analogy to tobacco, pharma, and food research, arguing that preregistration cannot neutralize platform-controlled data access [[Heiss2026-qv]]. Munger's metascientific autopsy of the Meta2020 partnership comes to a kindred conclusion from a different angle: even the most ambitious academic–industry collaboration cannot deliver temporal or "poetic" validity on platforms that change faster than science can study them [[Munger2025-cz]]. Allen and Tucker, reviewing Piccardi et al.'s browser-extension experiment, treat platform-independent experimentation as the methodological corollary [[Allen2025-ot]], and Gauthier et al.'s independent X experiment — which finds conservative shifts that Meta2020 did not detect on Facebook/Instagram — illustrates what such independence can yield [[Gauthier2026-iq]].

## What Platforms Actually Do

Even with constrained access, several studies use ingenious designs to detect platform interventions. McNally et al. and the parallel Bastos pieces show that Facebook's News Feed is not a black box: documented ranking updates produce measurable, lagged engagement effects ~19–24 days later, concentrated on hard news [[McNally2025-dn]] [[Bastos2025-ya]] [[Bastos2025-ol]]. Giglietto's analyses of Meta's URL Shares Dataset and Content Library extend this longitudinally: partisan-alignment penalties and journalistic-quality rewards fluctuate in synchrony with Meta's "break the glass" interventions [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]], and Meta's political-content reduction policy quietly cut Italian MPs' reach by 72% — ten months before its announced global rollout, and never disclosed in DSA compliance reports [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]] [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]]. Votta et al. add evidence that ad delivery algorithms themselves shape political campaign reach beyond advertiser choices [[Votta2025-xz]]. Efstratiou et al.'s audit of post-Musk Twitter shows the algorithmic feed centralized around Musk himself and rewarded "agitating" content [[Efstratiou2025-gs]], while Brady et al.'s Bluesky field experiment demonstrates both the harms of engagement-based ranking and the feasibility of prosocial alternatives [[Brady2026-ln]].

## Moderation, Deplatforming, and the Retreat from Trust & Safety

Closely interwoven is a literature on how platforms govern content. Donovan's history situates "misinformation-at-scale" as a structural feature of engagement-driven business models and chronicles the post-2021 retreat from moderation [[Donovan2025-ws]], echoed in Moran et al.'s account of the destabilization of the trust-and-safety profession [[Moran2025-qn]]. Cazzamatta et al. empirically rebut Zuckerberg's January 2025 framing of fact-checkers as censors, showing only ~30% of debunked content is actually removed [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]]. Hurcombe et al. complement this with a discourse analysis of Meta's Newsroom, identifying how "inauthenticity" and "coordinated inauthentic behaviour" function as floating signifiers that displace responsibility onto outside actors [[Hurcombe2025-cs]] — a project De et al. generalize across Meta, TikTok, YouTube, and X under the rubric of "changecraft" [[De2026-ld]]. Simeone et al.'s in-situ analysis of the AER community shows targeted deplatforming can flip authority structures [[Simeone2025-vo]], while Rieder et al.'s "Tate-space" study documents the limits of removal when recommendation outpaces moderation [[Rieder2026-pp]]. Ventura et al.'s study of Brazil's X ban surfaces a darker counterpoint: even democratically motivated bans can produce durable partisan "sorting ratchets" [[Ventura2026-yc]].

## Synthesis and Stakes

Read together, the topic traces an arc from the closure of a relatively open research infrastructure, through a regulatory response (the DSA) that has partly reopened access while exposing the political contestation of "data quality" and "transparency," to a growing recognition that independent methods, infrastructural thinking, and structural critique of industry influence must complement any regulatory regime. Mahl et al.'s Delphi study captures the consensus among 47 experts that platform governance and limited researcher data access rank among the field's most pressing concerns [[Mahl2026-hc]]. Lewandowsky and Schiffrin et al. extend the stakes outward — to democratic backsliding and the global infrastructure of AI-driven fraud — arguing that platform accountability is inseparable from broader questions of democratic and consumer protection [[Lewandowsky2026-ob]] [[Schiffrin_undated-gi]]. Weinbrand et al.'s analysis of Google's AI Overviews signals that the same discursive and infrastructural battles are now being refought in the AI-mediated successor to search [[Weinbrand2026-sf]]. Whether the DSA-era settlement will prove a turning point or a holding action remains, across these papers, the field's open question.
