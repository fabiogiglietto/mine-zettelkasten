# AI Red-Teaming as Sociotechnical Practice

## From Technical Procedure to Sociotechnical Practice

The papers in this cluster share a foundational move: they refuse to treat AI evaluation — and red-teaming in particular — as a self-contained technical operation. [[Gillespie2026-aa]] makes the case most pointedly for red-teaming, arguing that what looks like adversarial probing of model outputs is in fact a stack of value judgments, labor arrangements, and psychological burdens. [[Matias2025-px]] generalizes the claim to AI evaluation writ large: because AI systems are sociotechnical artifacts, their reliability cannot be established without attending to the social and organizational layers in which they operate. [[Unknown2025-qj]] sits between these, taking red-teaming specifically as its object but emphasizing its democratic and public-interest valences. Together they constitute an emerging argument that the *science* of AI evaluation is inseparable from the *politics* of who participates in it.

## Labor, Value Judgments, and the Ghost-Work Lineage

[[Gillespie2026-aa]] situates red-teaming within the longer history of commercial content moderation and ghost work, arguing that the field is replaying patterns of outsourcing, NDA-bound opacity, and worker harm that took content moderation a decade to surface. The value-laden nature of identifying "harms" — which harms count, framed by whom, against which normative baselines — is shown to be inseparable from the labor arrangements that make red-teaming possible. This framing exposes a tension internal to industry self-regulation: when AI companies define harms internally and proprietarily, the people doing the imaginative, transgressive work of finding failures are simultaneously least equipped (by demographic narrowness) and most exposed (by psychological risk) to do it well. The paper's warning against narratives of imminent automation echoes longstanding STS skepticism that "the human is about to be removed from the loop."

## Publics as Epistemic Resource

Where [[Gillespie2026-aa]] focuses on the *costs* of the current labor regime, [[Matias2025-px]] and [[Unknown2025-qj]] foreground what is *gained* when evaluation is opened up. [[Matias2025-px]] develops the strongest epistemic argument: public involvement is not a political concession traded against scientific rigor but a means of *improving* rigor. Across five stages — equipoise, measurement, explanation, inference, interpretation — lived-experience expertise produces situated knowledge that internal evaluators systematically miss, as illustrated by the Allegheny Family Screening Tool reanalysis and the Chicago police complaints recoding. [[Unknown2025-qj]] complements this with empirical observation of public red-teaming events, showing that public-facing settings surface vulnerability framings that internal industry red-teaming does not. The two papers converge on the idea that publics are not merely stakeholders to consult but epistemic contributors whose absence degrades the science.

## Tensions: Democratization vs. Extraction

A productive tension runs across the cluster around what public participation actually accomplishes. [[Unknown2025-qj]] reads public red-teaming events as democratizing, broadening who can identify harms. [[Gillespie2026-aa]] is more cautious, noting that volunteer and event-based red-teaming (e.g., DEFCON-style exercises) risks extractive reliance on marginalized communities asked to surface harms that affect them, without commensurate protections or pay. [[Matias2025-px]] anticipates this critique by drawing on participatory science traditions that have developed norms for consent, meaningful contribution, and worker treatment — suggesting that the question is less whether to involve publics than under what institutional conditions. The papers thus collectively pose, but do not resolve, the question of how to distinguish genuine public-interest red-teaming from its co-opted forms.

## Toward a Research and Governance Agenda

All three papers end by gesturing toward institutional change rather than methodological tweaks. [[Gillespie2026-aa]] calls explicitly for a coordinated, interdisciplinary research network to study red-teaming empirically before market-driven forms calcify. [[Matias2025-px]] argues that trustworthy AI presupposes trustworthy science, which presupposes inclusive process — positioning participatory methods as infrastructure for AI governance rather than supplements to it. [[Unknown2025-qj]] suggests that the institutional context of red-teaming events itself shapes what counts as a harm, implying that governance design and epistemic outcomes are entangled. The arc across the cluster moves from diagnosis (red-teaming and evaluation are already sociotechnical, whether acknowledged or not) to prescription (they should be organized deliberately as such, with attention to labor, participation, and the public interest).
