# Platform Governance & Research Data Access

## The Closing Window and the Regulatory Pivot

A clear arc runs through this collection: the once-permissive infrastructure of social media research has collapsed, and a new regulatory order is being negotiated in its place. [[Freelon2024-sc]] periodizes the post-API age across two decades and six platforms, while [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] quantifies its scholarly cost — a 13% decline in Twitter-based publications in 2024 after the $42,000/month Enterprise pricing took effect. [[Yang2026-tq]] extends that picture across disciplines, showing the share of empirical social-media papers tripled before plateauing and declining in 2022–2024 as APIs closed. [[Bruns2026-yv]] reads the same arc as the substantive 'death' of Twitter as a public-debate infrastructure. Together these papers reframe data access as a question not of technical convenience but of what society is permitted to know about itself.

The EU Digital Services Act sits at the center of this collection's hopes and frustrations. [[Pierri2025-hm]], [[Ohme2026-nv]], and [[de-Vreese2026-zx]] cast Article 40 as a landmark transparency mechanism while documenting platform foot-dragging, opaque procedures, and 'independence by permission' dynamics. [[Peters2026-mo]] traces how academic and NGO actors fought, partially successfully, to write *data quality* into the Delegated Regulation — a reminder that DSA implementation is a contested political process, not a technical rollout. [[Heiss2026-qv]] and [[Philipp2026-tl]] echo the warning that narrow eligibility and a circular resource problem (data needed for grants, grants needed for data) may reproduce inequities Article 40 was meant to remedy.

## Audits of What the New Access Actually Yields

A second strand stress-tests the new infrastructure. [[Rieder2025-ju]] shows YouTube's Data API is 'forgetful by design,' losing the majority of findable videos within weeks; [[Jurg2025-ur]] documents inconsistent News Funding Notices and opaque removal statements during the 2024 EU elections. [[Entrena-Serrano2025-gw]] finds TikTok's DSA-expanded Research API riddled with limitations. [[Tonneau2025-bv]] uses DSA transparency data to expose stark cross-lingual disparities in moderation workforces — Global South languages receive a fraction of English's per-content moderator allocation. [[Schulte2026-df]] and [[Hepp2026-oi]] argue from a 2025 German election case that platform observability should itself be treated as reusable research infrastructure rather than improvised study-by-study. [[Lukito2026-nb]] systematizes the landscape across 72 tools, naming the 'price, proficiency, or permission' barriers that determine who can study elections at all.

## Industry Influence and the Integrity of the Evidence Base

Closing data pipelines have intensified concern about the integrity of what remains. [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] finds that ~49% of high-profile social-media papers have disclosable industry ties, mostly undisclosed, with editor and reviewer entanglements pushing 'industrial saturation' to around 80%. [[Bak-Coleman2025-pm]] generalizes this into a tobacco/pharma-style critique of industry-shaped tech research, and [[Heiss2026-qv]] extends the analogy to the social sciences as a whole. [[Munger2025-cz]] reads the Meta 2020 collaboration as the apex *and* the limit of this mode, arguing temporal validity decays too fast for one-off field experiments to govern platforms. [[Allen2025-ot]] responds by championing platform-independent experimental designs (browser extensions, LLM-based reranking) as the necessary middle path. [[Hurcombe2025-cs]] and [[De2026-ld]] document how platforms themselves shape the discursive environment — Meta's Newsroom framing problematic content as exogenous, and 'changecraft' across Meta/TikTok/YouTube/X normalizing infrastructural shifts as continuity.

## What Closures Reveal About Platforms Themselves

A striking feature of this corpus is that several of its empirical findings depend on now-vanishing pipelines. Bastos2025-ya, [[McNally2025-dn]], and Bastos2025-ol use CrowdTangle plus a hand-built timeline of Facebook ranking updates to show that algorithmic interventions produced measurable 19–24-day lagged effects on hard-news engagement — direct evidence against the 'black box' framing. [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] uses Meta's URL Shares Dataset to show that partisan reach penalties and quality rewards spike around the 2020 'break the glass' interventions. [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]] and [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]] use the Meta Content Library to detect a 72% drop in Italian MPs' reach beginning *ten months before* Meta's announced global rollout of political content reduction, with only partial recovery after the January 2025 reversal — and asymmetric effects favoring extremist accounts that increased posting volume. [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]] and [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] further reveal how the very thresholds Meta uses to anonymize datasets generate platform-wide artifacts that researchers must learn to read around. The lesson is double-edged: DSA-enabled infrastructure can surface consequential, undisclosed governance moves, but each new access regime also embeds its own distortions.

## Algorithmic Politics in a Constrained Empirical Field

Within these constraints, a robust experimental literature continues to probe the political effects of feed design. [[Gauthier2026-iq]] finds X's For-You algorithm shifts attitudes rightward and seeds persistent conservative follows even after switch-off. [[Efstratiou2025-gs]] shows the same algorithm centralizes attention on Musk and rewards 'agitating' content, with the partisan tilt largely mediated by behavior. [[Bouchaud2026-lr]] reconstructs X's embedding space via data donation and finds a ρ=0.887 linear direction encoding Left–Right ideology. [[Renault2025-uh]] shows Republicans are flagged on Community Notes 2.3× more often than Democrats even under cross-partisan bridging. [[Brown2026-br]] disentangles YouTube algorithm from user choice, finding rabbit holes but no radicalization pathways and a platform-wide conservative tilt. [[Brady2026-ln]] runs a registered experiment on Bluesky showing engagement-based feeds amplify intergroup-moralized-emotional content and distort perceived social norms — and that prosocial alternatives are feasible. [[Ventura2026-yc]] documents how Brazil's X ban produced durable rightward sorting, and [[Votta2025-xz]] turns the lens on ad-delivery algorithms shaping political reach.

## Moderation, Migration, and the Post-Social Horizon

A final strand reads platform governance through what happens to content and communities. [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] and [[Farkas2026-lr]] examine fact-checkers' position after Meta's 2025 termination of the third-party program, finding removal was always rare (~30%) and that defensive rhetoric sustains fragile partnerships. [[Donovan2025-ws]] historicizes 'misinformation-at-scale' as endogenous to engagement-driven business models, and [[Moran2025-qn]] tracks the destabilization of the trust-and-safety profession. [[Simeone2025-vo]] and [[Rieder2026-pp]] examine deplatforming's reach: targeted bans can flip a network's authority structure, but ambient ideological circulation (the 'Tate-space') routinely outpaces moderation. [[Vincent_undated-re]] and [[Schiffrin_undated-gi]] document ongoing harms — high TikTok mis/disinformation prevalence, industrial-scale deepfake fraud — that strain gatekeeper-based regulation. [[Mahl2026-hc]] and [[Lewandowsky2026-ob]] frame platform accountability as a precondition for democratic resilience.

Stepping back, [[Tornberg2026-lc]], [[Tornberg2025-ir]], and [[Helmond2026-ll]] suggest the object of governance is itself dissolving: a shift from social-graph to algorithmic-broadcast logics, retreat into semi-private spheres, and AI-mediated communication mean that DSA-era access regimes may already be calibrated to a fading paradigm. [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]] and the unreviewable [[Unknown2025-ed60bc90]] round out the picture — independent volunteer auditing and tool inventories will likely remain part of the methodological repertoire even as regulation matures. The collection's central tension is thus that platform governance research is now constituted by, and constrained to, the very access regimes whose adequacy it must adjudicate.
