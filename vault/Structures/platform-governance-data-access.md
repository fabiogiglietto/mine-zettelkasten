# Platform Governance and Research Data Access

## From Open APIs to Walled Gardens: A Periodization

The papers collected here narrate, often explicitly, a historical arc of platform data access. [[Freelon2024-sc]] provides the canonical periodization — from the laissez-faire APIs of the late 2000s through Cambridge Analytica, the Academic API's brief "Golden Age," and the current patchwork of paywalled, vetted, and unofficial routes — which [[Giglietto2026-855a54cb]] echoes specifically for Twitter/X as a trajectory from "Wild West to Walled Garden." [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] quantifies the consequences with a bibliometric eulogy to Twitter as research infrastructure: 25% annual growth through 2022, stagnation in 2023, a 13% decline in 2024. Bastos2025-ya reads as a companion farewell. [[Yang2026-tq]]'s systematic review of 2010–2024 social science corroborates this trajectory at scale, showing that the entire empirical study of social media plateaued just as APIs closed. The arc is summarized for Twitter specifically by [[Bruns2026-yv]]'s elegiac account of the platform's "death" and the failure of successors (Mastodon, Threads, Bluesky) to address the underlying governance problems that doomed it.

## The DSA as Pivot: Opportunity and Friction

Article 40 of the EU Digital Services Act runs through almost every recent paper as the central institutional response. [[Ohme2026-nv]] and [[Philipp2026-tl]] frame Article 40 as a substantive opening for platform research, particularly around elections; [[Pierri2025-hm]] reports from inside an ERC pilot on the misaligned incentives, resource asymmetries, and "circular" funding problems that hobble implementation. [[de-Vreese2026-zx]] reads current turbulence not as failure but as evidence that binding regulation is finally biting — counseling a "marathon" stance. [[Lukito2026-nb]]'s catalog of 72 access tools formalizes the resulting landscape as a "price, proficiency, or permission" problem, while [[Schulte2026-df]] reframes the issue as one of **research infrastructure** rather than individual study design. [[Peters2026-mo]] tracks the political negotiation of *data quality* itself within the DSA consultation process, showing that academics and NGOs — not platforms or regulators — pushed the concept onto the agenda. Audits of the new access regimes are uniformly critical: [[Entrena-Serrano2025-gw]] documents persistent flaws in TikTok's Research API; [[Rieder2025-ju]] shows YouTube's search endpoint is "forgetful by design"; [[Unknown2025-ed60bc90]] surveys the state of play across tools. [[Tonneau2025-bv]] uses DSA transparency reports themselves to expose stark cross-lingual disparities in moderator allocation.

## Industry Influence and the Crisis of Independence

A parallel literature questions whether platform-cooperative research can ever be independent. [[Heiss2026-qv]] draws explicit analogies to tobacco and pharma; [[Bak-Coleman2025-pm]] and [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] develop this into a major empirical indictment, finding that roughly half of high-profile social media papers have disclosable industry ties, mostly undisclosed, with an estimated "industrial saturation" of ~80% once editors and reviewers are counted. [[Munger2025-cz]] turns the same lens on the Meta 2020 partnership, arguing that even its methodological apex reveals the structural limits of reactive empirical science on rapidly-changing platforms. [[Allen2025-ot]] reads more optimistically about platform-independent experimentation via browser extensions, while [[Bouchaud2026-lr]] demonstrates the power of data donation to reverse-engineer X's recommender embeddings without platform cooperation. [[Farkas2026-lr]] examines an adjacent dependency relationship — European fact-checkers' rhetorical defenses of their platform funding ties.

## Algorithmic Curation: From Networked to Clustered Publics

A second strand theorizes what platforms have *become* under algorithmic curation. [[Gerbaudo2026-fo]] argues TikTok represents a "second generation" shift from networked to *clustered* publics — item-centric, statistically constructed, opaque. [[Tornberg2026-lc]] generalizes this into a call for "post-social media studies," joined by [[Boyd2026-op]]'s proposal to rename the field "parasocial media." [[De2026-ld]] analyzes the discursive work — "changecraft" — by which platforms narrate these transitions as continuity. Empirical audits document the political consequences: [[Gauthier2026-iq]] finds X's algorithmic feed causally shifts attitudes rightward and creates persistent following patterns; [[Efstratiou2025-gs]] shows post-acquisition Twitter centralized around Musk himself and rewarded agitation; [[Brown2026-br]] finds no echo chambers or radicalization pathways on YouTube but strong rabbit holes and a platform-wide conservative tilt. [[McNally2025-dn]] uses CrowdTangle data to show that News Feed interventions produce detectable, lagged effects on engagement with hard news. [[Bakshy2015-rn]]'s landmark Facebook study sits as the precursor against which much of this work explicitly positions itself.

## Visibility Policies, Moderation, and Their Limits

A third cluster examines concrete moderation and visibility regimes. [[Giglietto2025-1765bb4f]] and its sibling [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]] use the DSA-enabled Meta Content Library to show that Meta's political content reduction policy cut Italian MPs' reach by 72% — and was rolled out ten months before publicly announced. [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] extends the analysis to US URLs, finding partisan-audience penalties and quality rewards that fluctuate with known governance interventions like the 2020 "break the glass" measures. [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]] and [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] document earlier methodological complications of Meta's URL Shares Dataset, particularly the artifact-producing 100-share threshold. [[Hurcombe2025-cs]] traces how Meta's Newsroom discursively framed problematic content as external. [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] documents the rupture of Zuckerberg's January 2025 dismantling of third-party fact-checking; [[Renault2025-uh]] uses Community Notes data to undermine the bias rationale offered for the shift; [[Donovan2025-ws]] places the entire arc in historical context as a retreat from the post-2016 moderation consensus. [[Moran2025-qn]] documents the parallel upheaval in trust-and-safety as a profession. [[Jurg2025-ur]] and [[Rieder2026-pp]] show the limits of moderation through case audits of YouTube — election coverage and the persistent post-deplatforming "Tate-space." [[Simeone2025-vo]] offers a more optimistic reading from the earlier deplatforming era, showing real network ripple effects from the 2021 election-audit account suspensions. Bastos2025-ol examines troll farm visual identities through Twitter's Moderation Research Consortium.

## Downstream Consequences and Edge Cases

Several papers track the downstream consequences of these governance dynamics. [[Ventura2026-yc]] documents how Brazil's X ban produced a durable partisan "sorting ratchet." [[Vincent_undated-re]] reports continued high mis/disinformation prevalence on TikTok in European measurements. [[Karo2026-dn]] and [[Nangle2026-yo]] show how extremist actors — jihadist and far-right respectively — exploit algorithmic affordances and softening strategies to evade moderation. [[Ahuja2025-ku]] develops a "law-to-design" framework operationalizing DSA Article 25's dark-pattern prohibitions. [[Votta2025-xz]] turns to ad delivery algorithms in political campaigns. [[Schiffrin_undated-gi]] extends the gatekeeper logic to deepfake-enabled financial fraud, and [[Lewandowsky2026-ob]] situates the whole apparatus within democratic backsliding. [[Mahl2026-hc]]'s Delphi study consolidates expert consensus that platform governance — not individual-level interventions — is where the most important leverage lies. [[Brady2026-ln]] offers a constructive endpoint: a registered field experiment on Bluesky showing that *prosocial* algorithm design can reduce IME amplification and correct norm misperceptions without sacrificing platform enjoyment.

## Threads Across the Collection

Three meta-themes recur. First, **independence by permission** is a structural condition — invoked by [[Freelon2024-sc]], [[Pierri2025-hm]], [[Heiss2026-qv]], and [[Giglietto2026-855a54cb]] — that even the DSA only partly displaces. Second, the field is converging on the view that platforms are not neutral conduits but **active curators** whose interventions are temporally volatile, often undisclosed, and consequential at scale — a thesis advanced empirically by the Giglietto papers, [[McNally2025-dn]], [[Gauthier2026-iq]], and [[Efstratiou2025-gs]]. Third, the very category of "social media" is dissolving: [[Tornberg2026-lc]], [[Boyd2026-op]], and [[Gerbaudo2026-fo]] each propose new vocabularies (post-social, parasocial, clustered), suggesting that the closure of APIs is occurring just as the object of study itself transforms — a doubly moving target that makes the regulatory and infrastructural questions raised by [[de-Vreese2026-zx]], [[Lukito2026-nb]], and [[Schulte2026-df]] all the more urgent.
