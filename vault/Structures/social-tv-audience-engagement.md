# Social TV & Audience Engagement

## Hybrid Media, Second Screens, and the Limits of Networked Participation

The single paper currently filed here, [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]], sets the conceptual and empirical baseline for this strand of work: political talk-show audiences on Twitter are not an autonomous "networked public" but a *hybrid* formation in which broadcast logic continues to dictate rhythm, agenda, and visibility. The 2013 Italian general election campaign is treated as a natural experiment in second-screening, with nearly 2.5 million hashtagged tweets across 1,076 episodes serving as the evidentiary spine. The analytical move — borrowed from Chadwick's hybrid media system and Couldry's practice approach — is to refuse the dichotomy between "old" and "new" media and to ask instead what audiences, politicians, and broadcasters *do* in the seams between them.

### From participatory promise to constrained practice

A through-line of the argument is the gap between the participatory affordances ascribed to Twitter and the thin, asymmetrical practices actually observed. [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] shows that only a narrow slice of the Twitter population engaged at all, that broadcasters rarely incorporated tweets on-air, and that party accounts (M5S most strikingly) largely abstained from the conversation. This positions second-screening less as a democratizing supplement to TV than as a *spontaneous* audience activity tolerated rather than orchestrated by the Italian TV industry — a finding that anchors later questions in this Zettelkasten about whether engagement metrics translate into substantive influence on political agendas.

### Participation "in" vs. "through" the media

The paper's most portable conceptual distinction is between participation *through* the media (commenting, expressing opinions, requesting interaction) and participation *in* the media (introducing new issues, frames, or sources into the agenda). Empirically, the former overwhelms the latter: ~80% opinion/comment, ~29% requests for interaction, but only 2.4% agenda-shaping contributions. This skew — combined with the elevated use of irony and vulgar language in interaction requests — frames second-screen talk as expressive and affective rather than deliberative, a characterization that future entries under this topic can test against entertainment formats or non-electoral contexts.

### TV logic as the gravitational center

Finally, [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] documents how tweet peaks track broadcast schedules and media events rather than disrupting them: Berlusconi's appearance on *Servizio Pubblico* structures PDL-related conversation; PD tweets follow issue streaming; M5S's absence is itself a function of Grillo's media strategy. The implication for the broader research line is that Twitter-TV audiences should be studied as *co-produced* with broadcast programming, which motivates the PI's ongoing attention to hashtag publics, talk-show formats, and the institutional asymmetries between audiences and elites. Subsequent notes filed here should extend, qualify, or complicate this baseline — particularly by examining whether non-political genres, later platforms, or differently structured media systems reproduce or break the patterns observed in 2013.
