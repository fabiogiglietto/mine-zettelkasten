# Social TV and Audience Engagement

## Hybrid Media Systems as the Frame

The single anchor under this topic, [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]], sets the conceptual register for how Social TV is approached here: not as a parallel digital sphere displacing broadcast, but as a *hybrid* space in which TV logic and networked publics co-produce political talk. Drawing on Chadwick's hybrid media systems and Couldry's practice approach, the paper insists that Twitter activity around political talk shows must be read *through* the broadcast agenda rather than against it. This framing is what continues to anchor a view of second-screen practice as interdependence rather than disruption.

## Participation "In" vs. "Through" the Media

A central analytical move in [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] is the distinction between participation *in* the media (shaping the agenda, introducing issues, angles, sources) and participation *through* the media (commenting, reacting, asking for interaction). The empirical weight falls overwhelmingly on the latter: roughly 80% of coded tweets are opinions/comments and only 2.4% introduce new issues or sources. Audiences are present and vocal, but their contribution is largely reactive — a finding that complicates more optimistic readings of second-screen activity as agenda-building from below.

## Narrow Access and Asymmetric Engagement

Despite an impressive 1.89 million tweets during airtime, only ~187,000 unique users participated — a small slice of the Italian Twitter population, itself a slice of the TV audience. Politicians and broadcasters, with rare exceptions, did not meaningfully engage back: only 3 of 11 talk shows surfaced tweets on air, party accounts contributed under a thousand tweets, and M5S was effectively absent from this hybrid space even as it dominated the offline campaign. [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] thus tempers claims about networked audiences by documenting the structural thinness of the connected public and the failure of institutional actors to activate it.

## TV Logic Shapes the Networked Conversation

The temporal and thematic contours of Twitter activity track the broadcast calendar: peaks align with episodes and media events, and content clusters reflect what is happening on screen (e.g., PDL-related tweets organising around Berlusconi's *Servizio Pubblico* appearance). Even tonal features — irony, vulgarity, requests for interaction — map onto specific broadcast moments. The argument in [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] is therefore that second-screen practice does not escape TV logic so much as extend and inflect it, which is the durable claim this topic carries forward into later work on hybrid media systems.

## Open Threads

Filed alone here, [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] leaves several threads for later notes to pick up: how broadcaster strategies might (or might not) convert spontaneous tweeting into integrated participation; whether the asymmetries observed in Italy generalise to systems with stronger social-TV production cultures; and how the "in/through" distinction holds up as platforms evolve beyond the Twitter-and-talk-show configuration that defined this foundational study.
