# Health Misinformation and COVID-19

## Health Misinformation and COVID-19

### From content to coordination: how problematic information circulates

A first strand in this collection moves the analytical focus from individual false claims toward the *infrastructures* that carry them. [[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]] is foundational here: building on Coordinated Link Sharing Behavior (CLSB) and the CooRnet toolkit, it maps an Italian covid-skeptic coordinated network as an offshoot of pre-existing political clusters tied to the League and Five Star Movement, and shows how such actors adapt tactically — link laundering through reputable outlets, hiding URLs in first comments, exploiting Catholic groups — to evade platform enforcement. The companion piece [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] picks up this same Italian network and zooms in on the "Alternative Influence Network" layer, identifying an Italian Intellectual Dark Web cluster whose posts attract share-heavy, amplification-oriented engagement, and arguing that legacy media remediation of intellectuals' and experts' statements is what gives these narratives their reach.

[[Song2025-yh]] extends the CLSB lens comparatively to the UK and US, demonstrating that coordination is *not* the exclusive province of misinformation: pro-vaccine institutional actors (NHS, CDC) also coordinate, and the political grammar of anti-vaccine talk differs by national context — safety in the UK, freedom in the US. [[Efstratiou2026-ij]] complements this by showing that on Twitter, COVID-19 scientific discourse is shaped by a small, almost wholly contrarian coordinated retweet network that disproportionately amplifies a handful of credentialed dissenting experts, and that low-trust news outlets align with these contrarian superspreaders. Together these papers reframe the "misinformation problem" as one of asymmetric coordination structures embedded in broader media ecologies.

### Causal traces: from online speech to offline outcomes

A second strand asks whether such circulation actually changes beliefs and behaviour. [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] offers the most direct causal claim, fitting a SIRVA epidemic model to county-level US data to attribute roughly 14,000 vaccine refusals, 545 cases, and 8 deaths to antivaccine Twitter exposure — a lower bound, but a mechanistic bridge from platform speech to epidemic outcomes. [[Adam2026-tz]] complicates the picture at the belief level: combining panel survey data with web-tracking in Germany and Switzerland, it finds both contagion effects from alternative media and mitigation effects from mainstream debunking, but also a "backfire" pathway in which populist-leaning users counterargue against mainstream corrections. The two papers converge on the idea that exposure matters, but disagree on the smoothness of the dose–response: aggregate effects coexist with strong predisposition-driven heterogeneity.

[[Lyons2026-ca]] sharpens this heterogeneity. Linking US survey data to web and YouTube tracking, it shows that exposure to low-credibility health content is rare in aggregate but heavily concentrated among older adults and people already consuming dubious political news — and, strikingly, that referrals come from other low-credibility sites rather than search engines or social media, suggesting habitual cross-domain consumption rather than algorithmic accident. Read against [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] and [[Adam2026-tz]], this implies that the population-level harms of misinformation may be driven by relatively small, highly exposed subgroups whose information diets are entangled across health, politics, and platforms.

### Supply, demand, and the news media's continuing centrality

A third strand reframes misinformation as a problem of the broader information ecosystem rather than only of "bad" content. [[Scalco2026-bd]] formalises this by operationalising "information voids" as anomalous gaps between demand (Wikipedia, Google Trends) and supply (Facebook, Twitter, GDELT) around COVID-19 vaccine events in six European countries, finding that voids persist longer than periods of overabundance and that during voids the share of high-credibility content drops while misinformation rises. This dovetails with [[Efstratiou2026-ij]]'s finding that superspreaders on Twitter typically precede news coverage of scientific papers: when journalistic supply lags, platform actors fill the space.

Yet legacy media remain pivotal even where the framing is "alternative." [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] shows that Italian covid-skeptic narratives circulate largely as remediated quotes from mainstream TV and newspapers — a manipulation strategy fact-checkers struggle to flag because the quotations are accurate. [[Ghezzi2023-8bebc91f]] turns the lens onto the news outlets themselves, documenting that British newspapers systematically partisan-sort their scientific sources, with right-leaning outlets citing Great Barrington Declaration signatories and left-leaning outlets citing John Snow Memorandum signatories, and the two scientific groups also segregating in co-authorship and Twitter sharing. Importantly, this paper insists on a distinction the rest of the corpus sometimes blurs: politicised expert selection is not the same as misinformation, and "less precautionary" need not mean "fringe."

### Epistemic authority and political contestation

A final thread treats COVID-19 information disorder as part of a broader contest over who gets to narrate reality. [[Rodarte2026-dk]] and the closely related [[Li2026-wq]] analyse Brazilian parliamentarians' Twitter activity during the Manaus healthcare collapse, identifying three distinct strategies of epistemic authority: opposition deputies engaged in "derivative reporting" that mimicked journalistic format, Bolsonaro's allies severed ties with mainstream media and posed as unmediated state authority, and local Amazonas politicians built brokerage narratives grounded in proximity. The Brazilian case shows political elites operating as media producers rather than mere sources — a structural condition that makes the coordinated and remediation dynamics described in [[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]], [[Unknown2023-9137f448]], and [[Song2025-yh]] more consequential, because the actors gaming platform affordances are often incumbents rather than outsiders.

Taken together, the eleven papers trace an arc: from cataloguing problematic content, to mapping the coordinated infrastructures that move it, to causally linking exposure with belief and behaviour, to embedding all of this within an information ecosystem where legacy media, platform actors, and political elites jointly produce the conditions under which health (mis)information thrives. The recurring methodological move — pairing digital-trace data with behavioural or survey signals — and the recurring substantive finding — that misinformation is concentrated, coordinated, and politically structured rather than diffusely viral — together define the current research frontier on this topic.
