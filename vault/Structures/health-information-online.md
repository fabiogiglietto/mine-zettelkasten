# Health Information & Public Health Communication

## Mapping the terrain

The papers gathered here approach health information not as isolated false claims but as flows shaped by audiences, platforms, coordinated actors, and legacy media. Two broad strands cut across them: studies that estimate *who is exposed* to problematic health content and with what consequences, and studies that map *how* such content is produced, coordinated and amplified across hybrid media ecosystems. COVID-19 sits at the centre of nearly all of them, providing a shared empirical case through which questions of vaccine hesitancy, scientific authority and platform governance become tractable.

## Exposure, predispositions, and the audience side

A first cluster reconstructs exposure pathways using web-tracking and panel data, complicating any simple "misinformation causes belief" story. [[Adam2026-tz]] combines panel surveys with browsing traces in Germany and Switzerland to show that conspiracy beliefs are a "marriage" of media and predisposition: alternative media produce contagion effects, mainstream media produce (modest) mitigation, but populists selectively engage with the former and even *counter-argue* when exposed to debunking. [[Lyons2026-ca]] reaches a complementary conclusion from the US health domain: low-credibility health exposure is rare in the aggregate but heavily concentrated among older adults and those with conspiracist worldviews, and arrives not via search or social media but via referrals from other low-credibility sites — pointing to habitual, cross-domain consumption rather than algorithmic accident. Together, these studies recast the "infodemic" frame: problematic health content travels along grooves carved by prior beliefs and consumption habits.

[[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] pushes the audience-side analysis to its consequential limit, using a SIRVA compartmental model fit to county-level data to estimate that antivaccine tweets caused on the order of 14,000 refusals, 545 cases, and 8 deaths in the US in 2021. Where [[Adam2026-tz]] and [[Lyons2026-ca]] emphasise concentration and conditioning, [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] insists that even at the population margin, online speech leaves a measurable epidemiological footprint.

## Supply, voids, and the rhythm of the ecosystem

A second strand reframes the question from belief to *ecosystem dynamics*. [[Scalco2026-bd]] operationalises the notion of "information voids" as anomalous demand–supply imbalances across Facebook, Twitter, news and search/Wikipedia traces, showing that voids around vaccine events in six European countries last longer than overabundance episodes and coincide with drops in highly credible content and rises in misinformation. This sits productively beside [[Adam2026-tz]]'s finding that even mainstream outlets circulated substantial conspiracy-supporting material during the pandemic: the problem is not only what fringe actors push but what reliable sources fail to supply when public demand spikes.

## Coordinated production and the architecture of amplification

A third, methodologically distinct cluster turns to the supply-side architecture, using network methods to map coordinated actors. [[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]] documents the rise of an Italian covid-skeptic coordinated network as an offshoot of pre-existing political clusters, and shows how Coordinated Link Sharing Behaviour (CLSB) — combined with a distinctive comments-to-shares signature — can surface problematic content faster than routine fact-checking. [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] complements this by zooming into the *content* circulating through these Italian skeptic networks, identifying an "Intellectual Dark Web" influencer cluster whose share-heavy engagement profile signals hyperpartisan amplification, and arguing that legacy media remain indispensable conduits via the *remediation* of expert quotes — a manipulation technique that fact-checkers struggle to flag because the statements were genuinely uttered.

[[Song2025-yh]] generalises the CLSB lens cross-nationally, showing that coordinated link sharing is ideologically agnostic: anti-vaccine CLSB dominated in the UK and emphasised vaccine safety, while pro-vaccine CLSB dominated in the US and emphasised institutional sources, with national political culture shaping the dominant frame (safety vs. freedom). [[Efstratiou2026-ij]] connects coordination to expert authority itself: a contrarian coordinated retweet network disproportionately amplifies a small set of *credentialed* anti-consensus experts, and superspreader activity on Twitter tends to precede news coverage of scientific papers, with high-trust outlets aligned with conformist superspreaders and low-trust outlets with contrarian ones.

## Science, expertise and the politics of citation

Several papers converge on the question of how scientific authority itself becomes a contested resource. [[Ghezzi2023-8bebc91f]] shows that UK newspapers selectively cite Great Barrington vs. John Snow signatories along left/right lines, with the bifurcation visible in co-authorship and Twitter sharing too — yet, crucially, both groups remain mainstream rather than fringe. This nuances [[Efstratiou2026-ij]]'s portrait of credentialed contrarians and [[Unknown2023-9137f448]]'s account of remediated experts: politicisation of science operates partly through *selective sourcing* of legitimate scientists, not only through the elevation of unprincipled celebrities like Montagnier or Kennedy noted in [[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]].

## Cross-cutting threads

Three threads run through the collection. First, **legacy media remain pivotal** — as conduits, remediators, and aligners — even in studies whose primary objects are platforms ([[Unknown2023-9137f448]], [[Efstratiou2026-ij]], [[Ghezzi2023-8bebc91f]], [[Adam2026-tz]]). Second, **coordination is ideologically agnostic and behaviourally legible**: CLSB signatures, share-to-comment ratios, and retweet-network anomalies recur as diagnostic signals ([[Giglietto2022-0e951ac5]], [[Song2025-yh]], [[Unknown2023-9137f448]], [[Efstratiou2026-ij]]). Third, **audience effects are heterogeneous and conditional**: aggregate exposure is small, but concentrated subpopulations — older adults, populists, the vaccine-hesitant — drive most of the downstream harm ([[Lyons2026-ca]], [[Adam2026-tz]], [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]]), while ecosystem-level voids ([[Scalco2026-bd]]) determine when those subpopulations are most exposed. Read together, the papers argue for a multi-level account of public-health communication: predispositions and demand shape what audiences seek, coordinated actors and remediation shape what they find, and legacy outlets still set much of the frame within which both operate.
