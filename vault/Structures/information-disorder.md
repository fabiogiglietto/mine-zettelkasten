# Information Disorder & Problematic Content

## From "Fake News" to Information Disorder: A Conceptual Repositioning

A first thread running across this collection is a sustained dissatisfaction with the very vocabulary that organized early misinformation research. Several authors argue that the label "fake news" has itself become a political weapon rather than an analytic category: [[Yoo2026-ev]] shows how Newsmax and Occupy Democrats deploy it almost exclusively to delegitimize opponents, while [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] argues that creator-centric typologies miss how false content actually circulates in hybrid systems, and proposes a propagation-centered model. [[Frischlich2025-vn]] extends this critique by dissecting the dominant analogies — infodemic, information warfare, information pollution — showing each carries conceptual baggage that distorts policy responses. [[Sadler2025-vu]] pushes further still, arguing that disinformation studies has treated "narrative" intuitively and naively, and calling for a hermeneutic realist approach in which stories *disclose* rather than simply reflect or falsify reality. Together these papers signal a field that has outgrown its founding binaries (true/false, mis/disinformation) and is searching for vocabularies adequate to hybrid, narrative, and infrastructural realities.

## Measurement, Source Lists, and the Limits of "Fake News"-Style Metrics

A parallel methodological reckoning runs through several quantitative contributions. [[Luhring2025-od]] audits the NewsGuard database — the workhorse of source-based misinformation research — and shows that binary trustworthy/untrustworthy cutoffs can substantially distort findings, while [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]] uses Meta's URL Shares Dataset to map European exposure dynamics that differ markedly from US patterns. [[Goel2025-iq]] cuts deeper, arguing that domain-list approaches systematically miss the larger phenomenon of *mainstream* articles repurposed to support misleading narratives. [[Waight2025-al]] develops an LLM-based pipeline for measuring narrative similarity across languages, demonstrating that exact text-reuse methods miss most of the cross-source narrative diffusion they purport to detect. [[Scalco2026-bd]] operationalizes "information voids" quantitatively, while [[Nenno2025-xa]] interrogates whether Western news-values typologies even travel to non-WEIRD contexts. The cumulative message: how we measure misinformation largely determines what we find — and most existing instruments are calibrated to a narrow, US-centric slice of the problem.

## Exposure, Effects, and the "Misperception of Harm" Debate

A third thread challenges alarmist accounts of misinformation's reach and impact. [[Budak2024-ef]] consolidates the empirical pushback: average exposure is low, concentration is high, algorithms matter less than commonly claimed, and societal-harm narratives outrun the evidence. [[Bakshy2015-rn]] anticipated this by showing that individual click choices, not the News Feed algorithm, do most of the work suppressing cross-cutting exposure. [[Lyons2026-ca]] finds low-credibility health exposure rare overall but heavily concentrated among older adults, while [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]] documents how misinformation diffuses distinctively but reaches limited audiences on Facebook. [[Hartmann2025-px]] systematically reviews the echo-chamber literature, arguing that conceptual heterogeneity — not the phenomenon itself — explains why studies disagree. Against this skeptical wave, [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] uses epidemic modeling to attribute thousands of vaccine refusals to antivaccine tweets, and [[Kim2026-wg]] shows that microtargeted suppression ads produced large turnout effects among targeted subgroups that ATEs missed. The debate is no longer about whether misinformation exists but about where, for whom, and through what mechanisms its effects concentrate.

## Predispositions, Narratives, and the Social Construction of Belief

Many papers move beyond exposure-effect models toward identity, motivated reasoning, and narrative co-construction. [[Adam2026-tz]] traces a "marriage" of media and predispositions, with populist attitudes producing both reinforcement through alternative media and backfire when populists meet mainstream debunking. [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]] documents a "hostile misinformation effect" paralleling the classic hostile media effect: citizens systematically perceive their own side as the prime target of misinformation. [[Rossini2026-jn]] links electoral misinformation belief to declining tolerance in Brazil, and [[Suau_Martinez2026-lv]] argues that cumulative exposure to *narratives* across the hybrid ecosystem — not isolated claims — drives belief. [[Marwick2025-ov]] reframes ConspiracyTok creators as performing "populist knowledge production" within a "generous epistemology" that empowers marginalized standpoints even as it circulates disinformation. [[Starbird2025-jj]] and [[Prochaska2025-ef]] develop CSCW-grounded accounts of collective sensemaking, showing that misleading rumors are produced through interactions between often-accurate evidence and politically charged frames, with influencers increasingly leaving frames implicit for audiences to supply. [[Elfes2026-jb]] operationalizes "narrative polarisation" computationally, while [[Lieu2025-nl]] finds that content category (solutions-attacking, scientist-attacking) — not logical fallacy type — drives climate misinformation effects.

## Hybrid Media Systems and Elite-Driven Information Disorder

A robust strand reframes information disorder as an elite phenomenon embedded in hybrid media systems rather than a grassroots pathology. [[Rodarte2026-dk]] and its close companion Li2026-wq show Brazilian parliamentarians waging narrative contests during the Manaus crisis through distinct epistemic strategies — derivative reporting, direct authority claims, brokerage. [[Tai2026-qk]] documents how institutional and ideological positions shape elected officials' misinformation sharing, and [[Renault2025-uh]] shows that Republicans are flagged 2.3× more often than Democrats on X's Community Notes even under crowdsourced evaluation. [[Knupfer2025-vt]] introduces a "logic of connective faction" linking digitally networked party elites to hyper-partisan media, using the CRT case to show how factions are rewarded with engagement and attention. [[Bennett2025-xs]] generalizes this into a "digital surrogate organization" framework for democratic backsliding. [[Copland2025-em]] traces how Sky News Australia transformed into a node of transnational right-wing network propaganda, while [[Donovan2025-ws]] historicizes misinformation-at-scale as a structural feature of engagement-driven business models.

## Platforms, Affordances, and Algorithmic Governance

Platform-specific dynamics receive sustained attention. [[Bruns2026-yv]] mourns Twitter's death and the failure of successors to learn from its governance mistakes. [[Vincent_undated-re]] flags TikTok as the European platform with the highest misinformation prevalence, and [[Bosch2024-hj]] highlights sound as TikTok's distinctive propaganda affordance. [[Mosleh2024-op]] documents divergent partisan/quality engagement patterns across seven platforms, challenging single-platform generalization. [[Rieder2026-pp]] shows YouTube's recommendation engine outpacing its moderation in sustaining the "Tate-space" after deplatforming, and [[Askanius2026-de]] maps how Swedish far-right YouTubers hybridize activism with influencer marketing. [[Nangle2026-yo]] analyses Instagram's affective-discursive ecology of far-right softening, while [[Rothut2026-or]] develops "protest-facilitated mainstreaming" via Telegram. [[Ventura2025-sw]] turns to WhatsApp deactivation in Brazil to test messaging-app effects, and [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] uses Facebook URL data to show platform governance interventions actively dampening partisan content reach — Facebook as active curator, not neutral conduit. [[Arceneaux2026-xk]] extends agenda-building theory to include social bots as machine actors, and [[Di-Domenico2026-zq]] shows how influencer credibility legitimizes misinformation and intensifies toxicity beyond what regular users elicit.

## Generative AI and the Coming Crisis of Authentication

A rapidly emerging frontier concerns generative AI's transformation of the information environment. [[Emilio2026-ik]] articulates the "Generative AI Paradox": as synthetic media becomes ubiquitous, societies may rationally discount all digital evidence, raising the cost of truth itself. [[Waight2026-ts]] shows that state media control already shapes LLM outputs through training data — a form of geopolitical "laundering" of propaganda. [[Triedman2025-uy]] dissects Grokipedia as a politically inflected, low-quality-source-heavy AI fork of Wikipedia. [[DeVerna2025-dl]] finds that even reasoning- and search-enabled LLMs perform poorly at political fact-checking without curated context, and [[Choi2026-bz]] documents modality-congruent carryover effects from authenticating deepfakes. Yet [[Hameleers2026-mc]] complicates the AI panic: AI-generated still images do not universally outperform textual disinformation, and low-tech video decontextualization sometimes proves more persuasive than synthetic media. [[Mahl2026-hc]]'s Delphi study notably found experts in 2022 *not* anticipating generative AI's impact — a humbling note on the field's predictive limits.

## Interventions: Inoculation, Fact-Checking, and the Limits of Individual-Level Fixes

The intervention literature shows both promise and chastening complexity. [[van-der-Linden2026-jt]] demonstrates scalable Instagram prebunking with effects persisting five months. [[Farkas2026-lr]] examines how European fact-checkers defend their platform partnerships through rhetorical apologia, while [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] empirically rebuts Zuckerberg's "censor" framing of fact-checkers by showing removal occurs in only ~30% of cases. [[Xue2025-bp]] highlights emotionality as a fact-checking strategy, and [[Rohrbach2026-rc]] cautions that journalistic strategies themselves can inadvertently seed conspiracy belief. [[Hourigan2026-oc]] shifts attention to *everyday* encounters with perceived misinformation, finding that users blame mainstream and alternative outlets more than fringe sources, with clickbait the central irritant. [[Spampatti2026-kx]] imports six lessons from climate psychology — including the need to engage motivational and systemic drivers, not just cognition — and [[Mahl2026-hc]]'s expert panel ranks platform governance and journalism reform above individual-level interventions like fact-checking and literacy.

## Comparative, Global, and Cross-Platform Horizons

Finally, the collection signals a decisive shift beyond US-centric, Twitter-centric inquiry. [[Humprecht2025-ml]] explicitly frames the agenda of broadening beyond WEIRD contexts. Brazilian electoral and pandemic dynamics dominate several papers ([[Rodarte2026-dk]], [[Rossini2026-jn]], [[Ventura2025-sw]]); European cases proliferate ([[Gattermann2025-yx]], [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]], [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]], [[Askanius2026-de]]); transnational right-wing flows are mapped in [[Copland2025-em]] and [[Bennett2025-xs]]; and the political economy of state-coordinated media is interrogated in [[Waight2026-ts]] and [[Graham2025-gp]]. [[Thiele2025-ol]] offers a theoretical typology for attributing coordinated manipulation across cases, [[FitzGerald2025-nv]] traces how campaigns persist by appropriating emerging events, and the Italian-Covid case study [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] situates Alternative Influence Networks within hybrid remediation cycles. [[Gardam2025-er]] meanwhile pushes methods toward visual-first analysis. The arc of this Zettel is clear: from a field anchored in US elections, individual cognition, and binary truth-judgments toward one increasingly attuned to narrative, infrastructure, political economy, and global variation — yet still wrestling with measurement, definition, and the question of what, if anything, has actually changed.
