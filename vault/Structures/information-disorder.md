# Information Disorder and Disinformation

## Reframing the Object: From Fake News to Narratives, Systems, and Infrastructures

A first arc running through these papers is conceptual: a sustained dissatisfaction with "fake news" as the operative category and a search for richer alternatives. [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] sets an early agenda by shifting attention from creators' intent to propagators' judgments within a hybrid news system, arguing that misleading information is best understood as composite cascades. [[Sadler2025-vu]], [[Suau_Martinez2026-lv]], and [[Elfes2026-jb]] push this further toward narrative as the appropriate unit of analysis — hermeneutic, ethical, and structurally rhetorical — while [[Waight2025-al]] operationalizes "narrative similarity" computationally with LLM-based pairwise comparison. [[Goel2025-iq]] complicates source-based metrics by showing that factually true mainstream content is routinely repurposed in service of misleading narratives, a finding echoed by [[Marwick2025-vx]] on processual "redpilling" and by [[Starbird2025-jj]] and [[Prochaska2025-ef]], whose evidence-frame and deep-story frameworks treat rumoring as collective sensemaking rather than discrete falsehood. [[Frischlich2025-vn]] and [[Mahl2026-hc]] meanwhile call for complexity-aware, multi-level framings to replace single analogies (virus, warfare, pollution) and siloed agendas. [[Yoo2026-ev]] turns the meta-question back on the term itself, showing how "fake news" functions in alternative U.S. media as a weaponized label rather than a descriptor.

## Hybrid Systems, Elites, and the Politics of Epistemic Authority

A second arc maps misinformation onto the hybrid media system and the contests over epistemic authority within it. [[Rodarte2026-dk]] shows Brazilian parliamentarians pursuing rival modes of truth-telling — derivative reporting, direct authority, brokerage — during the Manaus crisis, while [[Rossini2026-jn]] and [[Kansaon2025-id]] document how electoral misinformation and coordinated WhatsApp networks reshape democratic norms and street politics in Brazil. [[Knupfer2025-vt]] and [[Bennett2025-xs]] develop overlapping accounts of how digitally networked factions and "digital surrogate organizations" radicalize formal parties, with the MAGA Republican Party as their central case. [[Copland2025-em]] traces how Sky News Australia plugged into this transnational right-wing network, [[Rothut2026-or]] theorizes "protest-facilitated mainstreaming" through Querdenken on Telegram, and [[Askanius2026-de]] and [[Rieder2026-pp]] document the alternative influence networks and ambient ideology that sustain far-right and manosphere figures on YouTube. [[Marwick2025-ov]] complicates the demographic picture by showing that "ConspiracyTok" is disproportionately young, non-White, and female, while [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] points to remediation of legacy media as a key vector for an Italian Intellectual Dark Web. [[Tai2026-qk]] and [[Renault2025-uh]] add elite-level evidence that misinformation sharing is institutionally and ideologically asymmetric.

## Exposure, Diffusion, and the Empirics of "How Much"

A third arc consists of careful empirical work pushing back on alarmist exposure claims while pinpointing where harms concentrate. [[Budak2024-ef]] crystallizes the revisionist position: average exposure is modest, algorithms less central than supposed, societal effects overstated. [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]], [[Appel2026-qr]], [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]], and [[Mosleh2024-op]] document highly skewed diffusion on Facebook, Instagram, and across seven platforms, with reach concentrated in a small minority of users and a handful of networks. [[Lyons2026-ca]] extends this concentration finding to low-credibility health content, where older adults dominate exposure. [[Hourigan2026-oc]] reframes the question from below, showing that what users themselves flag as misinformation is mostly mainstream-news clickbait, not fringe content. [[Vincent_undated-re]] provides ongoing prevalence monitoring under EU frameworks, and [[Scalco2026-bd]] proposes detecting "information voids" where demand outstrips reliable supply. Against the modest-exposure baseline, [[Kim2026-wg]] and [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] argue that targeted suppression ads and antivaccine tweets nonetheless produced measurable downstream harms — turnout suppression and vaccine refusal — when heterogeneity and mechanism are taken seriously. [[Adam2026-tz]] integrates exposure with predispositions, showing both contagion and mitigation pathways depending on media diet.

## Coordination, Infrastructures, and Production Studies

A fourth arc opens the black box of who produces and routes problematic content. [[Poliakoff2026-fa]] offers a rare production study of the IRA as a labor-market actor, [[Thiele2025-ol]] proposes a typology of coordinated social media manipulation grounded in rational-choice attribution, and [[Gaw2025-ru]] reads Philippine influence operations as political brokerage. [[FitzGerald2025-nv]] tracks the temporal adaptation of such campaigns, while [[Graham2025-gp]] and [[Zhao2025-ny]] show how hashtag campaigns and hijacking parasitize platform infrastructures. [[Arceneaux2026-xk]] extends agenda-building theory to social bots as algorithmic agenda-builders, [[Di-Domenico2026-zq]] reframes influencer-driven misinformation as source-amplified toxicity, and [[Donovan2025-ws]] historicizes "misinformation-at-scale" as constitutive of engagement-driven business models. [[Simeone2025-vo]] shows what targeted deplatforming can do at the network level, complementing [[Rieder2026-pp]]'s skepticism that moderation can outpace recommendation.

## Generative AI and the New Synthetic Terrain

A fifth arc addresses the emerging GenAI landscape. [[Emilio2026-ik]] frames the central risk as "synthetic realities" rather than isolated deepfakes, while [[Dierickx2026-tw]] proposes "emergent facts" as a new epistemological category. [[Waight2026-ts]] shows how state media control infiltrates LLM training data, biasing model outputs by language. [[Triedman2025-uy]] dissects Grokipedia as a case of AI-mediated ideological forking, and [[Choi2026-bz]] traces modality-congruent carryover effects of deepfake exposure. Empirically, however, [[Hameleers2026-mc]] finds AI-generated visuals do not universally outperform textual or decontextualized-video disinformation. On the response side, [[DeVerna2025-dl]] shows that LLM fact-checking requires curated context, [[Szabo2026-rd]] introduces conversational inoculation, and [[Shi2026-ko]] examines AI-disclosure labels on Douyin fact-checking videos.

## Interventions, Fact-Checking, and Governance

A final arc concerns what to do. [[van-der-Linden2026-jt]] demonstrates that prebunking can scale via Instagram ads with persistent effects, while [[Spampatti2026-kx]] imports lessons from climate psychology, urging behavioral outcomes and collective research organization. [[Rohrbach2026-rc]] reminds us that journalism itself can seed conspiracy beliefs. The fact-checking literature is internally reflexive: [[Farkas2026-lr]] and [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] examine how European fact-checkers rhetorically defend platform partnerships and navigate Meta's retreat from third-party verification, [[Xue2025-bp]] probes emotionality in fact-check posts, and [[Luhring2025-od]] audits NewsGuard as the field's de facto measurement infrastructure. [[Ventura2025-sw]] tests the limits of feed-centric thinking via a WhatsApp deactivation experiment. At the macro level, [[Gattermann2025-yx]], [[Humprecht2025-ml]], and [[Nenno2025-xa]] foreground comparative and non-WEIRD contexts, [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]] documents a "hostile misinformation effect" where citizens see their own side as the prime target, and [[Marwick2026-qd]] offers a melancholic reflection on the foreclosed alternatives — community-oriented infrastructures that might have made the problem smaller in the first place.

## Convergences and Open Tensions

Across these arcs, several convergences and unresolved tensions emerge. There is broad agreement that the object of study should shift from discrete false claims toward narratives, networks, and institutional infrastructures, and that exposure is more concentrated and less algorithmically driven than public discourse assumes. Yet sharp disagreements remain about whether to characterize misinformation effects as overstated ([[Budak2024-ef]]) or as systematically underestimated by aggregate designs ([[Kim2026-wg]], [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]]). The field is also pulled between individual-cognitive interventions (prebunking, inoculation, literacy) and structural-institutional critiques that locate the problem in platform political economy, party radicalization, and the labor markets of disinformation-for-hire. The hybrid media system frame, originating with [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] and renewed in [[Suau_Martinez2026-lv]], [[Knupfer2025-vt]], and [[Bennett2025-xs]], remains the most productive shared scaffold for holding these tensions together.
