# Information Disorder and Disinformation Narratives

## From discrete falsehoods to narrative ecologies

A through-line of recent work is the rejection of "fake news" as a discrete-claim phenomenon in favour of conceptualising disinformation as cultural narrative that circulates across platforms, genres, and modalities. [[Marwick2026-qd]] makes the strongest theoretical case, arguing that successful disinformation operates as identity-affirming, cathartic stories signalled by visual and discursive catchphrases. [[Sadler2025-vu]] complements this from a hermeneutic-narratological angle, showing that content can be ethically and referentially problematic without containing outright falsehoods, while [[Prochaska2025-ef]] and [[Starbird2025-jj]] empirically demonstrate how "deep stories" and evidence–frame interactions sustain election rumours that single-tweet analyses miss. [[Suau_Martinez2026-lv]] adds survey-based evidence that repeated narrative exposure across hybrid ecosystems shapes belief, and [[Waight2025-al]] provides the methodological scaffolding — LLM-based narrative-similarity measurement — needed to track such cross-source diffusion at scale, complementing [[Goel2025-iq]]'s demonstration that mainstream articles are routinely repurposed into misleading narratives invisible to domain blocklists. Older work by [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] anticipates this shift, urging analytical attention away from creator intent toward propagation dynamics in a hybrid news system.

## Information disorder as complex sociotechnical system

A second line moves up the scale of analysis. [[Frischlich2025-vn]] critiques the dominant infodemic/warfare/pollution analogies as obscuring multilevel dynamics, calling for portfolio interventions; [[Mahl2026-hc]]'s Delphi study and [[Spampatti2026-kx]]'s climate-psychology import converge on the same conclusion that no single lever — fact-checking, literacy, regulation — will suffice. [[Donovan2025-ws]] historicises this systemic view, treating "misinformation-at-scale" as a feature of engagement-driven business models, while [[Emilio2026-ik]] extends the diagnosis into the generative-AI era with the concept of "synthetic reality" and an emerging epistemic-security agenda. [[Budak2024-ef]] inserts a counterweight: public discourse may systematically overstate algorithmic and aggregate harms.

## Cross-platform ecosystems and propagation infrastructures

Platform-comparative and ecosystem-tracing studies document how narratives move. [[Mosleh2024-op]] shows engagement with low-quality news diverges sharply across seven platforms, while [[Vincent_undated-re]] flags TikTok's rising mis/disinformation share in Europe. Platform-specific work spans YouTube ([[Rieder2026-pp]], [[Askanius2026-de]]), Instagram ([[Nangle2026-yo]], [[Gardam2025-er]], [[van-der-Linden2026-jt]]), TikTok ([[Hollingshead2026-vx]], [[Bosch2024-hj]], [[Marwick2025-ov]]), Telegram ([[Bailard2024-pj]], [[Rothut2026-or]], [[Kuznetsova2025-nu]]), Facebook ([[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]], [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]], [[Song2025-yh]], [[Unknown2023-9137f448]]), and WhatsApp ([[Ventura2025-sw]]). [[Copland2025-em]] traces how Sky News Australia became a transnational node bridging Australian and US right-wing ecosystems, and [[Efstratiou2026-ij]] maps Twitter-to-news pathways in COVID science discourse. [[Graham2025-gp]] reframes propaganda as parasitic on the infrastructures of truth themselves.

## Who spreads what, and where coordination matters

Several papers ask who the propagators are. [[Tornberg2025-ir]] finds neither populism nor right-wing ideology alone predicts party misinformation sharing — only their conjunction in radical-right populism — while [[Tai2026-qk]] documents institutional and ideological checks on elected officials' sharing. [[Renault2025-uh]] shows Republicans are flagged 2.3× more often than Democrats even under crowdsourced Community Notes. Studies of coordinated behaviour ([[Thiele2025-ol]], [[Kuznetsova2025-nu]], [[Song2025-yh]], [[Zhao2025-ny]], [[Arceneaux2026-xk]]) probe the strategic logic and effects of such operations, with [[Thiele2025-ol]] developing a rational-choice typology and [[Poliakoff2026-fa]] reconstructing the Internet Research Agency as a media-labour organisation. [[Kim2026-wg]] provides rare causal evidence that IRA-style microtargeted suppression ads depressed turnout among targeted Black voters, and [[FitzGerald2025-nv]] traces how influence campaigns persist by appropriating new events. [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] links antivaccine tweets to county-level vaccination shortfalls and downstream cases and deaths.

## Mainstreaming, far-right ecologies, and connective action

A cluster of work theorises how fringe ideas move toward the centre. [[Bennett2025-xs]] argues that "digital surrogate organisations" enabled by connective action have pulled conservative parties toward illiberalism, a framework that resonates with [[Rothut2026-or]]'s "protest-facilitated mainstreaming," [[Askanius2026-de]]'s Swedish alternative influence network, [[Rieder2026-pp]]'s post-deplatforming Tate-space, and [[Nangle2026-yo]]'s affective-discursive Instagram ecology. [[Bailard2024-pj]] establishes a temporal cycle in which online framing predicts offline Proud Boys violence; [[Marwick2025-vx]] reframes "redpilling" as processual socialisation rather than sudden conversion; and [[Marwick2025-ov]]'s ConspiracyTok shows how vernacular knowledge production diversifies who participates in conspiracy culture. [[Grusauskaite2026-po]] adds that symbolic boundary work — not platform affordances alone — binds heterogeneous Great Reset publics into a "we."

## Health, science, and conspiracy ecosystems

Health and science misinformation forms its own dense cluster. [[Adam2026-tz]] combines panel and tracking data to show contagion via alternative media, mitigation via mainstream debunking, and indirect reinforcement and backfire pathways shaped by populism and mistrust. [[Lyons2026-ca]] demonstrates that exposure to low-credibility health content is rare overall but concentrated among older adults and cross-domain news omnivores. [[Efstratiou2026-ij]] details how a small coordinated contrarian network amplifies credentialed dissenters, while [[Ghezzi2023-8bebc91f]] documents partisan polarisation in UK newspaper sourcing of COVID scientists. [[Song2025-yh]] and [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] show that coordination is ideologically agnostic — pro-vaccine and credible-source amplification employ similar tactics — and [[Lieu2025-nl]] finds content category (especially attacks on climate solutions) outweighs fallacy type in persuasiveness, while [[Gardam2025-er]] develops visual-first methods for climate denial on Instagram.

## Generative AI: vector and mitigator

LLMs occupy an ambivalent position. [[Emilio2026-ik]] and [[Choi2026-bz]] flag synthetic-reality and modality-congruent susceptibility risks, while [[Hameleers2026-mc]] tempers alarm by showing that AI-generated visuals do not universally outperform text or decontextualised real video. [[Waight2026-ts]] reveals a subtler institutional risk: state-controlled media is memorised by commercial LLMs, producing language-conditioned pro-regime outputs. [[Triedman2025-uy]] examines Grokipedia's systematic citation of low-quality sources, and [[Waight2025-al]] illustrates the constructive side by using LLMs to detect Russian narrative diffusion. On the mitigation side, [[Costello2024-bg]] shows GPT-4 dialogues durably reduce conspiracy belief, [[Szabo2026-rd]] extends this through Conversational Inoculation, and [[DeVerna2025-dl]] demonstrates that LLM fact-checking improves dramatically only with curated retrieval — not reasoning or generic web search.

## Interventions and their limits

The intervention literature spans psychological, journalistic, and platform-governance levers. [[van-der-Linden2026-jt]] provides rare field evidence that prebunking can be scaled in Instagram feeds with five-month persistence, complemented by the conversational approach of [[Szabo2026-rd]]. [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] and [[Farkas2026-lr]] examine fact-checking under platform retreat, finding most verified content is neither removed nor labelled and that fact-checkers rhetorically defend partnerships through differentiation and transcendence. [[Simeone2025-vo]] documents the network-wide ripple effects of deplatforming, while [[Rieder2026-pp]] shows recommendation systems can outpace moderation. [[Rohrbach2026-rc]] cautions that journalistic strategies themselves may help form conspiracy beliefs, and [[Xue2025-bp]] interrogates the emotionality of fact-checks. [[Donovan2025-ws]] situates these debates historically as platforms migrate everyday governance of speech into private hands.

## Audiences, perceptions, and democratic effects

A final strand examines how citizens encounter, perceive, and respond. [[Hourigan2026-oc]] finds Australians attribute misinformation overwhelmingly to mainstream outlets and clickbait, far from "fringe" stereotypes. [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]] documents a "hostile misinformation effect" mirroring the classic hostile media effect, and [[Gattermann2025-yx]] links far-right electoral performance to citizen concern. [[Rossini2026-jn]] shows electoral misinformation belief predicts political intolerance in Brazil, while [[Yoo2026-ev]] traces how "fake news" itself is weaponised as a delegitimising rhetorical label by hyperpartisan outlets. [[Di-Domenico2026-zq]] foregrounds influencer credibility and parasocial bonds as engines of online toxicity, and [[Bruns2026-yv]] mourns the death of Twitter as a forum for public debate.

## Decentring WEIRD assumptions and methodological reflexivity

Finally, the field is reflexively interrogating its own scope and tools. [[Humprecht2025-ml]] frames a special issue on de-WEIRDing misinformation research; [[Nenno2025-xa]] empirically demonstrates that news-value patterns in flagged content vary across 24 countries, with the US and Brazil as outliers; and studies in Brazil ([[Rossini2026-jn]], [[Ventura2025-sw]]), Russia/Belarus ([[Kuznetsova2025-nu]]), Sweden ([[Askanius2026-de]]), Australia ([[Hourigan2026-oc]], [[Copland2025-em]]), and across Europe ([[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]], [[Farkas2026-lr]], [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]]) extend the geographic frame. Methodologically, [[Luhring2025-od]] audits NewsGuard's stability and warns against binary trustworthy/untrustworthy classifications; [[Scalco2026-bd]] operationalises "information voids" as detectable demand–supply imbalances; and [[Goel2025-iq]] and [[Waight2025-al]] push beyond domain-level measurement toward narrative-level analysis. Read together, these papers chart a field moving from individual cognition and discrete falsehoods toward narrative ecologies, institutional infrastructures, and globally comparative, complexity-aware inquiry.
