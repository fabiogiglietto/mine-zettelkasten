# Information Disorder and Disinformation

## From False Claims to Narrative Ecologies

A central conceptual shift cuts across this collection: the move from treating misinformation as discrete false items to analyzing it as narratives, frames, and cascades within hybrid media systems. [[Giglietto2019-e9be81c1]] sets the terms by displacing the creator-centric definition of "fake news" in favor of a propagation-centered, observer-dependent model of hybrid circulation. [[Suau_Martinez2026-lv]] empirically operationalizes this turn, showing that recurrent narrative exposure across outlets — not single claims — drives belief. [[Sadler2025-vu]] pushes the argument philosophically, urging a hermeneutic-narratological account in which ethically problematic stories need not be literally false. [[Starbird2025-jj]] and [[Prochaska2025-ef]] develop this empirically through evidence-frame analysis and "deep stories" of election integrity, demonstrating that misleading rumors often weld accurate evidence to distorted frames and rely on audience-supplied interpretive context. [[Goel2025-iq]] extends the same logic to mainstream news: co-sharing analysis reveals that factually accurate articles from reputable outlets are routinely repurposed to advance misleading narratives, complicating purely source-based measurement. [[Waight2025-al]] then provides the methodological infrastructure for narrative-similarity measurement at scale via LLM pipelines.

## Measurement, Sources, and Operationalization

A parallel methodological debate concerns how to measure misinformation prevalence and exposure. [[Luhring2025-od]] audits NewsGuard, finding that continuous trustworthiness scores are reasonably stable but binary cutoffs distort downstream results — a caution applicable to many studies here. [[Rossi2023-847d5a9f]] applies such ratings comparatively across France, Germany, and Italy, while [[Vincent_undated-re]] provides repeated platform-level measurements in Europe, both showing that exposure is more concentrated and platform-specific than alarmist accounts suggest. [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]] and [[Budak2024-ef]] converge on a similar deflationary message: misinformation exposure is concentrated among a small subset of users and algorithms are not the dominant pathway. [[Lyons2026-ca]] reinforces this with health content, where the top 10% of users account for 77% of exposure, predominantly older adults reached via cross-domain referrals rather than search or social platforms. [[Mosleh2024-op]] adds cross-platform variation as a corrective to Twitter/Facebook-centrism, while [[Scalco2026-bd]] develops a complementary supply-demand framework for detecting "information voids."

## Hybrid Ecosystems, Mainstream Media, and Alternative Outlets
 
Several studies destabilize the boundary between fringe and mainstream. [[Adam2026-tz]] finds that even quality mainstream outlets carried substantial conspiracy-supporting COVID content, though predispositions and selective engagement still channel its effects. [[Hourigan2026-oc]] shows everyday Australians attribute most perceived misinformation to mainstream and alternative outlets, not fringe sources, with clickbait the central grievance. [[Copland2025-em]] traces how Sky News Australia, a News Corp cable channel, transformed into a node of transnational network propaganda, while [[Yoo2026-ev]] documents how U.S. hyperpartisan outlets weaponize the very label "fake news." [[Rothut2026-or]] adds protest-facilitated mainstreaming via Telegram, showing how movement infrastructures funnel attention from heterogeneous publics toward radical milieus. Together these papers complicate clean distinctions between mainstream, alternative, and extremist information environments.

## Platforms, Affordances, and Modality

Platform-specific dynamics receive sustained attention. [[Bosch2024-hj]] foregrounds TikTok's sonic affordances as a distinctive vector of war disinformation, and [[Shi2026-ko]] examines AI disclosure on Douyin. [[Ventura2025-sw]] uses WhatsApp deactivation in Brazil to probe encrypted-messaging effects, complementing [[Rossini2026-jn]]'s finding that messaging-app news use indirectly fuels intolerance via misinformation belief. [[Bruns2026-yv]] eulogizes Twitter and assesses its successors, while [[Donovan2025-ws]] historicizes "misinformation-at-scale" as a structural feature of engagement-driven business models. On modality, [[Hameleers2026-mc]] finds visual and AI-generated disinformation are not uniformly more persuasive than text — context matters more than format — and [[Choi2026-bz]] traces modality-congruent carryover effects from deepfake exposure.

## AI, LLMs, and Synthetic Realities

A clutch of papers tracks generative AI's emerging role. [[Emilio2026-ik]] formalizes the threat of layered "synthetic realities" and the paradox that ubiquitous fakery may produce blanket cynicism. [[Waight2026-ts]] shows that state media control already shapes LLM outputs via training data, with pro-regime bias in lower-media-freedom languages. [[Triedman2025-uy]] dissects Grokipedia's heavy reliance on Wikipedia derivatives but with degraded sourcing on controversial topics. [[Szabo2026-rd]] explores LLM chatbots as conversational inoculation agents, suggesting AI is both threat and resource.

## Producers, Operations, and Coordinated Manipulation

Several pieces foreground the supply side. [[Poliakoff2026-fa]] reconstructs the Internet Research Agency as a labor-market actor through OSINT analysis of CVs, while [[Thiele2025-ol]] offers a rational-choice typology for attributing coordinated social media manipulation. [[Kim2026-wg]] documents targeted digital voter suppression in 2016, [[Graham2025-gp]] dissects the #IStandWithPutin campaign as parasitic on truth infrastructure, and [[FitzGerald2025-nv]] tracks how manipulation campaigns persist by appropriating emerging events. [[Arceneaux2026-xk]] extends agenda-building theory to social bots, [[Bollenbacher2026-vz]] links antivaccine tweets causally to vaccination outcomes, and [[Di-Domenico2026-zq]] highlights influencers as legitimating sources for brand-related misinformation and toxicity. [[Tai2026-qk]] examines elected officials' own misinformation sharing.

## Audiences, Beliefs, and Perceptions

A psychological strand examines how citizens process misinformation. [[Marwick2025-ov]] reframes ConspiracyTok as populist knowledge production under a "generous epistemology," while [[Rohrbach2026-rc]] asks how journalistic strategies inadvertently cultivate conspiracy beliefs. [[Rossini2026-jn]] links electoral misinformation belief to political intolerance in Brazil, and [[Van_Erkel2026-mk]] identifies a "hostile misinformation effect" whereby partisans perceive their own side as disproportionately targeted. [[Gattermann2025-yx]] traces how far-right electoral performance shapes citizen disinformation concerns across Europe, and [[Nenno2025-xa]] examines whether news values in perceived misinformation generalize beyond WEIRD contexts. [[Bennett2025-xs]] connects these belief dynamics to institutional erosion through "digital surrogate organizations."

## Interventions: From Fact-Checking to Inoculation

The collection assesses a portfolio of remedies. [[Farkas2026-lr]] examines how European fact-checkers rhetorically defend platform partnerships, and [[Cazzamatta2026-lo]] documents what actually happens to debunked content post-verification — mostly nothing, undermining censorship narratives. [[Renault2025-uh]] shows that even crowdsourced Community Notes flag Republicans 2.3x more often than Democrats, challenging claims that professional fact-checkers are uniquely biased. [[Xue2025-bp]] interrogates the emotionality of fact-checking itself. On the proactive side, [[van-der-Linden2026-jt]] demonstrates scalable Instagram prebunking, [[Szabo2026-rd]] tests LLM-driven conversational inoculation, and [[Mahl2026-hc]]'s Delphi study ranks platform governance and journalism reform above individual-level fixes.

## Framing the Field: Critique and Consolidation

Finally, several pieces step back to assess the field itself. [[Frischlich2025-vn]] critiques the conceptual baggage of virus, warfare, and pollution analogies, advocating multilevel complexity-aware framing. [[Spampatti2026-kx]] imports six lessons from climate psychology — emphasizing real-world behavior, generalizability, and organized researcher coalitions. [[Humprecht2025-ml]] frames the editorial agenda for de-WEIRDing misinformation research, and [[Mahl2026-hc]] provides expert-driven synthesis pointing toward integrative, anticipatory governance. Together these meta-level interventions argue that the next phase of disinformation scholarship must be comparative, narrative-aware, institutionally embedded, and modest about both the harms and the fixes.
