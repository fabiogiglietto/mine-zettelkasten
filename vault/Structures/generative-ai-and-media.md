# Generative AI and Media Manipulation

## The contested epistemic landscape of generative AI

The papers gathered here trace a research front that has moved decisively beyond "deepfakes" as discrete artifacts toward the broader epistemic conditions that generative AI creates. [[Emilio2026-ik]] supplies the unifying frame: the central risk is not the isolated synthetic clip but coherent *synthetic realities* layered across content, identity, interaction, and institutions, producing a paradox in which rational discounting of all digital evidence becomes its own harm. [[Dierickx2026-tw]] approaches the same terrain philosophically, arguing that GenAI outputs constitute a new category of "emergent facts" that elude the evidence-based, interpretative, and rule-based criteria traditional fact-checking relies on. Together they reframe the field's object of study from artefact authenticity to *epistemic security*.

## Persuasion: from speculation to mechanism

A cluster of large experimental studies has begun to pin down what actually makes conversational AI persuasive — and how dangerous it is. [[Hackenburg2025-dj]] identifies post-training and prompting (not scale or microtargeting) as the dominant levers, with information density as the key mechanism, but documents a stark persuasion–accuracy trade-off. [[Lin2025-xp]] extends this from issues to real elections across four contexts, finding effects that exceed traditional ads, persist a month later, and rely on facts more than psychological tactics — while also revealing that LLMs advocating right-leaning positions consistently produce more inaccurate claims. [[DiGiuseppe2026-pu]] introduces an important boundary condition: perceived political neutrality is itself a lever, and brief out-party bias warnings cut persuasion by ~25%. These papers collectively domesticate alarmist Cambridge-Analytica-style narratives while raising more specific concerns about the *information environment* LLMs construct.

## Coordinated manipulation and the swarm turn

Where individual persuasion ends, coordination begins. [[Schroeder2026-im]] articulates the threat model of "malicious AI swarms" — multi-agent LLM systems capable of persistent, adaptive, cross-platform influence — and [[Orlando2025-ul]] provides the first systematic empirical demonstration that such coordination emerges even with minimal scaffolding: simply revealing teammate identities produces coordination nearly equivalent to explicit collective deliberation. [[Giglietto2026-9b6a992d]] grounds this in a live case, showing an exponential post-ChatGPT explosion in coordinated Facebook gambling promotion that exploits Meta's regulatory gap between paid ads and "organic" content through AI-generated visual persuasion. [[Schiffrin_undated-gi]] situates these dynamics within the broader scam ecosystem and argues for shifting liability toward platform and financial gatekeepers rather than victims. The arc here pushes the discussion from content to *infrastructure and incentives*.

## State power, training data, and the politics of model outputs

A distinctive contribution of this corpus is to surface political-economic pathways of influence operating *upstream* of users. [[Waight2026-ts]] shows that state-coordinated Chinese media is memorised by commercial LLMs and produces measurably more pro-regime outputs in lower-media-freedom languages — a form of "laundering" of state narratives through ostensibly neutral systems. [[Triedman2025-uy]] documents the converse phenomenon in a Western context: Grokipedia as an AI-generated Wikipedia fork that systematically over-cites unreliable and blacklisted sources on controversial topics, even pioneering "LLM auto-citogenesis" via @grok citations. These papers complicate purely user-facing accounts of AI persuasion by showing how training data, model design, and platform substitution embed political projects into the substrate of computational knowledge.

## Audience effects and the limits of medium novelty

Counterweights to the swarm/synthetic-reality alarm come from work on audience reception. [[Hameleers2026-mc]] finds that visual disinformation — including AI-generated images — does not universally outperform text; only decontextualised real video proved more credible on the salient Ukraine issue, suggesting that low-tech recontextualization may matter more than generative novelty. [[Choi2026-bz]] adds a metacognitive dimension via "modality-congruent carryover effects" on confidence and susceptibility. Combined with the persuasion experiments above, these findings caution against treating GenAI as automatically transformative of message effects, even as the *scale* and *coordination* affordances clearly shift.

## Verification, fact-checking, and tools for journalists

If GenAI both produces and threatens factuality, can it also assist verification? [[DeVerna2025-dl]] offers a cautiously affirmative answer: zero-shot and reasoning-enabled LLMs fact-check poorly, but curated RAG over fact-checking summaries raises macro F1 by an average of 233%, suggesting the bottleneck is *access to curated evidence* rather than raw capability. Citation analysis reveals ideological skew toward credible but left-leaning sources — a finding that intersects directly with the asymmetric inaccuracy patterns in [[Lin2025-xp]] and the source-quality gradient in [[Triedman2025-uy]]. [[Dierickx2026-tw]]'s four indicators (accuracy, verifiability, contextual relevance, consistency) supply an epistemological scaffold for the practical verification workflows these tools enable.

## Journalism's framings and AI hype as infrastructure

A reflexive thread examines how journalism itself shapes the discursive conditions for these developments. [[Dodds2026-df]] theorises AI hype as systemic infrastructure rather than mere narrative bubble, embedded in sourcing patterns, professional anxiety, and managerial pressures. [[Nguyen2026-vm]] empirically maps the global news framing of LLMs, finding that mentalistic-agentic framings are present but not dominant and serve multiple rhetorical functions — including critique. [[Wang2025-zy]] adds a cross-national comparative angle on utopian/dystopian imaginaries, while [[Stanusch2026-ec]] uses the Altman firing as a controversy-mapping case to surface how industry actors "premediate" and "preclude" critique to monopolise futures. [[Weinbrand2026-sf]] extends this to platform-led reframing, showing how Google's AI Overviews are discursively positioned by Google, journalists, and SEO marketers — with users and democratic concerns notably absent.

## A paradigmatic horizon

Finally, [[Tornberg2026-lc]] and [[Tornberg2025-ir]] argue that these threads together signal a paradigm shift: the "social media" framework is dissolving under the combined pressure of algorithmic broadcasting, generative content, and retreat into semi-private spheres. AI-mediated communication becomes a media form without publics — communication without shared texts — eroding the epistemic infrastructure on which democratic discourse has rested. Read alongside [[Emilio2026-ik]]'s synthetic-reality stack and [[Schroeder2026-im]]'s swarm threat model, this points toward a coherent emerging research agenda: not "is generative AI dangerous?" but how to reconstitute provenance, verification, and shared reference in an information environment whose default substrate is increasingly synthetic.
