# News Ecosystems, Partisanship and Polarisation

## From Sources to Stories: The Unit-of-Analysis Problem

A central thread running through this corpus is dissatisfaction with the source-level (domain-list) paradigm that has long underwritten studies of partisan news. [[Green2025-ap]] argues that conventional domain measures mistake heterogeneity for moderation: stories from a single outlet often reach radically different partisan audiences once "unbundled" through networked curation, with moderate-scoring domains masking the greatest within-source variance. [[Goel2025-iq]] sharpens the critique from the misinformation side, showing that mainstream articles co-shared with fake news disproportionately contain misleading narratives — meaning factually accurate content from reputable outlets is routinely repurposed to support false claims, a phenomenon invisible to blocklist methodologies. [[Gaisbauer2025-by]] generalises the methodological move, calling for a "political cartography" that maps circulation across story, outlet and content levels simultaneously. [[Lai2024-to]] complements this by extending latent-ideology estimation from actors to YouTube videos themselves, treating individual content items as ideologically scalable. The collective implication: source-level partisanship scores systematically misrepresent what users actually see and share.

## Algorithmic Curation as Active Gatekeeping

A second cluster reopens the "black box" question with new empirical leverage. [[Bastos2025-ya]], [[McNally2025-dn]] and [[Bastos2025-ol]] (closely related analyses of Guardian engagement on Facebook 2011–2020) argue that Facebook's News Feed responds to identifiable strategic interventions with ~19–24 day lags, and treats hard news distinctively from soft content. [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] extends this longitudinally to 130k highly-circulated URLs, showing that the shares-to-views amplification rate, the penalty on partisan content, and the reward for journalistic quality all fluctuate sharply around known governance events such as the 2020 "break the glass" measures — direct evidence of active curation rather than neutral conduit. On X, [[Gauthier2026-iq]] finds that algorithmic feeds causally shift attitudes in a conservative direction and, crucially, do so partly through *persistent* follow choices, while [[Efstratiou2025-gs]] shows the post-Musk algorithm rewarded agitation and proximity to Musk himself rather than right-leaning content per se. [[Brady2026-ln]] closes the loop experimentally on Bluesky, demonstrating that engagement-based ranking amplifies intergroup-moralised-emotional content and distorts perceived norms — and that a "diversified extremity" algorithm can mitigate this without harming enjoyment. Together these papers reposition platform algorithms from inscrutable infrastructure to auditable, contestable governance regimes.

## Hyperpartisan and Alternative Influence Ecosystems

Several papers map the supply-side architecture of partisan information environments. [[Knupfer2025-vt]] develops a "logic of connective faction" showing that CRT-adopting Republicans are simultaneously more ideologically extreme, more deeply tied to right-wing digital networks, and more rewarded with engagement — a feedback loop in which hyperpartisan media function as quasi-organisational digital surrogates for intra-party factions. [[Copland2025-em]] documents how Sky News Australia became a transnational node in this ecosystem via a dual Facebook/YouTube strategy, while [[Askanius2026-de]] traces a Swedish far-right "alternative influence network" of YouTubers, alternative outlets and lifestyle influencers that hybridised activist tactics with influencer monetisation during COVID-19. [[Unknown2023-9137f448]] identifies an analogous Italian "Intellectual Dark Web" cluster whose share-skewed engagement on Facebook depends crucially on remediation of legacy-media interviews. [[Rieder2026-pp]] shows that even deplatformed figures like Andrew Tate persist through a decentralised "ambient ideology" of remixes and Shorts that outpaces moderation. [[Rothut2026-or]] adds a movement-level mechanism: anti-COVID protest infrastructures on Telegram operated as meso-level conduits routing broader publics toward established far-right and conspiracist communities, with shared anti-elitism — not ideology — as the bridge.

## Partisan Attention, Insularity and Affective Engagement

A further line of work measures how partisan publics actually engage. [[Giglietto2019-882f1900]] introduces the insularity/comments-to-shares logic, finding that more insular sources generate proportionally more amplification while cross-partisan sources generate contestation — with Italian populists (especially M5S) uniquely able to mobilise both modes strategically. [[Kalsnes2025-zb]] extends the affective story to Scandinavia: "Angry" reactions predict sharing while "Love" reactions suppress it, and populist parties dominate the angry-reaction economy. [[Anwar2024-34dba628]]'s review consolidates the broader pattern that political and far-right content disproportionately mobilises negative reactions, which in turn drive engagement. [[Mosleh2024-op]] importantly cautions against generalising from any single platform, documenting divergent partisan and low-quality news engagement patterns across seven platforms. [[Kristensen2025-ni]] raises the parallel question of whether alternative platforms themselves drive discursive polarisation.

## Polarisation: Echo Chambers, Narratives and Sedimentation

The papers on polarisation itself are notably ambivalent about the strong echo-chamber thesis. [[Bakshy2015-rn]] originally showed that on Facebook, individual choice suppresses cross-cutting exposure more than the algorithm does — though [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]] now reframes that finding as historically contingent on governance regimes. [[Hartmann2025-px]]'s systematic review argues that much disagreement about whether echo chambers "exist" is itself an artefact of conceptual and operational heterogeneity. Several papers nonetheless document robust fragmentation through alternative lenses: [[Dehghan2026-sy]] shows minimal cross-ideological overlap across 11 political subreddits over 16 years, arguing for a "sedimentation of polarisation" in which platform design, moderation and discourse become entangled preconditions rather than outcomes. [[Elfes2026-jb]] introduces "narrative polarisation," showing that YouTube videos on Israel–Palestine encode strongly divergent actantial roles even where surface comment discourse converges. [[Yoo2026-ev]] documents how "fake news" itself has been weaponised symmetrically by hyperpartisan outlets to redraw Hallin's spheres of legitimate debate. [[Ghezzi2023-8bebc91f]] extends polarisation into science communication, showing UK newspapers segregate citations of COVID-19 scientists along partisan lines.

## Synthesis: An Emerging Consensus

Read together, these papers point toward a converging picture. Partisanship and polarisation on social platforms are produced not by any single mechanism — not algorithms alone, not selective exposure alone, not hyperpartisan supply alone — but by *entanglements* of platform governance, source-level production, story-level curation, factional elite incentives, and affective engagement architectures. The methodological corollary, voiced from many directions ([[Green2025-ap]], [[Goel2025-iq]], [[Gaisbauer2025-by]], [[Hartmann2025-px]], [[Giglietto2026-632ef967]]), is that progress requires moving below the domain and above the platform: tracking how specific stories travel through specific governance regimes to specific partisan publics, and treating both algorithms and conceptual frameworks as historically situated objects of audit.
