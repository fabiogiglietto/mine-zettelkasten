# Social TV, Second Screen and Networked Audiences

## Two Faces of Networked Audiencehood

The papers gathered here approach "networked audiences" from opposite ends of the public/private continuum, yet converge on a shared insight: participation on social platforms is rarely the autonomous, self-directed practice it is sometimes imagined to be. It is structured — by the cooperation of friendship networks in one case, by the logic of broadcast television in the other — and its meaning emerges only in relation to those structuring forces.

## Participation as Collaborative Practice

[[Farci2017-2a88c00e]] reframes Facebook intimacy as a *collaborative* accomplishment rather than a private act of self-disclosure. The five practices it identifies — showing rather than telling, sharing implicit content, tagging, expectation of mutual understanding, and liking — all depend on a competent audience of friends willing to read between the lines, recognise themselves as addressees, and signal back. Intimacy here is not broadcast but co-produced in a semi-public symbolic space.

[[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] tells a structurally parallel but politically darker story. Second-screen tweeting during Italian talk shows is also a participatory practice that depends on the cooperation of others — politicians, broadcasters, fellow viewers — to acquire meaning. But here the cooperation largely fails to materialise: only 3 of 11 shows surfaced tweets on air, parties barely engaged, and Grillo's M5S was almost absent. Where Farci et al.'s friends sustain intimacy through low-cost mutual recognition (the "like"), Iannelli and Giglietto's viewers shout into a room whose other occupants are not listening.

## The Persistence of Older Logics

Both papers resist accounts that treat social media as autonomous from prior social or media forms. [[Farci2017-2a88c00e]] explicitly pushes back against narcissistic readings (Turkle, Bauman, Lasch), arguing that Facebook practices revive community-like dimensions of friendship rather than dissolving them. [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] makes the analogous move at the system level, drawing on hybrid-media-systems thinking (Chadwick) to insist that Twitter political talk follows the broadcast agenda — peaks track media events, content reacts to TV, and substantive agenda-building contributions are residual (2.4%). In both cases, the "networked" layer reorganises but does not displace its predecessor: friendship in one, television in the other.

## Who Gets to Participate

A quieter thread running across both studies concerns the *narrowness* of participation. Iannelli and Giglietto are explicit: 187,031 unique tweeters out of ~4 million Italian Twitter users is a thin slice, and engagement skews toward opinion (79.8%) and requests for interaction (28.6%) rather than agenda-shaping intervention. [[Farci2017-2a88c00e]] does not foreground exclusion in the same way, but its emphasis on the "expectation of mutual understanding" implies a tacit relational competence not equally distributed — networked intimacy presupposes an audience already attuned to one's codes. Both papers thus complicate celebratory readings of participatory culture by showing how participation is gated, whether by relational literacy or by structural inattention from elites.

## Open Threads

Read together, the papers invite further work on what might be called the *responsiveness* of networked publics. Farci et al. show what intimacy looks like when the network cooperates; Iannelli and Giglietto show what political participation looks like when it does not. A productive next move for this branch of the Zettelkasten would be to ask under what conditions networked audiences sustain genuine reciprocity — and when, instead, the affordances of liking, tagging, and hashtagging produce only the appearance of being heard.
