# Sociotechnical AI Evaluation, Red-Teaming, and Public Participation

## From Technical Fix to Sociotechnical Reckoning

The papers gathered here share a common move: they refuse to treat AI safety, evaluation, and accountability as engineering problems with engineering solutions. Instead, they reframe each as a sociotechnical practice — entangled with labor, lived experience, institutional incentives, and political economy. Read together, they trace an arc from reformist proposals that thicken evaluation with human judgment ([[Matias2025-px]], [[Unknown2025-qj]]), to a critical diagnosis of the labor regimes such judgment depends on ([[Gillespie2026-aa]]), to a structural critique that questions whether any of these reforms can escape co-optation by the industry they aim to discipline ([[Vertesi2026-lv]]).

## Public Participation as Epistemic Infrastructure

[[Matias2025-px]] and [[Unknown2025-qj]] converge on the claim that publics are not merely stakeholders to be consulted but epistemic contributors whose situated knowledge improves the rigor of AI evaluation. Matias and colleagues make the strong methodological case: public involvement strengthens equipoise, measurement, explanation, inference, and interpretation — stages where developers' tacit framings systematically miss harms (the Allegheny tool's bipartite-ranking bias, hidden categories in Chicago police complaints). The red-teaming study extends this logic into adversarial probing: public events at venues like DEFCON surface vulnerabilities that internal teams, constrained by NDAs and organizational framing, cannot or will not see. Both papers thus position participation as a *scientific* virtue, not just a democratic one — pushing back against assumptions that lay involvement degrades reliability.

## The Labor Question Behind Evaluation

[[Gillespie2026-aa]] sharpens this picture by asking who actually performs the sociotechnical labor of safety work. Drawing explicit parallels with the history of commercial content moderation, the essay shows red-teaming sliding into the same patterns — outsourcing, crowdwork precarity, NDA-shrouded conditions, and exposure to psychological harms including secondary trauma and moral injury. This is a productive tension with the more optimistic participatory framings: even where [[Unknown2025-qj]] sees public red-teaming as democratizing, [[Gillespie2026-aa]] warns that volunteer and event-based models may extractively rely on marginalized communities while obscuring the routine, hidden labor on which industry red-teaming depends. The promise of automating red-teaming away, both papers suggest, is more often a rhetorical move than a labor reality.

## Political Economy and the Limits of Reform

[[Vertesi2026-lv]] administers the bracing counter-argument. If [[Matias2025-px]] and [[Unknown2025-qj]] aim to improve evaluation, and [[Gillespie2026-aa]] aims to protect its workers, Vertesi and colleagues warn that all such interventions risk becoming "decoys" — substantive-seeming concerns that ultimately stabilize the Project of AI by lending it legitimacy. The "safety decoy" in particular implicates red-teaming discourse: ambiguous, often existential framings of safety let AI companies position themselves as the sole arbiters of risk, while participatory design can be enrolled into the "inevitability decoy" by lending democratic cover to foregone conclusions. The challenge this poses to the other three papers is direct: can participatory or labor-conscious reforms of evaluation reshape network power, or do they merely refine the surfaces of an entrenched political economy?

## A Shared Agenda, with Unresolved Tensions

Across the four papers, a shared agenda emerges: empirical study of safety practices as labor systems ([[Gillespie2026-aa]]), institutional redesign of evaluation to include lived-experience expertise ([[Matias2025-px]], [[Unknown2025-qj]]), and analytic attention to the financial and infrastructural assemblages that make AI possible ([[Vertesi2026-lv]]). The unresolved tension worth tracking is between *meliorist* and *structural* registers — between making AI evaluation better and questioning whether better evaluation is what accountability requires. A future entry point might examine where participatory red-teaming (the [[Unknown2025-qj]] / [[Matias2025-px]] line) becomes the kind of legitimation device [[Vertesi2026-lv]] warns about, and whether attention to labor conditions ([[Gillespie2026-aa]]) offers a middle path: material critique that resists both technical solutionism and total structural pessimism.
