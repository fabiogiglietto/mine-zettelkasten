# Online Radicalization and Far-Right Mainstreaming

## From Cause to Catalyst: Reframing the Internet's Role

The arc traced by these three papers moves from a panoramic stocktaking of the field to two close-grained case studies of how mainstreaming actually happens on contemporary platforms. [[Rothut2026-wt]] sets the conceptual terms: after 25 years of research, the internet is best understood not as a root cause of radicalization but as a catalyst operating through hybrid socio-technical environments where online and offline are no longer meaningfully separable. Their scoping review identifies an "empirical turn" since 2018 and a growing differentiation of radicalization phenomena beyond the traditional left-right axis — yet it also flags persistent gaps, particularly around longitudinal causal evidence and understudied platforms. The two empirical pieces here can be read as responses to precisely those gaps, drilling into the meso- and macro-level mechanisms of mainstreaming on Instagram and across Facebook/X/TikTok.

## Affect, Humour, and Multimodal Softening

A shared thesis emerges across [[Nangle2026-yo]] and [[Bouchafra2026-ts]]: contemporary far-right mainstreaming works less through overt hostility than through affectively resonant, multimodal forms — memes, Reels, short videos — that soften and normalise extremist worldviews. [[Nangle2026-yo]] theorises far-right Instagram as an "affective-discursive ecology" in which positive affect and humour, rather than aggression, do the ideological work of normalisation. [[Bouchafra2026-ts]] extends this empirically into electoral politics, showing that the Sweden Democrats made memes 67.5% of their 2024 EU campaign output and concentrated memetic, affectively charged securitising content on TikTok to reach younger voters. Both studies thus operationalise what [[Rothut2026-wt]] identifies at the macro level as the "mainstreaming of extremist ideas" — but locate it in concrete platform vernaculars and aesthetic strategies.

## Platform Logics and Algorithmic Proximity

The two empirical papers also converge on the claim that platform architectures are constitutive, not merely conduits. [[Nangle2026-yo]] foregrounds "algorithmic proximity" as a mechanism by which Instagram's recommender logics bring mainstream users into contact with far-right adjacent content, supporting both visibility and mobilisation. [[Bouchafra2026-ts]] offers a complementary cross-platform argument: the same political actor deliberately tailors content to each platform's "digital architecture," producing argumentative text-heavy posts on Facebook and X but viral, exemplificatory memes on TikTok. Read alongside [[Rothut2026-wt]]'s observation that research has overwhelmingly concentrated on Twitter/X, YouTube, and Facebook while neglecting TikTok, gaming spaces, and alternative platforms, [[Bouchafra2026-ts]] in particular helps close a documented blind spot in the field.

## Mainstreaming, Mobilisation, and Open Questions

Taken together, the three papers suggest that the analytic frontier has shifted from asking *whether* the internet radicalises to asking *how* extremist content is rendered ordinary, shareable, and electorally productive. [[Bouchafra2026-ts]]'s finding that SD distributed remixable meme templates — enacting a kind of "audience agency" in securitisation while preserving plausible deniability — resonates with the meso-level dynamics of co-radicalization and emotional convergence catalogued in [[Rothut2026-wt]], and with the participatory memetic culture analysed in [[Nangle2026-yo]]. Yet important tensions remain. Neither empirical study can settle the causal questions [[Rothut2026-wt]] identifies as unresolved: exposure, circulation, and even electoral gains among young voters are suggestive, but not proof of radicalization pathways at the individual level. The structure-note take-away is that affective multimodal content and platform-specific architectures form the current empirical centre of gravity, while longitudinal and recipient-level work — especially on TikTok and other understudied environments — remains the field's most pressing gap.
