# Digital Social Network Analysis

## From Counting Edges to Mapping Practices

The methodological backbone of this collection is a steady push beyond first-generation network analysis — beyond retweet graphs flattened into "hairballs" — toward representations that preserve time, modality, and discursive content. [[Bruns2025-fz]] frames the problem explicitly: conventional visualisations obscure rather than reveal multimodal interaction, motivating a turn to vector embeddings of *practices* rather than ties. [[Mannocci2025-ig]] and [[Iannucci2025-eg]] formalise this intuition for coordination detection, showing that monomodal or naively flattened approaches discard structurally central nodes and that multiplex representations — especially when augmented with temporal decay kernels — recover coordination patterns that single-layer methods miss. [[Gerard2025-br]] pushes furthest in this direction, abandoning interaction signals altogether in favour of *latent narrative affiliations*, a move motivated as much by API decay as by theoretical commitment. Together these papers trace an arc from edges, to layered edges, to edges-as-shared-discourse.

## Detecting Coordination — and Questioning Its Importance

A second, tightly coupled thread concerns the detection of coordinated inauthentic behaviour (CIB) across platforms and the limits of what such detection tells us. [[Yang2025-iv]] targets evasion-resistant signals in Facebook link sharing; [[Luceri2025-tr]] adapts network-based similarity methods to TikTok's video-first ecosystem; [[Kuznetsova2025-nu]] applies forwarding-network analysis to pro-regime Telegram channels; [[Kulichkina2026-zk]] surfaces coordinated multilingual activity around China's COVID-19 protests; and [[Minici2024-tf]] proposes a graph foundation model aimed at cross-campaign generalisation. Yet [[Di-Marco2025-aa]] delivers an important corrective: when the influence of coordinated accounts in real retweet cascades is benchmarked against optimal and greedy placements, observed CIB looks closer to random than to strategic — suggesting the detection literature may overstate the diffusion-shaping power of what it finds. [[Simeone2025-vo]] reinforces the point from the opposite direction: removing only a handful of *organically* central accounts via deplatforming produced disproportionate ripple effects on the Arizona Election Review community, whereas CIB barely registered as a driver of activity there.

## Algorithms, Amplification, and the Mechanics of Attention

A third cluster examines how platform infrastructures themselves shape visibility and tie formation. [[Efstratiou2025-gs]] audits post-acquisition Twitter and finds that apparent right-leaning amplification largely dissolves once one controls for agitating content, verification status, and proximity to Elon Musk — relocating the explanatory weight from "political bias" to perverse engagement incentives and owner-centric centralisation. [[Brown2026-br]] performs an analogous disentangling for YouTube, separating supply-side from demand-side effects to argue that echo chambers are mostly user-driven, rabbit holes are real and Markov-like, and radicalisation pathways are absent — though the algorithm exhibits a uniform moderately conservative tilt. [[Smith2025-kc]] supplies a causal microfoundation for these aggregate patterns by identifying the *tertius amplificans*: high-degree accounts whose retweets-with-attribution accelerate triad closure, locally generating the transitivity that global structural analyses observe.

## Curation, Fragmentation, and the Sedimentation of Polarisation

Whereas the algorithmic-audit papers ask what platforms push, a fourth thread asks what users assemble. [[Green2025-ap]] argues that source-level partisanship scores systematically mistake curation-driven heterogeneity for moderation: individual *stories* travel into distinct partisan bubbles even when their parent domains look centrist. [[Dehghan2026-sy]] zooms out to 16 years of political Reddit, finding minimal cross-ideological overlap on any measurable dimension and theorising polarisation as a *sedimented* condition produced by an entanglement of discourse, moderation, and platform materiality — r/politics, notably, fails to function as the bridging town square it is often imagined to be. [[Sarmiento2025-as]] supplies a complementary methodological lever, using unsupervised pipelines to surface emergent frames in polarised discussions without imposing pre-defined categories.

## Hyperpartisan Ecosystems and the Mainstreaming of the Fringe

A final thread uses these network-analytic tools to chart the architecture of contemporary right-wing media ecosystems and their entanglement with protest publics. [[Copland2025-em]] reconstructs Sky News Australia as a transnational node of network propaganda, deploying a bifurcated YouTube/Facebook strategy that inserts Australian content into the US right-wing pipeline. [[Askanius2026-de]] maps the Swedish far-right's alternative influence network on YouTube, showing how guest appearances, hyperlinking, and microcelebrity practices hybridise political extremism with influencer commerce — and how pandemic conditions catalysed alliances with wellness and conspiracy milieus. [[Rothut2026-or]] formalises this last dynamic as *protest-facilitated mainstreaming*: the Querdenken movement on Telegram, though grassroots in origin, became structurally embedded in far-right and QAnon networks via shared anti-elite framing, with far-right brokers — not protesters — occupying the network's centre. Read alongside [[Gerard2025-br]]'s "bridge users" who seed cross-platform narrative migration and [[Smith2025-kc]]'s attention brokers who causally accelerate tie formation, these studies converge on a common claim: the consequential structures of contemporary political communication are produced by small, well-placed brokerage roles operating at the seams between communities, platforms, and ideological registers.
