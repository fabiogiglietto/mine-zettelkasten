# Digital Methods and Research Ethics

## The shifting terrain of platform-based social research

The papers gathered here trace a discipline in flux. [[Yang2026-tq]] and [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] supply the bibliometric backbone: empirical social media research grew steeply for over a decade, dominated by Twitter/X and Facebook, before plateauing and declining in 2022–2024 as APIs closed. [[Freelon2024-sc]] gives this arc its conceptual shape, periodising platform-research relations from the "laissez-faire" era through today's "post-API age" of pay-to-play and walled gardens. Together they frame a field whose empirical capacity is tied to infrastructures it does not control — a recognition that motivates nearly every other paper in the topic.

## Auditing the instruments

A cluster of methodological audits interrogates the tools researchers still have. [[Rieder2025-ju]] shows YouTube's search API is "forgetful by design," with severe temporal decay and inconsistent repeated queries that compromise replicability. [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] demonstrates how a single anonymisation threshold in Meta's URL Shares Dataset produces spurious cross-country similarities that overwhelm genuine social signals. [[Ulloa2024-jm]] turns the critique on scraping itself, finding that the *environment* of ex-situ collection — paywalls, logins, consent walls — distorts measured news exposure far more than temporal decay. [[Luhring2025-od]] audits NewsGuard as a source-rating infrastructure, showing how seemingly minor choices (binary vs. continuous scores) can swing substantive conclusions. The collective message is that platform and vendor data are not neutral substrates: their construction quietly shapes findings.

## Alternative routes and their limits

When official channels narrow, researchers improvise. [[Iannelli2018-ebd918b7]] pioneers Facebook Ads as a recruitment instrument for niche populations, exploiting Pixel-based tracking to refine response-rate measurement — though its effectiveness at isolating ideologically distinctive samples proves inconclusive. [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]] uses volunteer browser plugins to audit political advertising in Brazil, an explicitly crowdsourced workaround to platform opacity. [[Bruns2025-fz]] proposes "practice mapping" via vector embeddings as a methodological advance beyond network "furballs." [[Balluff2026-if]] cautions that the newest workaround — LLMs for annotation, synthetic data, and simulation — risks reintroducing the same dependencies on opaque corporate infrastructures, with added problems of reproducibility, bias, and environmental cost.

## Validity beyond access

Even with access, the social inferences drawable from platform traces are constrained. [[Oswald2025-km]] foregrounds the production-consumption gap: a small minority generates most political content, so visible discourse misrepresents the silent majority for citizens and researchers alike. This concern compounds the platform-level distortions documented by [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] and [[Rieder2025-ju]], and partly explains the misalignment noted in [[Yang2026-tq]] between research attention (Twitter-heavy) and actual public platform use (YouTube-heavy). Methodological validity, these papers insist, is not solved by larger N.

## The political economy of platform research

A final, increasingly central strand reframes data access as a question of power and integrity. [[Bak-Coleman2025-pm]] and [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] argue — drawing explicit analogies to tobacco, pharma, and fossil-fuel science — that the tech industry's monopoly over data, funding, and access has produced an "industrial saturation" of roughly 80% in high-profile social media research, with most ties undisclosed. [[Heiss2026-qv]] develops the parallel argument programmatically, contending that open-science practices alone cannot neutralise an asymmetry in which platforms control the very questions that can be asked. [[Peters2026-mo]] traces how "data quality" itself became a contested object in the EU DSA consultation process, with academic and NGO actors only partially succeeding against platform framings. [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] and [[Freelon2024-sc]] connect this regulatory contest to the bibliometric evidence, treating instruments like the DSA and the proposed Platform Accountability and Transparency Act as necessary infrastructures for an independent science of platforms.

## A field reflecting on itself

Read together, these papers describe a methodological field that has moved from celebratory adoption of digital traces toward sustained reflexivity about its dependencies. The arc runs from documenting *what* platforms enabled ([[Yang2026-tq]], [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]]) through auditing *how* their instruments distort ([[Rieder2025-ju]], [[Ulloa2024-jm]], [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]], [[Luhring2025-od]]) to interrogating *who* shapes the resulting evidence base ([[Bak-Coleman2025-pm]], [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]], [[Heiss2026-qv]], [[Peters2026-mo]]). The recurring move is to treat research infrastructures — APIs, ad platforms, rating databases, LLMs, regulatory texts — as themselves objects of social-scientific scrutiny rather than transparent windows onto social life.
