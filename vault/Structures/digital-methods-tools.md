# Digital Methods and Open Research Tools

## The Closing of an Empirical Era

A spine runs through these papers: the empirical study of social media is being structurally reshaped by platforms' withdrawal of data access, and the field is collectively scrambling to document, theorize and work around this transformation. [[Yang2026-tq]] provides the broadest empirical statement of the problem — a systematic review showing that the proportion of social science papers using social media data tripled to 2022 and then plateaued and declined as APIs closed. [[Murtfeldt2025-wu]] makes the same point with sharper focus, "eulogizing" the Twitter API by quantifying 33,306 studies it enabled and the 13% drop in output following its 2023 paywall. [[Freelon2024-sc]] periodizes this trajectory across six platforms, and [[Giglietto2026-855a54cb]] tells the parallel story for Twitter/X specifically, framing the arc from "Wild West" through "Golden Age" to the post-Musk walled garden. Together these four papers establish that data access is not a technical detail but a force shaping what social science can know — a point [[Unknown2025-ed60bc90]] picks up via the EU's Digital Services Act.

## Auditing the Tools We Inherit

A second cluster turns the methodological gaze inward, auditing the tools and data infrastructures researchers actually use. [[Rieder2025-ju]] shows that YouTube's search API is "forgetful by design," exhibiting severe temporal decay that undermines retrospective research and DSA compliance alike. [[Luhring2025-od]] performs an analogous audit of NewsGuard, finding that continuous trust scores are stable but binary thresholds can flip headline results. [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]] surfaces a subtler artifact in Meta's URL Shares Dataset, where the 100-share anonymization threshold creates spurious cross-country similarities driven by Feed-ranking changes rather than social signals. [[Ulloa2024-jm]] extends this skepticism to web-tracking workflows, demonstrating that ex-situ scraping introduces ~34% content disparity — far more than time delay alone — and that in-situ capture is qualitatively different. The collective lesson is that the instruments through which we read the social media environment are themselves variable, opaque, and consequential for substantive findings.

## Building Alternatives: Workflows and Recruitment

If platform-provided infrastructure is unreliable, researchers must build their own. [[Iannelli2018-ebd918b7]] develops a Facebook-ad recruitment procedure with Pixel-based conversion tracking for niche populations, showing both its efficiency and the limits of "interest" targeting for reaching ideologically distinctive samples. [[Inacio-da-Silva2026-zf]] demonstrates volunteer browser-plugin auditing of political ads in Brazil. [[Allen2025-ot]] frames Piccardi et al.'s browser-extension reranking as a "platform-independent" experimental paradigm necessitated by closed APIs. [[Giglietto2023-fa71a001]] proposes an iterative workflow that uses known coordinated accounts to surface new ones, rejecting the static-list paradigm. [[Fan2026-af]] points toward a future where donated user-sequence data, analyzed with sequence mining, HMMs, and embedding models, replaces aggregated cross-sectional snapshots. These approaches share a posture: independent research as an act of methodological self-sufficiency.

## Detecting Coordination: A Maturing Research Program

The most internally cumulative thread concerns coordinated inauthentic behavior (CIB). [[Yang2025-iv]] argues that timing-based detection is too easily evaded and proposes statistical regularities in link-sharing speed/frequency as more robust signals. [[Mannocci2025-ig]] systematically compares five operationalizations of multimodal coordination on UK election data, finding that multiplex community detection best balances integration with inclusiveness, and that the prevailing practice of network flattening loses structurally important nodes. [[Iannucci2025-eg]] pushes further by combining temporal kernels with multiplex layers, benchmarking against 26 labeled datasets. [[Luceri2025-tr]] extends CIB detection to TikTok's video-first ecosystem, while [[Pante2025-pq]] applies the maturing methodological apparatus reflexively, questioning earlier claims of inter-state coordination once control datasets are introduced. The arc is one of disciplinary self-correction: from single-modality, timing-based heuristics toward multiplex, temporally aware, control-anchored detection.

## Rethinking What Social Media Data Represent

A reflective strand interrogates what platform traces actually measure. [[Oswald2025-km]] foregrounds the production–consumption gap, warning that the visible "tip of the iceberg" misrepresents silent majorities for both citizens and researchers. [[Green2025-ap]] argues that domain-level partisanship measures conflate heterogeneity with moderation, and that story-level "curation bubbles" reveal a more polarized circulation than source-level analyses suggest. [[McNally2025-dn]] uses time-series methods on a decade of Guardian engagement to show that Facebook's News Feed is not a black box but a sociotechnical system with detectable, lagged, content-differentiated effects — a finding with direct DSA-auditing implications. [[Gaisbauer2025-by]] critiques the field's outlet-level, unidimensional treatment of partisanship; [[Bruns2025-fz]] proposes "practice mapping" via vector embeddings to escape the network-visualization "furball." [[Scalco2026-bd]] introduces a quantitative operationalization of "information voids" as demand–supply imbalances, connecting infrastructural measurement to substantive misinformation outcomes. Each paper insists that the gap between trace data and the social phenomena researchers want to describe must itself be theorized.

## Methodological Reflexivity and the Politics of the Field

A final cluster takes the field's epistemics as its object. [[Bak-Coleman2026-mk]] documents that roughly half of high-profile social media papers have undisclosed industry ties, and that "industrial saturation" extends to editors and reviewers — a finding that complicates every other claim in this Zettelkasten. [[Balluff2026-if]] makes a parallel intervention regarding LLMs, warning against unreflective adoption given reproducibility, bias, environmental, and ethical costs. [[Stanusch2026-ec]] reads the AI industry's own self-imaginaries during the Altman controversy, showing how "premediation" and "preclusion" absorb critique. [[Gardam2025-er]] argues for visual-first methods on image-driven platforms, and [[Anwar2024-34dba628]] systematically reviews how Facebook Reactions have been operationalized as affective signals — illustrating both the productivity and fragmentation of platform-affordance research.

## The Arc

Taken together, these papers describe a field whose data foundations are eroding, whose tools demand auditing, whose methods are diversifying out of necessity, and whose internal incentives — industry ties, opaque LLMs, platform-dependent collaborations — are themselves under scrutiny. The recurring move is from naive reliance on platform-provided affordances toward methodological self-awareness: multiplex rather than flat, temporal rather than cross-sectional, in-situ rather than scraped, independent rather than industry-tied, and regulatory rather than philanthropic in the sources of legitimate access. Whether the EU DSA can stabilize this transition, or whether the field will fragment into bespoke workflows for each platform and team, is the open question these papers collectively pose.
