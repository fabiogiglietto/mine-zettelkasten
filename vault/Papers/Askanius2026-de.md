# The alternative influence network (AIN) of far-right YouTubers in Sweden: Connectivity and hybridisation of online extremism during the Covid-19 pandemic

## Summary

This study maps the Swedish far-right "alternative influence network" (AIN) on YouTube during the Covid-19 pandemic (April 2020–March 2022), combining social network analysis of 52 channels and 1,512 videos with qualitative close reading of influencer practices. Building on Lewis's AIN concept and Petersen and Johansen's hybridisation framework, the authors argue that Swedish far-right YouTubers, organised groups, and hyper-partisan alternative media form a cohesive yet ideologically heterogeneous ecosystem stitched together through guest appearances, hyperlinking, and in-video mentions. The pandemic, they show, accelerated a hybridisation of online extremism at the levels of actors, practices, and content — fusing traditional far-right propaganda with the commercial logics of influencer culture and forging new alliances with anti-vaccine, conspiracy, and wellness milieus.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic mapping of the Swedish-language far-right AIN on YouTube during the pandemic.
- Extends Lewis's AIN concept to a Nordic context and adapts it to capture pandemic-era convergence with the "Freedom Movement".
- Empirically operationalises Petersen and Johansen's hybridisation framework across three levels: actors, practices, and content.
- Demonstrates the methodological payoff of combining SNA with qualitative close reading for extremism research.
- Documents the monetisation infrastructure (donations, Patreon, Super Chat, merchandise, sponsorships) underpinning professionalised far-right activism.
- Shows how deplatforming reshapes rather than eliminates extremist visibility, through coded language, on-screen URLs, and "phantom videos" redirecting to alt-tech.

## Methods

A mixed-methods design combining social network analysis with qualitative case study. The authors purposively sampled 25 Swedish far-right YouTube channels (influencers, groups, alternative media) and expanded via snowball sampling to 52 channels, yielding a corpus of 8,531 videos. Manual coding of 1,512 videos across three sampled months captured three connective practices — guest appearances, hyperlinking (description and in-video), and in-video mentions (in 347 videos from selected weeks). Networks were visualised in Gephi as directed graphs with weighted edges, alongside close readings of self-branding, monetisation, and moderation-evasion tactics.

## Findings

- Over 90% of analysed videos referenced another AIN actor through at least one connective practice.
- The guest appearance network (151 nodes, 228 directed links, 480 guest cases) forms star-like clusters around bridging hosts rather than a single tight community.
- Hyperlinking was the densest practice: 6,172 hyperlinks (294 nodes, 557 directed links), largely pointing to alternative news media, advertisers, and alt-tech platforms.
- Alternative outlets (Riks, FriaTider, Samnytt) act as central legitimising hubs that host influencers as "alternative experts".
- A self-named "Swedish Tube Family" subcluster (7.3% of nodes, 19.7% of links) produced 55.6% of sampled content via coordinated mutual promotion.
- In-video mentions are densely concentrated and largely unidirectional (only 17 of 334 ties reciprocal); the Sweden Democrats were the most-mentioned actor.
- Influencers cultivated parasocial intimacy through microcelebrity tactics — strategic authenticity, signature greetings, in-group labels — fused with explicit monetisation (Swish, Bitcoin, Patreon, merchandise).
- Moderation was evaded with on-screen URLs, misspellings, coded euphemisms (e.g., "kanelbulle" for vaccines), and "phantom videos" redirecting audiences to Swebbtube, Rumble, Odysee, and BitChute.
- The Swedish AIN is transnationally networked with US, UK, and German far-right and pandemic-protest actors, with several channels broadcasting in English.
