# Cracking Open the European Newsfeed

> Rossi, L., Giglietto, F., & Marino, G. (2023). Cracking Open the European Newsfeed. *Journal of Quantitative Description: Digital Media*. https://doi.org/10.51685/jqd.2023.020
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.51685/jqd.2023.020)

## Summary

This paper replicates and extends the US-focused study by Guess et al. (2021) on Facebook news quality to the European context, examining the prevalence, views, and sharing of trustworthy and untrustworthy URLs in Germany, France, and Italy from 2019 to 2022. Combining Meta's privacy-protected URL Shares Dataset with NewsGuard domain ratings, the authors find that while total URL volume on Facebook has declined and the *proportion* of untrustworthy URLs has risen — particularly during national election years in Germany and Italy — the share of *Views* from untrustworthy sources has remained relatively stable. Echoing US findings, older cohorts (45+) dominate both exposure to and sharing of low-quality news, while users under 34 play a marginal role. The paper doubles as a methodological reflection on the limits of using these datasets for cross-national longitudinal comparison.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic comparative description of trustworthy vs. untrustworthy news circulation on Facebook across three large European countries using Meta's URL Shares Dataset.
- Temporal (through 2022) and geographic (EU) extension of Guess et al. (2021).
- Methodological critique of the URL Shares Dataset — especially the 100-share inclusion threshold — and of longitudinal use of NewsGuard ratings, including empirical checks of rating stability.
- Cross-national documentation of age-skew in misinformation engagement in Europe, mirroring US patterns.
- Discussion of implications for forthcoming EU Digital Services Act transparency and data-access obligations.

## Methods

The authors replicate Guess et al. (2021) using adapted public code (Aslett, 2022) on Meta's URL Shares Dataset v10.1 (Jan 2017–Oct 2022), filtered by `top_country` for France, Germany, Italy, and the US. Domains are classified Trustworthy (≥60) or Untrustworthy (<60) using an October 2022 NewsGuard snapshot of 8,506 rated domains, applied retrospectively after verifying that only ~1–3% of domains changed category over the period. Coverage is benchmarked against Lasser-Rupp, Media Bias/Fact Check, and Grinberg et al. (2019). Confidence intervals around Views and Shares account for the dataset's differential-privacy noise (σ=14 shares; σ=2,228 views). Engagement is disaggregated across six age cohorts.

## Findings

- France has the highest URL volume on Facebook, followed by Italy; Germany's is roughly half or less, consistent with lower Facebook news use there.
- In 2019, untrustworthy URL shares were similar (~13–14%) across the three countries; by 2022 they diverged sharply — France 11%, Germany ~21%, Italy 22%, with peaks aligning with national election years.
- Absolute Views and Shares of untrustworthy URLs peaked in 2020 and then declined, tracking the overall URL drop.
- Despite the rising *proportion* of untrustworthy URLs, the *proportion* of Views from untrustworthy sources stayed flat or slightly declined, suggesting platform-level dampening of exposure.
- Untrustworthy Shares rose to 22% in Germany (2021) and 26% in Italy (2022).
- Sharing skews older than exposure: 65+ dominates in France (28%), 55–64 in Germany (36%), 45–54 in Italy (33%); users <34 are marginal.
- NewsGuard coverage correlates more tightly with US data than EU data, justifying EU-only comparisons.

## Connections

This paper sits squarely in the lineage of large-scale platform-data studies of misinformation exposure that began with [[Bakshy2015-rn]] and was crystallized by [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]] and [[Budak2024-ef]], to which it offers a European comparative counterpoint. Its methodological reflections on the URL Shares Dataset, NewsGuard, and the 100-share threshold connect to broader debates on platform data access and transparency under the DSA found in [[Rieder2025-ju]], [[Rieder2026-pp]], and [[Ohme2026-nv]]. The age-skew findings resonate with the recurring empirical pattern that older users disproportionately drive untrustworthy sharing, as also discussed in [[Freelon2024-sc]] and [[Allen2025-ot]].
