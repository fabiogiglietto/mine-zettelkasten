# Cracking Open the European Newsfeed

> Rossi, L., Giglietto, F., & Marino, G. (2023). Cracking Open the European Newsfeed. *Journal of Quantitative Description: Digital Media*. https://doi.org/10.51685/jqd.2023.020
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.51685/jqd.2023.020)

## Summary

This paper extends Guess et al.'s (2021) US-focused analysis of news quality on Facebook to a European setting, examining the prevalence, exposure, and sharing of trustworthy and untrustworthy URLs in Germany, France, and Italy from 2019 to 2022. Drawing on Meta's privacy-protected URL Shares Dataset combined with NewsGuard domain ratings, the authors document a steady decline in total URLs shared alongside a rising *share* of untrustworthy URLs in Germany and Italy — peaking in national election years — while the *percentage of Views* from untrustworthy sources remains comparatively stable. They argue that older cohorts (45+) dominate both exposure to and sharing of untrustworthy content, replicating US findings in three distinct European media ecosystems. The piece also functions as a methodological audit of the dataset, flagging the 100-share threshold, NewsGuard's US-skewed coverage, and differential privacy noise as constraints on cross-national inference.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic comparative quantification of trustworthy vs. untrustworthy news circulation on Facebook across France, Germany, and Italy using Meta's URL Shares Dataset.
- Temporal and geographic extension of Guess et al. (2021), covering 2019–2022 in three EU contexts.
- Methodological critique of the URL Shares Dataset (100-share threshold, differential privacy noise) and of longitudinal use of NewsGuard ratings, including a stability check showing only 1–3% of domain categorisations shifted over the period.
- Documentation of age-skewed engagement with untrustworthy content in European contexts, mirroring patterns previously identified in the US.
- Empirically grounded reflections on what the EU Digital Services Act's transparency obligations should enable for platform research.

## Methods

The authors adapt Aslett's (2022) replication code for Guess et al. (2021) to European data. They analyse Meta's URL Shares Dataset v10.1 (Jan 2017–Oct 2022), filtered by `top_country` for DE, FR, IT, and the US, classifying domains via NewsGuard's October 2022 snapshot (8,506 rated domains; Trustworthy ≥60, Untrustworthy <60) applied retrospectively. NewsGuard coverage is benchmarked against the Lasser–Rupp list, Media Bias/Fact Check, and Grinberg et al. (2019). Confidence intervals account for differential privacy noise (σ=14 for shares, σ=2,228 for views), and Views/Shares are disaggregated across six age cohorts.

## Findings

- France consistently shows the highest URL volume, Italy second, Germany roughly half of France — consistent with lower Facebook news adoption in Germany.
- In 2019, untrustworthy URL shares were similar (~13–14%) across the three countries; by 2022 they diverged sharply (FR 11%, DE ~21%, IT 22%), with peaks coinciding with national elections.
- Absolute Views and Shares of untrustworthy URLs peaked in 2020 and then declined alongside overall URL volume.
- The *percentage* of Views from untrustworthy sources stayed stable or slightly declined despite the rising share of untrustworthy URLs, suggesting platform-level dampening of exposure.
- Sharing of untrustworthy content skews older than exposure does: 65+ dominates in France (28%), 55–64 in Germany (36%), and 45–54 in Italy (33%); users under 34 play a marginal role.
- US data exhibit a stronger correlation between NewsGuard's share of untrustworthy domains and dataset-level untrustworthy URLs than European data, justifying the EU-only comparative framing.

## Connections

This paper sits squarely within the lineage of large-scale quantitative audits of misinformation prevalence on Facebook initiated by Guess and colleagues and continued in [[Gonzalez-Bailon2024-rq]] and [[Bakshy2015-rn]], extending that agenda comparatively into Europe. It also speaks directly to the platform-data-access literature, including [[Rieder2025-ju]], [[Rieder2026-pp]], and [[Bruns2026-yv]], which probe the limits and politics of industry-academic data partnerships under the DSA. Its empirical emphasis on age-skewed sharing of low-quality content connects to broader debates about who actually spreads misinformation, echoing themes in [[Budak2024-ef]] and [[Allen2025-ot]].
