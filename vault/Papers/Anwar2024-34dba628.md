# Facebook reactions in the context of politics and social issues: a systematic literature review

> Anwar, S., & Giglietto, F. (2024). Facebook reactions in the context of politics and social issues: a systematic literature review. *Frontiers in Sociology*. https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoc.2024.1379265
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoc.2024.1379265)

## Summary

This systematic literature review synthesises 64 peer-reviewed studies (2016–2023) on how users engage with Facebook's "Reactions" feature (Love, Haha, Wow, Sad, Angry) introduced in February 2016. Framing Reactions as paralinguistic digital affordances that convey nuanced affect at low cognitive cost, the authors organise the fragmented, interdisciplinary literature into three clusters — introductory/foundational work, politics and far-right groups, and other social issues (psychological, healthcare, business, miscellaneous) — and extract cross-study regularities. Their central argument is that Reactions function as a compact, comparable proxy for audience emotion, with sociopolitical content consistently drawing broader and more negative reaction profiles than lifestyle or entertainment content, and with anger in particular fuelling engagement with populist and far-right messaging.

## Key Contributions

- First comprehensive systematic synthesis of Facebook Reactions research from their 2016 introduction through 2023.
- A thematic taxonomy that organises a scattered, cross-disciplinary literature into coherent categories.
- Identification of recurring cross-study patterns, especially the role of negative emotion in driving political engagement.
- Diagnosis of methodological gaps: inconsistent data sources, weak cross-cultural comparability, and underexplored event- and tabloid-based topics.
- A research agenda that includes LLM-assisted reaction categorisation and applications to misinformation, fact-checking, and digital activism.

## Methods

The review follows Briner and Denyer's (2012) five-step protocol: framing research questions, searching (Scopus, Google Scholar, and other engines from July–September 2023 using "Facebook" + "Reaction" Boolean queries), selecting peer-reviewed articles and conference proceedings from 2016–2023, evaluating them, and synthesising results. The final corpus of 64 articles was thematically coded via Armstrong's (2010) comparative naturalistic method, grouped into (A) Introduction of Facebook Reactions (6), (B) Politics and Far-right Groups (19), and (C) Other Social Issues (39), with further sub-topics.

## Findings

- Lifestyle and entertainment content skews toward positive reactions (mainly Love and Haha), while sociopolitical content elicits a wider, more negative emotional spectrum.
- Across political campaigns (Mexico, Brexit, EU Parliament), negative emotional appeals — especially anger — outperform positive content in engagement and sharing.
- Populist and far-right leaders attract disproportionately more Angry and Haha reactions than mainstream politicians; anger-laden posts are shared more.
- Hyperpartisan and junk news show a bivalent emotional logic: Love and Anger rarely co-occur, whereas Anger co-occurs with Sad and Wow.
- Reactions have been used to predict emotion classes, measure controversy via entropy of reaction distributions, and label sentiment — though mappings to emotion categories are sometimes ambiguous (e.g., Wow as disbelief; Haha as mockery).
- Reactions serve as effective sentiment labels for low-resource languages such as Sinhala and Bangla.
- Cultural context shapes reaction patterns, evidenced in comparisons of China-critical, China-supporting, and neutral media audiences in Hong Kong.

## Connections

This review consolidates the empirical basis for a stream of work that uses Facebook Reactions and engagement metrics to trace political and problematic information flows, closely aligned with [[Giglietto2020-9d8acdd7]], [[Giglietto2022-b30e8b4e]], [[Giglietto2023-fa71a001]], and [[Giglietto2025-1e9a0917]] on coordinated link sharing and junk news, as well as [[Iannelli2018-ebd918b7]] and [[Iannelli2015-e0818c3e]] on affective and political engagement on the platform. Its emphasis on anger-driven engagement with populist and far-right content resonates with populism-focused analyses such as [[Larsson2026-ro]] and with platform-affordance perspectives in [[Rieder2025-ju]] and [[Bruns2025-fz]].
