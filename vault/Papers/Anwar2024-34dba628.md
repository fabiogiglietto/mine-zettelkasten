# Facebook reactions in the context of politics and social issues: a systematic literature review

> Anwar, S., & Giglietto, F. (2024). Facebook reactions in the context of politics and social issues: a systematic literature review. *Frontiers in Sociology*. https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoc.2024.1379265
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoc.2024.1379265)

## Summary

This systematic literature review synthesises 64 peer-reviewed studies (2016–2023) on Facebook's "Reactions" feature (Love, Haha, Wow, Sad, Angry), introduced in February 2016 as an emotional expansion of the Like button. Anwar and Giglietto organise the fragmented, interdisciplinary literature into three thematic clusters — the introduction of Reactions, politics and far-right groups, and other social issues (psychological, healthcare, business, miscellaneous) — and extract cross-study regularities via meta-analysis. Their central argument is that Reactions operate as paralinguistic digital affordances that make affective response legible at scale, and that negative emotional content, especially anger, consistently drives higher engagement in political communication, particularly for populist and far-right actors.

## Key Contributions

- First comprehensive systematic synthesis of Facebook Reactions research across the 2016–2023 window.
- Thematic taxonomy organising an interdisciplinary literature into coherent categories (introduction, politics/far-right, other social issues).
- Identification of cross-study regularities, notably the anger–engagement relationship in political and populist content.
- Diagnosis of methodological gaps: absence of unified data sources, thin cross-cultural comparison, and underexplored event-based or tabloid domains.
- Agenda-setting suggestions, including LLM-assisted categorisation of reaction patterns and applications to misinformation and digital activism research.

## Methods

Five-step systematic review following Briner and Denyer (2012): question formulation, study retrieval, selection/evaluation, analysis, and reporting. Searches in Scopus, Google Scholar, and other engines were run monthly from July–September 2023 combining "Facebook" and "Reaction" with Boolean variants. Inclusion was restricted to peer-reviewed journal articles and conference proceedings (2016–2023), yielding 64 items. Each article was thematically coded via a comparative naturalistic method (Armstrong, 2010) by research question, results, and conclusions, and grouped into three major categories with finer subtopics.

## Findings

- Of 64 articles, 6 concerned the introduction of Reactions, 19 politics and far-right groups, and 39 other social issues.
- In political campaigns (Mexico, Brexit, EU Parliament elections), negative emotional appeals — especially anger — significantly outperformed positive content in engagement and sharing.
- Populist and far-right leaders disproportionately attract "angry" and "haha" reactions relative to mainstream politicians; anger-triggering messaging boosts sharing.
- Hyperpartisan and junk news show a bivalent emotional logic: "love" and "anger" rarely co-occur, while "anger" co-occurs with "sad" and "wow".
- Reactions have been used computationally to predict emotion classes, controversy (via entropy of reaction distributions), and sentiment, though reaction-to-emotion mappings remain ambiguous.
- Cross-linguistic work (Sinhala, Bangla) shows Reactions can serve as sentiment labels for low-resource languages.
- Lifestyle/entertainment posts skew positive; sociopolitical posts elicit a broader, more negative spectrum.
- Cultural context matters, e.g., contrasting reaction patterns across China-critical, China-supporting, and neutral audiences in Hong Kong.
