# Facebook reactions in the context of politics and social issues: a systematic literature review

> Anwar, S., & Giglietto, F. (2024). Facebook reactions in the context of politics and social issues: a systematic literature review. *Frontiers in Sociology*. https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoc.2024.1379265
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoc.2024.1379265)

## Summary

This systematic literature review synthesizes 64 peer-reviewed studies (2016–2023) on how users engage with Facebook's Reactions (Love, Haha, Wow, Sad, Angry) since their 2016 rollout. Organizing the literature into three clusters — the introduction of Reactions, politics and far-right groups, and other social issues (health, business, psychology, miscellaneous) — the authors argue that Reactions function as paralinguistic digital affordances that carry nuanced emotional signals at low cognitive cost. The meta-analysis surfaces a robust cross-study regularity: lifestyle and entertainment content skews positive, while sociopolitical content elicits a wider, more negative emotional spectrum, with anger in particular driving engagement for populist and far-right actors.

## Key Contributions

- First comprehensive systematic synthesis of Facebook Reactions research from 2016–2023 across political, health, business, and psychological domains.
- A thematic taxonomy that structures a fragmented, interdisciplinary literature into coherent categories.
- Identification of cross-study regularities, especially the link between negative emotion (anger) and engagement with populist/far-right content.
- Diagnosis of methodological gaps: lack of unified data sources, limited cross-cultural comparative work, underexplored event-based and tabloid topics.
- Research agenda proposing LLM-based categorization of reaction patterns and applications to misinformation, fact-checking, and digital activism.

## Methods

Five-step systematic review following Briner and Denyer (2012): question formulation, searching (Scopus, Google Scholar, other engines, July–September 2023 using "Facebook" and "Reaction" plus Boolean operators), selection (peer-reviewed journal and conference articles 2016–2023, n=64), analysis via Armstrong's (2010) comparative naturalistic method, and reporting. Articles were thematically coded by research question, results, and conclusions, then grouped into three top-level categories with finer sub-topics (political news, populism, healthcare, business, emotion detection, etc.).

## Findings

- Distribution: 6 articles on the introduction of Reactions, 19 on politics/far-right, 39 on other social issues.
- Negative emotional appeals — especially anger — drove significantly more engagement and sharing in campaigns around Mexico's elections, Brexit, and EU Parliament elections.
- Populist and far-right leaders receive disproportionately more Angry and Haha reactions than mainstream politicians; anger-triggering posts get shared more.
- Hyperpartisan and junk news show a bivalent emotional logic: Love and Anger rarely co-occur, while Anger tends to co-occur with Sad and Wow.
- Reactions have been used computationally to predict emotion classes, controversy (via entropy of reaction distributions), and sentiment, though reaction-to-emotion mappings remain ambiguous (e.g., Wow as disbelief, Haha as mockery).
- Cross-linguistic studies (Sinhala, Bangla) show Reactions' utility as labels for sentiment analysis in low-resource languages.
- Cultural context shapes reaction patterns, e.g., across China-critical, China-supporting, and neutral audiences in Hong Kong.
