# Americans and AI 2026: Chatbots, Smart Devices and Views on Impact

> Jeffrey, G., William, B., Monica, A., Michelle, F., Eugenie, P., & Colleen, M. (2026). Americans and AI 2026: Chatbots, Smart Devices and Views on Impact.
>
> [View paper](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Americans%20and%20AI%202026%3A%20Chatbots%2C%20Smart%20Devices%20and%20Views%20on%20Impact)

## Summary

This Pew Research Center report presents results from a February 2026 nationally representative survey of 5,119 U.S. adults tracking use of and attitudes toward AI. It documents that AI has moved from novelty to embedded infrastructure — roughly half of adults now use AI chatbots, most encounter AI search summaries, and AI-enabled smart devices are common in American homes — yet public sentiment remains distinctly negative. Americans across age groups are more likely to expect harm than benefit from AI at both societal and personal levels, believe development is proceeding too fast, and distrust both government and industry to manage it responsibly. The central tension the report foregrounds is this widening gap between rising adoption and persistent skepticism.

## Key Contributions

- Updated 2026 benchmarks on chatbot adoption, use frequency, brand share (ChatGPT dominant at 44%), and everyday use cases.
- First Pew measures of AI-enabled smart home device ownership and exposure to AI search summaries.
- Documentation of a striking partisan realignment in confidence in AI regulation between 2024 and 2026.
- Detailed demographic breakdowns by age, gender, race/ethnicity, income, and party, including an Asian-adult oversample.
- A novel open-ended item capturing lay mental models of what "AI" refers to.
- Extension of Pew's 2022–2026 longitudinal series on AI awareness and use.

## Methods

Nationally representative survey of 5,119 U.S. adults (Wave 187 of Pew's American Trends Panel), fielded Feb 17–23, 2026, by SSRS via mixed-mode collection (4,930 web, 189 phone; English and Spanish). Sampling was address-based with a non-Hispanic Asian oversample; weights were calibrated to ACS, CPS, NPORS, and validated 2024 vote benchmarks. Margin of error is ±1.6 points for the full sample. An open-ended question about what technology comes to mind for "AI" was coded into categories, and trend comparisons draw on Pew surveys from 2022 through 2025.

## Findings

- 49% of U.S. adults use AI chatbots; 24% use them daily and 4% almost constantly.
- ChatGPT use has reached 44% (up from ~18% in 2023), followed by Gemini (~24%), Copilot, and Meta AI.
- Top uses: information search (~42%), work tasks (38% of employed), entertainment, image/video creation, and medical advice; 10% use chatbots for emotional support.
- ~33% own AI-enabled smart speakers; smaller shares own smart doorbells (18%), robot vacuums (13%), or smart thermostats (11%); 37% own a smartwatch; 60% read AI search summaries.
- 40% expect negative societal impact from AI over 20 years vs. 16% positive; 31% expect negative personal impact vs. 23% positive.
- 63% say AI advances too quickly; 71% expect AI to reduce personal information security.
- 67% lack confidence in government to regulate AI (up from 62% in 2024); ~60% lack confidence in industry self-governance.
- Partisan reversal: Democratic lack of confidence in regulation rose 20 points to 74%; Republican fell from 70% to 61%.
- Sharp age gradient: 60%+ chatbot use among under-50s vs. 23% among 65+.
- Asian adults are outliers: 70% chatbot use and 41% expecting positive personal impact — the only racial/ethnic group tilting net-positive.
- Persistent gender gap in views and frequency, though overall use rates have converged.
- Among non-users, 70% are unlikely to adopt within a year; top barriers are lack of interest (60%), privacy concerns (54%), and accuracy distrust (45%).

## Connections

This survey provides the population-level backdrop against which more focused studies of generative AI's media and civic effects operate — for example, work on AI-mediated persuasion such as [[Hackenburg2025-dj]] and [[Hackenburg2026-ud]], and research on public susceptibility to and correction of AI-related misinformation like [[Costello2024-bg]]. The documented distrust of AI accuracy and concern about information security also connects to studies of AI in news and journalism ecosystems such as [[Dierickx2026-tw]] and [[Hepp2026-oi]]. Most other papers in the topic cluster address narrower production or content questions and do not directly engage the public-opinion terrain mapped here.
