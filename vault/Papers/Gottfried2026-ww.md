# Americans and AI 2026: Chatbots, Smart Devices and Views on Impact

> Jeffrey, G., William, B., Monica, A., Michelle, F., Eugenie, P., & Colleen, M. (2026). Americans and AI 2026: Chatbots, Smart Devices and Views on Impact.
>
> [View paper](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Americans%20and%20AI%202026%3A%20Chatbots%2C%20Smart%20Devices%20and%20Views%20on%20Impact)

## Summary

This Pew Research Center report documents the state of U.S. public engagement with artificial intelligence as of early 2026, drawing on a nationally representative survey of 5,119 adults. It argues that AI has moved decisively from novelty to embedded infrastructure — roughly half of Americans now use chatbots and AI summaries are routinely encountered in search — yet public sentiment remains tilted negative across nearly every demographic group. The report's central tension is between accelerating adoption and persistent skepticism about safety, pace of development, and the capacity of government or industry to govern AI responsibly.

## Key Contributions

- Provides a 2026 benchmark on chatbot adoption, frequency, brand share, and use cases among U.S. adults.
- Introduces fresh measures of AI-enabled smart home device ownership and exposure to AI search summaries.
- Documents a notable partisan realignment in attitudes toward AI regulation between 2024 and 2026.
- Supplies fine-grained demographic breakdowns (age, gender, race/ethnicity, income, party).
- Captures lay mental models of AI via an open-ended "what comes to mind" question.
- Extends Pew's longitudinal trend series on AI awareness from 2022 through 2026.

## Methods

Nationally representative survey of 5,119 U.S. adults (February 17–23, 2026), fielded through Pew's American Trends Panel (Wave 187) in English and Spanish. Mixed-mode: 4,930 web and 189 telephone interviews, administered by SSRS, with address-based recruitment and an oversample of non-Hispanic Asian adults. Weighting calibrated against ACS, CPS, NPORS, and validated 2024 vote benchmarks. Margin of error ±1.6 points; trend comparisons drawn from Pew surveys in 2022–2025.

## Findings

- 49% of U.S. adults use AI chatbots; 24% use them daily and 4% almost constantly.
- ChatGPT reaches 44% of adults (up from ~18% in 2023), followed by Gemini (~24%), Copilot, and Meta AI.
- Top uses: information search (42%), work tasks (38% of employed), entertainment, image/video generation, and medical advice; 10% use chatbots for emotional support.
- 60% read AI summaries atop search results; ~33% own AI-enabled smart speakers; 37% own smartwatches.
- 40% expect AI's 20-year societal impact to be negative vs. 16% positive; 31% expect negative personal impact vs. 23% positive.
- 63% say AI is advancing too quickly; 71% expect it to weaken personal information security.
- 67% have little/no confidence in government to regulate AI; Democrats' distrust rose 20 points since 2024 (to 74%) while Republicans' fell from 70% to 61%.
- Sharp age gradient: 60%+ chatbot use under 50 vs. 23% among 65+.
- Asian adults are the only racial/ethnic group with net-positive views of AI's personal impact (41% positive); 70% use chatbots.
- Persistent gender gap in attitudes, with women more skeptical though use rates have converged.
- 96% have heard at least a little about AI; 48% have heard "a lot" (up from 26% in 2022).

## Connections

This survey provides population-level grounding for work on generative-AI's social embedding, complementing experimental and behavioral studies of chatbot influence such as [[Hackenburg2025-dj]] and [[Hackenburg2026-ud]] on persuasion, and [[Costello2024-bg]] on belief change through dialogue. The documented gap between heavy adoption and deep skepticism — especially around information security and regulatory trust — offers a public-opinion counterpart to platform- and discourse-level analyses like [[Hepp2026-oi]] and [[Manovich2026-ih]] of how generative AI is reshaping everyday media environments.
