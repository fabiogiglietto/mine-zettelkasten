# Disinformation as cultural narrative: Conceptualizing disinformation as cross-platform, identity-affirming, cathartic stories

> Marwick, A. E., Schnabel, E., McGregor, S., & Schmitt, C. (2026). Disinformation as cultural narrative: Conceptualizing disinformation as cross-platform, identity-affirming, cathartic stories. *Political Communication*, 1–25. https://doi.org/10.1080/10584609.2026.2644198
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1080/10584609.2026.2644198)

## Summary

Despite a title invoking disinformation as cultural narrative, this reflective essay is actually an autobiographical meditation on the lost intimacy of pre-platform internet sociality. Marwick draws on two decades of personal experience — Prodigy, IRC, university VAX/VMS bulletin boards, and especially LiveJournal in the early 2000s — to argue that today's commercial platform ecosystem has foreclosed the reciprocal, semi-private, subcultural forms of community that once flourished online. The current state of social media, she contends, was not inevitable but the product of specific political-economic and design choices privileging scale, advertising, surveillance, and virality over stewardship, safety, and care.

## Key Contributions

- A first-person historiographic account of pre-platform internet sociality, contributing to the cultural memory of LiveJournal and adjacent communities.
- A normative critique of platform political economy, arguing that social media should have been treated as infrastructure requiring stewardship rather than as an engagement-optimized commercial product.
- An imaginative provocation: future online community will not come from rebuilding mega-platforms (e.g., Bluesky-as-Twitter) but from infrastructures supporting reciprocity, vulnerability, and small-scale sociality.
- Adds to a growing reframing of social media as social infrastructure rather than product.

## Methods

Critical-essayistic reflection rather than empirical study. Marwick uses autobiographical fieldnotes and historical recollection of specific early platforms, situating her personal experience within broader debates in platform studies and critical political economy.

## Findings

- LiveJournal's granular friends-only privacy filters enabled audience management, trust, and vulnerability that current platforms structurally inhibit.
- Sustained reciprocal writing on LiveJournal produced lifelong friendships and romantic partnerships — affective outcomes rarely afforded by today's platforms.
- The intimacy of early internet culture was tied to its subcultural smallness and niche user bases, conditions difficult to reproduce at platform scale.
- Contemporary platforms generate harassment, polarization, and corporate capture, pushing users toward ambient, ephemeral, or encrypted-chat communication as refuge.
