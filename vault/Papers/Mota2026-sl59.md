# Weapon, wonder or both? Political framings of AI in global north and south media

> Mota, B. M. F. (2026). Weapon, wonder or both? Political framings of AI in global north and south media. *Information, Communication & Society*. https://doi.org/10.1080/1369118x.2026.2691775
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1080/1369118x.2026.2691775)

## Summary

This paper offers a large-scale comparative analysis of how mainstream English-language news media in the Global North and Global South politically frame artificial intelligence between 2010 and 2024. Combining LDA topic modelling and multi-lexicon sentiment analysis on 6,829 articles from twelve newspapers, the author argues that AI should be read as political infrastructure rather than a mere technical artifact, and shows that Northern outlets cover AI through a pessimistic, risk-oriented lens while Southern outlets foreground developmental opportunity. The framing divergence reflects deeper geopolitical asymmetries — particularly US–China rivalry and infrastructural dependence on Big Tech — and reconfigures questions of sovereignty and democratic accountability.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic comparative study of political AI framings across Global North/South news outlets.
- A reproducible mixed-method computational pipeline applied to a 15-year, 12-outlet corpus, with open data and scripts.
- Empirical periodisation of AI-politics coverage into incipient (2010–2016), crisis-politicisation (2017–2021), and exponential acceleration (2022–2024) phases.
- Reframes "AI coverage" research toward AI as a site of political contestation involving corporate power, geopolitics, and governance.
- Theoretical extension of "risk entanglement" to a *state-platform* configuration linking infrastructural dependence and regulatory pressure.

## Methods

- Corpus of 6,829 English-language articles retrieved from LexisNexis using AI- and politics-related keyword combinations, drawn from twelve newspapers spanning Europe, North America, Africa, Asia, and Oceania.
- LDA topic modelling (Gibbs sampling, α=δ=0.1, 5,000 iterations), with 15 topics selected via Arun, Cao, Deveaud, and Griffiths–Steyvers metrics; topics interpreted using bigram coherence.
- Headline sentiment analysis triangulating Bing, sentimentr, NRC, and AFINN lexicons, with Mann–Whitney non-parametric testing of North–South differences.
- Comparative interpretation grounded in Hallin and Mancini's media systems framework, framing theory, and risk-society scholarship.

## Findings

- Article volume rose ~567% from 2022 to 2024; Cambridge Analytica (2018) and ChatGPT (2022) mark major inflection points.
- Fifteen frames identified; the top five (Social Media/Tech Impact, Digital Economy, China–US Relations, Tech Companies/Innovation, AI and Society) account for over 60% of coverage.
- Geopolitical frames make up ~21% of coverage, signalling AI's centrality to a new technological cold war narrative.
- Global North headlines skew negative (41.7% negative vs. 37.8% positive; AFINN mean −0.0113); *The Guardian* is most negative (68.15%).
- Global South headlines skew positive (51.5% vs. 22.7%; AFINN mean +0.102); *China Daily* is most positive (71.01%).
- Mann–Whitney test confirms a statistically significant North–South sentiment gap (p<0.001).
- NRC emotions: North combines fear (17.85%) with trust (21.03%); South emphasises anticipation (16.90%).
- Outlet-level specialisation: digital economy frames dominate in *The Korea Herald* and *Toronto Star*; geopolitics in *The Straits Times*; US domestic politics in *The New York Times*.
- Post-2022 discourse expands from present harms (bias, disinformation, unemployment) to existential/AGI risks amplified by elite open letters.
