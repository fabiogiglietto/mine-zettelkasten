# When do parties lie? Misinformation and radical-right populism across 26 countries

## Summary

Note: the supplied title ("When do parties lie?...") does not match the paper's actual content, which is "Towards a Post-Social Media Studies" by Petter Törnberg and Richard Rogers. This conceptual paper argues that the analytical paradigm of "social media" — built around user-generated content circulating through social graphs — is unraveling and must be replaced by what the authors call *post-social media studies*. Three interlocking shifts drive this transformation: the move from social-graph to interest-based algorithmic recommendation, the rise of generative AI as a producer of synthetic content, and a retreat from public platforms into private and semi-private spaces. The result is a new "user-spectator" whose dominant mode is passive watching, and three emergent formations — algorithmic broadcasting platforms, semi-private micro-communities, and AI-mediated communication — each demanding new vocabularies, methods, and normative frameworks.

## Key Contributions

- Proposes "post-social media studies" as a programmatic research agenda succeeding two decades of social media scholarship.
- Articulates a tripartite typology of emergent formations: algorithmic broadcasting platforms, semi-private spheres/micro-communities, and AI-mediated communication.
- Introduces and synthesizes concepts including *algorithmic publics*, *imitation publics*, and the *user-spectator* to replace *networked publics*.
- Maps distinct methodological reorientations to each formation — flow analysis and rhythmanalysis, ethnographic/design-analytic methods, and AI auditing.
- Frames the democratic stakes of communicative infrastructures that no longer reliably produce shared publics or stable texts.

## Methods

Conceptual and theoretical synthesis rather than original empirical work. The authors integrate platform studies, communication theory, digital sociology, and human–machine communication scholarship, and draw illustratively on secondary evidence — Meta antitrust filings showing only 7% (Instagram) and 17% (Facebook) of time on followed content, the reorientation of TikTok/Meta/YouTube toward recommendation, and survey statistics on AI chatbot adoption — to motivate a comparative analytical framework.

## Findings

- Major platforms have shifted from social-graph distribution to algorithmic recommendation driven by inferred interest and passive attention signals (watch time, dwell time, completion rate).
- Active participation on legacy public platforms has dropped roughly 50% on Facebook and Twitter/X between 2020 and 2024.
- AI chatbot use (52% of U.S. adults in 2025) now exceeds posting activity on Facebook (35%), Instagram (20%), and Twitter/X (11%).
- Generative AI lets platforms populate feeds with synthetic content (e.g., "Shrimp Jesus"), reducing dependence on user-generated material.
- Communicative practice is migrating to semi-private spheres (messaging groups, Substack, invite-only communities) organized around trust rather than visibility.
- AI chatbots constitute a media form *without publics*: communication is individualized, generative, and ephemeral, leaving no shared trace.
- Each formation poses distinct research challenges: algorithmic opacity, access/ethics, and absence of shared texts respectively.
