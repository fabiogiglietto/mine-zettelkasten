# When do parties lie? Misinformation and radical-right populism across 26 countries

> Törnberg, P., & Chueri, J. (2026). When do parties lie? Misinformation and radical-right populism across 26 countries. *The International Journal of Press/Politics*, 19401612241311886. https://doi.org/10.1177/19401612241311886
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1177/19401612241311886)

## Summary

This conceptual paper argues that the "social media" paradigm — organized around user-generated content circulating through social graphs — is unraveling and must be succeeded by what the authors call "post-social media studies." Törnberg and Rogers identify three converging dynamics dissolving the old paradigm: a platform shift from social-graph to interest-based algorithmic recommendation, the rise of generative AI as a substitute producer of content, and a retreat from public feeds into semi-private spaces. These yield three emergent formations — algorithmic broadcasting platforms, semi-private spheres and micro-communities, and AI-mediated communication — each demanding new conceptual vocabularies, methods, and normative frameworks. The piece is programmatic, calling for a paradigm shift comparable in scope to the original emergence of social media studies.

(Note: the metadata title supplied with this entry — "When do parties lie? Misinformation and radical-right populism across 26 countries" — does not match the actual paper, which is "Towards a Post-Social Media Studies" by Petter Törnberg and Richard Rogers.)

## Key Contributions

- Proposes "post-social media studies" as a research agenda for digital communication after the unraveling of the social media paradigm.
- Articulates a typology of three emergent post-social formations: algorithmic broadcasting platforms, semi-private spheres, and AI-mediated communication.
- Introduces and synthesizes concepts including "algorithmic publics," "imitation publics," and the "user-spectator" / "algorithmic viewer" to replace "networked publics."
- Maps methodological reorientations to each formation: flow analysis and rhythmanalysis for broadcasting platforms; ethnographic and design-analytic approaches for semi-private spheres; AI auditing for chatbot-mediated communication.
- Frames the democratic stakes of communicative infrastructures that no longer reliably generate shared publics or stable texts.

## Methods

Conceptual synthesis rather than original empirical work. The authors integrate platform studies, communication theory, digital sociology, and human–machine communication scholarship, and draw on secondary evidence — including Meta antitrust filings (only 7% of Instagram and 17% of Facebook time spent on followed content), industry shifts (TikTok's For You Page, Meta and YouTube reorientations), and survey data on AI chatbot adoption — to motivate a comparative analytical framework distinguishing the three emergent formations.

## Findings

- Major platforms have reoriented from social-graph distribution to interest-based recommendation tuned to passive attention signals (watch time, dwell time, completion rate).
- Active participation on legacy public platforms has dropped sharply (~50% decline in posting/viewing on Facebook and Twitter/X between 2020 and 2024, by the authors' citation).
- AI chatbot use (52% of U.S. adults in 2025) now exceeds posting activity on Facebook (35%), Instagram (20%), and Twitter/X (11%).
- Generative AI enables platforms to populate feeds with synthetic content (e.g., the "Shrimp Jesus" phenomenon), reducing reliance on user-generated content.
- Communication is migrating to semi-private spheres — messaging groups, Substack, invite-only communities — organized around trust and reciprocity rather than visibility and scale.
- Each formation poses distinct research challenges: algorithmic opacity for broadcasting platforms, access and ethics for semi-private spheres, and the absence of shared texts and publics for AI-mediated communication.
- AI chatbots constitute a "media form without publics": individualized, generative, ephemeral exchanges that leave no shared trace.
