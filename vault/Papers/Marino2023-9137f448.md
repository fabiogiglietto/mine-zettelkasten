# The power of Alternative Influence Networks (AIN) for spreading Covid-19 problematic information on Facebook during a year of pandemic

> Marino, F. G. G. (2023). The power of Alternative Influence Networks (AIN) for spreading Covid-19 problematic information on Facebook during a year of pandemic. https://doi.org/10.1445/106772
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1445/106772)

## Summary

This paper maps the Italian "Alternative Influence Network" (AIN) ecosystem on Facebook during the first year of the Covid-19 pandemic, asking how covid-skeptic accounts amplified problematic information by mentioning specific public figures. Building on prior work that detected coordinated link-sharing behavior, the authors extract and cluster the public figures most frequently named in 175,228 posts and analyze the engagement signatures and content surrounding them. They argue that a distinct "Intellectual Dark Web" (IDW) subnetwork — composed of contrarian intellectuals, dissident scientists, and alternative micro-celebrities — produces highly shareable problematic content, but that legacy media (mainstream-affiliated journalists' pages, right-leaning newspapers) remain the central amplifiers. The key mechanism, they contend, is remediation: AIN actors recycle expert statements from TV talk shows and newspapers to launder covid-skeptic and conspiratorial framings while sidestepping fact-checking.

## Key Contributions

- Empirical mapping of the Italian Covid-19 AIN, identifying a distinct IDW cluster with its own engagement signature.
- Operationalizes a comments/shares ratio metric to distinguish hyperpartisan amplification (share-heavy) from contested debate (comment-heavy).
- Extends remediation theory to alternative media, showing how legacy-produced expert statements get repurposed to bolster problematic narratives.
- Contributes to inter-media agenda-setting research by documenting AIN–alternative–legacy co-production of news visibility.
- Offers a replicable mixed-methods workflow combining coordinated-behavior detection, NER, clustering, engagement analysis, and stratified content analysis.

## Methods

The authors collected 175,228 Facebook posts (March 2020–March 2021) from a previously identified list of covid-skeptic Italian accounts via CrowdTangle. They extracted capitalized proper nouns across message, title, description, and image text fields, selected the top 2,000 terms, and manually classified 254 last names into five non-exclusive categories (Alternative Influencers, Doctors/Scientists, Journalists, Other Public Figures, Politicians). After filtering out figures holding apical public roles, 79 figures were submitted to k-means clustering (five clusters). Engagement statistics and a comments/shares ratio (range −1 to 1) were computed per cluster, followed by deductive content analysis of a stratified sample of 150 posts from the top 10% most-engaging content.

## Findings

- Five clusters emerged: Politicians, Other Public Figures, Journalists, Doctors/Scientists/Experts, and IDW Influencers.
- IDW Influencers showed the lowest comments/shares ratio (−0.28), indicating share-driven amplification; Doctors/Scientists also skewed share-heavy (−0.21).
- Journalists' posts had the highest ratio (+0.16), signaling more contested/commented engagement.
- Politicians dominated by post volume (9,879) but achieved moderate engagement; IDW Influencers (1,649 posts) and Other Public Figures (1,958) achieved high mean interactions (1,030 and 983 respectively) despite low volume.
- Top amplifying pages were dominated by mainstream-affiliated journalists (Nicola Porro, Gianluigi Paragone) and right-wing newspapers (La Verità, Libero); covid-skeptic micro-celebrities (Montanari, Messora) ranked lower.
- Recurring content themes: anti-immigration and anti-government framings (Politicians); judicial scandals (Other Public Figures); covid-skepticism quoting figures like Zangrillo or Tarro (Doctors/Scientists); microchip and global-tyranny conspiracies (IDW), often sourced from TV talk show clips.
