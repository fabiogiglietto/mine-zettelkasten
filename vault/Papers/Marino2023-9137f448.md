# The power of Alternative Influence Networks (AIN) for spreading Covid-19 problematic information on Facebook during a year of pandemic

> Marino, F. G. G. (2023). The power of Alternative Influence Networks (AIN) for spreading Covid-19 problematic information on Facebook during a year of pandemic. https://doi.org/10.1445/106772
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1445/106772)

## Summary

This paper investigates how Alternative Influence Networks (AIN) circulated Covid-19 problematic information on Italian Facebook during the first year of the pandemic. Building on a previously identified set of covid-skeptic accounts detected via coordinated link-sharing behavior, the authors analyze 175,228 posts to identify which public figures are mentioned, cluster them by type, and measure engagement patterns. They argue that a distinct "Intellectual Dark Web" (IDW) cluster produces disproportionately shareable content, and that legacy media — particularly mainstream journalists and right-leaning newspapers — remain the central conduits amplifying these voices. The central theoretical claim is that remediation of expert statements from TV and newspapers is a key mechanism through which AIN actors launder problematic narratives past fact-checkers.

## Key Contributions

- Empirically maps the Italian AIN during Covid-19, isolating an IDW subnetwork with distinct engagement signatures.
- Demonstrates the analytical utility of a comments/shares ratio metric (range -1 to 1) for distinguishing hyperpartisan amplification from contested debate.
- Extends Bolter & Grusin's remediation theory to alternative media, showing how AIN recycle legacy-media expert content to bolster covid-skeptic narratives.
- Contributes to inter-media agenda-setting research by documenting AIN–alternative outlet–legacy media co-production of news visibility.
- Offers a replicable mixed-methods workflow: coordinated-behavior detection → named entity extraction → clustering → engagement metrics → stratified content analysis.

## Methods

Posts were collected via CrowdTangle from a pre-identified set of Italian covid-skeptic accounts (March 2020–March 2021). Capitalized proper nouns were extracted from message, title, description, and image-text fields; the top 2,000 terms were reduced to 254 manually classified last names across five non-exclusive categories (Alternative Influencers, Doctors/Scientists/Experts, Journalists, Other Public Figures, Politicians). After filtering figures in apical public roles, k-means clustering of the remaining 79 figures produced five clusters. Engagement statistics and a comments/shares ratio were computed per cluster, and a deductive content analysis was performed on a stratified random sample of 150 top-decile posts.

## Findings

- Five clusters: Politicians, Other Public Figures, Journalists, Doctors/Scientists/Experts, IDW Influencers.
- IDW Influencers had the lowest comments/shares ratio (-0.28), signaling share-driven amplification; Doctors/Scientists/Experts also skewed toward shares (-0.21).
- Journalists' posts had the highest ratio (+0.16), indicating more contested/commented content; Politicians were most prolific (9,879 posts) with moderate engagement.
- Despite low volume, IDW Influencers (1,649 posts) and Other Public Figures (1,958 posts) achieved high mean interactions (1,030 and 983).
- Top amplifying pages were dominated by mainstream-affiliated journalists (Porro, Paragone) and right-wing newspapers (La Verità, Libero), with covid-skeptic micro-celebrities (Montanari, Messora) appearing further down.
- Themes: anti-immigration and anti-government frames in political clusters; judicial scandals among Other Public Figures; covid-skepticism quoting figures like Zangrillo or Tarro; conspiracy narratives (microchips, global tyranny) in the IDW cluster, often clipped from TV talk shows.
