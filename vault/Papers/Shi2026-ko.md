# The Governance-Embedded Interactive Media Effect: the role of AI-generated disclosure in user credibility and engagement based on fact-checking videos on Chinese TikTok (Douyin)

> Shi, Y. (2026). The Governance-Embedded Interactive Media Effect: the role of AI-generated disclosure in user credibility and engagement based on fact-checking videos on Chinese TikTok (Douyin). *Chinese Journal of Communication*, 1–16. https://doi.org/10.1080/17544750.2026.2623075
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1080/17544750.2026.2623075)

## Summary

Despite the title supplied, the structured summary describes a qualitative interview study of European professional fact-checkers and how they rhetorically defend their funding relationships with platform companies such as Meta, Alphabet, and ByteDance. Drawing on Ware and Linkugel's apologia theory in combination with boundary-work theory, the paper argues that fact-checkers rely overwhelmingly on *transformative* rhetorical strategies — differentiation and transcendence — rather than denial or bolstering, to assert editorial independence while acknowledging material dependence on platforms. These defences are reframed as boundary work that negotiates fact-checking's contested epistemic authority in a platform-dependent media ecosystem.

## Key Contributions

- First application of rhetorical apologia theory to fact-checkers' defences of platform partnerships.
- A typology of four modes of differentiation and three modes of transcendence, portable to adjacent journalism contexts.
- Extends prior work on internal boundaries within fact-checking by foregrounding the external boundary between fact-checkers and platforms.
- Addresses a geographic gap by centring European fact-checkers, who are under-studied relative to North American peers.
- Integrates apologia theory with boundary-work theory (the "wall" and "membrane" metaphors) to give finer-grained traction on rhetorical autonomy claims.

## Methods

- 12 semi-structured interviews (Nov 2023 – Mar 2024) with one fact-checker from each of 12 European organisations spanning Czechia, Estonia, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Latvia, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Slovakia, Spain, and Sweden.
- All organisations were EFCSN- and IFCN-certified and had received platform funding between 2021 and early 2024.
- An interview guide covered organisational structure, audience, societal role, editorial independence, potentials and challenges, and the role of platform companies; mean interview length ~71 minutes.
- Automated then manual transcription (~107,500 words), with four-phase NVivo coding: inductive themes → theme-based coding → application of the Ware–Linkugel apologia framework → quote grouping.
- Interviewees and organisations were anonymised to protect ongoing partnerships and reduce harassment risk.

## Findings

- **Differentiation modes:**
    - **D1** Fact-checking organisations vs. platform companies (asserting independence).
    - **D2** Among platforms — frequent rejection of TikTok/ByteDance while accepting Meta or Alphabet.
    - **D3** Platforms-as-institutions vs. specific well-intentioned employees or units.
    - **D4** Platform funding vs. state funding, with the former defended as less compromising.
- **Transcendence modes:**
    - **T1** Counterfactual appeal — without fact-checkers, misinformation would spread unchecked.
    - **T2** Appeal to broad anti-disinformation alliances ("we can't fight alone").
    - **T3** Appeal to the possibility of reforming platforms from within.
- Funding distribution in the sample: Meta (9), Alphabet (8), ByteDance/TikTok (2), Twitter (2), Spotify/Kinzen (2).
- Fact-checkers report routine harassment and accusations of censorship, collusion, or "globalist" service, often citing platform or Soros-linked funding as supposed proof.
- Boundary-drawing is inconsistent across organisations — some refuse TikTok or state money, others accept both — so defences sometimes implicitly criticise peers, signalling an emergent, unsettled field.
