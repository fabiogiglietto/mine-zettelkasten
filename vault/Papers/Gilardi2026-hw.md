# Quality perceptions and intended engagement in response to AI-generated and AI-assisted news

> Gilardi, F., Lorenzo, S. D., Ezzaini, J., Santa, B., Streiff, B., Zurfluh, E., & Hoes, E. (2026). Quality perceptions and intended engagement in response to AI-generated and AI-assisted news. *Scientific Reports*, 1–15. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-58743-0
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-58743-0)

## Summary

This preregistered survey experiment (N = 599, German-speaking Switzerland) tests whether readers can distinguish the quality of human-written, AI-assisted, and fully AI-generated news excerpts, and how disclosure of AI involvement shifts their intended engagement. Participants rated credibility, readability, and expertise blind to authorship, then were told how each article was produced and reported willingness to continue reading and to consume AI news in the future. The authors find that perceived quality is statistically equivalent across conditions, but disclosure of AI involvement paradoxically raises willingness to continue reading the specific article — an effect the authors interpret as curiosity or scrutiny rather than genuine acceptance, since it does not carry over to general future intentions.

## Key Contributions

- Provides current LLM-era evidence (GPT-4 Turbo stimuli) that AI-produced news is judged equivalent in quality to human-written journalism on credibility, readability, and expertise.
- Introduces a two-stage design that separates blind quality assessment from post-disclosure engagement, isolating source effects from message evaluation.
- Distinguishes short-term, curiosity-driven engagement from durable reading intentions, complicating the transparency-erodes-trust narrative.
- Extends work on algorithmic aversion and the "automation heuristic" into the generative AI era and to a Swiss German-language context.
- Publishes preregistered stimuli, equivalence tests, and robustness routines usable as a template for AI-journalism experiments.

## Methods

Between-subjects online experiment with three conditions: original SRF (Swiss public broadcaster) political news excerpts, ChatGPT-rewritten (AI-assisted) versions, and fully AI-generated versions produced from only title and lead. Participants read two 150-word excerpts with attention checks, rated quality on Sundar (1999)-derived Likert items before disclosure, then reported engagement intentions after learning the article's origin. Analysis used linear regression with demographic covariates, Benjamini–Hochberg-corrected pairwise contrasts, Cohen's d, η²/ω², and TOST equivalence tests (SESOI = ±0.20), with robustness checks using article-pair fixed effects and an aggregate quality index (α = 0.79).

## Findings

- No significant differences in expertise, readability, or credibility across conditions (omnibus p > .45; η² ≤ .003; pairwise d between −0.05 and 0.11); H1–H6 rejected.
- Equivalence tests confirmed statistical equivalence for all quality outcomes.
- AI-assisted (d = 0.53) and AI-generated (d = 0.61) conditions both produced significantly higher willingness to continue reading the presented article than the human-written control (both p < .001).
- No differences between AI-assisted and AI-generated conditions on any measure.
- Willingness to read AI-generated news in the future did not differ across conditions (H9–H10 rejected), and equivalence could not be established here — small effects remain possible.
