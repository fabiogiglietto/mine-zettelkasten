# Moving towards a socially-driven internet architectural design

> Sofia, R., Mendes, P., Damásio, M. J., Henriques, S., Giglietto, F., Giambitto, E., & Bogliolo, A. (2012). Moving towards a socially-driven internet architectural design. *ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review*. https://doi.org/10.1145/2317307.2317316
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1145/2317307.2317316)

## Summary

This position paper argues that Internet architectural design should be reframed as an interdisciplinary, socially-driven endeavor that integrates social capital theory with networking research. The authors trace the user's evolution from passive consumer to *prosumer* and ultimately to *network prosumer* — a role embodied in User-centric Networks (UCNs) where end users share and operate connectivity resources at lower OSI layers. They diagnose the current Internet supply chain as structurally asymmetric, with traffic-growth benefits unevenly distributed across its seven stages, and propose a symmetric variant that explicitly incorporates prosumers and UCNs. The paper further contends that social capital properties — trust, influence, reach, and engagement — should be operationalized as networking metrics, and that mismatches between social and networking interpretations of centrality must be resolved.

## Key Contributions

- A vision and set of design guidelines for a socially-driven Internet architecture that blends social capital theory with networking design.
- Extension of the prosumer concept into lower network layers via the "network prosumer" notion, grounded in User-centric Networks.
- A symmetric Internet supply-chain model that explicitly includes prosumers and UCNs as stages, contrasted with the prevailing asymmetric model.
- A comparative mapping between social capital centrality concepts (degree, betweenness, closeness) and their networking analogues, surfacing specific misalignments.
- Identification of reach, engagement, and influence as the social properties most readily operationalizable into candidate networking metrics, especially for pervasive and opportunistic routing.

## Methods

Conceptual, interdisciplinary position paper. The authors synthesize literature from networking research projects (GENI, EIFFEL, SocialNets, ULOOP) and social capital theorists (Bourdieu, Coleman, Putnam, Burt). They use the seven-stage Internet supply-chain model from Pigliapoco and Bogliolo as their diagnostic framework, perform a comparative analysis of centrality definitions across the two fields (summarized in a comparison table), and outline a socially-driven design cycle connecting social media, open data, social behavior models, and emerging media/business practices.

## Findings

- Vertical integration by operators clashes with the modular TCP/IP stack, constraining innovation and producing cost/price misalignment.
- Within the seven-stage supply chain, stages 2, 3, 5, and 7 capture gains from traffic growth, whereas stages 1, 4, and 6 stagnate due to weak price signals.
- UCNs rest on four properties — resource sharing, cooperation, trust, and self-organization — and push prosumer behavior down the protocol stack.
- Centrality concepts diverge across disciplines: high-betweenness nodes function as inter-cluster *bridgers* socially but as mere relays in networking; high-closeness nodes imply efficient diffusion socially yet often become bottlenecks in networks.
- The bridger role and the transitivity of strong ties are not adequately captured by current pervasive networking models.
- Prosumer activity exhibits power-law characteristics, indicating that consumer and prosumer modes coexist rather than supplant one another.
- Social capital can be modeled at individual or collective levels, with trust and reciprocity central to Putnam's collective framing.
