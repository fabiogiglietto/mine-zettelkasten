This paper investigates how Brazilian federal deputies used Twitter/X to contest narrative authority during the collapse of Manaus's healthcare system in late 2020 and early 2021. Drawing on nearly 48,000 tweets from 450 deputies and combining network analysis with qualitative and quantitative content analysis, the authors identify three distinct modes through which politicians stake claims to epistemic authority: objectivity (opposition deputies engaging in "derivative reporting" by repackaging journalistic content), authority (Bolsonaro allies bypassing the press to relay state communications directly), and proximity (local Amazonas politicians foregrounding firsthand brokerage with constituents). The paper extends gatekeeping theory by showing how political elites compete with journalists for gatekeeping positions in hybrid media environments.

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the concept of **derivative reporting** — politicians curating and repackaging news content (with journalistic aesthetics like logos and headlines) to borrow press credibility for partisan ends.
- Develops a typology of three epistemic claims in political crisis communication: **objectivity, authority, and proximity**.
- Provides rare empirical evidence on Latin American (Brazilian) parliamentarians' relationships to news media during a crisis.
- Demonstrates how network analysis of @mentions can map political alliances in fragmented multiparty systems.
- Extends networked gatekeeping theory to show how non-journalistic actors contest gatekeeping through distinct epistemic strategies.

## Methods

The authors collected 47,728 tweets from 450 federal deputies (88% of the Chamber) between December 2020 and March 2021, identifying Manaus-related posts via word embeddings and pointwise mutual information. Network analysis in Gephi (eigenvector centrality, ForceAtlas2, Louvain community detection) identified pro-government, anti-government, and Amazonas-local clusters. The top 10 central accounts in each cluster were content-analyzed: an inductive codebook captured praise, blame, and themes, with two coders reaching 98% agreement (Brennan-Prediger κ = 0.96) across 4,427 posts. Qualitative textual analysis examined language of blame, friend/enemy construction, and credibility. The framework is described as "computational grounded theory."

## Findings

- The network was sharply polarized into a red anti-government cluster (1,168 nodes, 77 deputies, 17,719 tweets) and a smaller but more engaged blue pro-government cluster (824 nodes, 126 deputies); density was extremely low (0.001).
- 41% of red-cluster tweets blamed the Bolsonaro administration; 10% called for impeachment, emphasizing deaths, oxygen shortages, and scientific authority.
- 24.4% of blue-cluster tweets praised Bolsonaro while deflecting responsibility to the Supreme Court and state-level corruption.
- Amazonas local deputies rarely blamed (9%) or praised (5%); 13.2% publicized their own crisis-response actions, performing brokerage rather than partisan combat.
- Six of the top ten URLs shared by the red cluster were news outlets; the blue cluster shared almost no news, linking instead to video platforms and political accounts.
- Red-cluster politicians mimicked journalistic visual formats and ideologically reframed press content (e.g., recasting ambiguous polls as impeachment evidence).
- Blue-cluster politicians attacked mainstream media credibility, branded relayed state content with their own logos, and contested platform moderation decisions.
