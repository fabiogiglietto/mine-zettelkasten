# ‘Us’ not ‘them’: scientists must use their skills to help stop polarization and division

> (2026). ‘Us’ not ‘them’: scientists must use their skills to help stop polarization and division. *Nature*. https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-026-01952-4
>
> [View paper](https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-026-01952-4)

## Summary

This Nature editorial argues that researchers studying social division have not merely an analytic interest but a civic obligation to design and test interventions that reduce polarization. Synthesizing recent work in political science, social psychology, and urban sociology, it spotlights one promising mechanism — reframing policy questions in "proximate," personal terms rather than abstract ideological ones — as a way to scramble predictable partisan responses. The piece situates this depolarization agenda within wider concerns about political violence, militarization of research budgets, and threats to international scientific collaboration.

## Key Contributions

- Reframes scientists studying polarization as active agents in countering it, not just observers.
- Foregrounds *proximate framing* of policies as a candidate depolarization mechanism warranting systematic study.
- Links depolarization research to debates over science-for-diplomacy, research funding, and political interference.
- Offers an accessible entry point to recent empirical and review literature on conflict reduction.

## Methods

Editorial commentary rather than original empirical work. It draws on a Canadian survey of 4,000 liberal- and conservative-identified respondents (Lucas et al.) comparing generic versus personally framed local-policy questions, a 2024 *Nature Reviews Psychology* review by Čehajić-Clancy and Halperin on conflict and depolarization, and Zhelnina's 2022 ethnographic study of Moscow residents resisting building demolition.

## Findings

- Generic policy framings elicited ideologically predictable responses; proximate framings produced ideologically mixed answers (e.g., liberals tolerating local security cameras, conservatives supporting immigrant services in their own municipality).
- In Moscow, politically opposed neighbors cooperated across ideological lines when organizing against demolition of their housing — a shared, concrete stake overrode partisan identity.
- Survey-stated intentions may not translate into behavior, and interventions that work in one context may not generalize, so rigorous cross-context replication is needed.
- Depolarization intervention research remains nascent and under-evaluated.

## Connections

The editorial's emphasis on personal, proximate framings as a depolarization lever connects to experimental work on online interventions and contact effects such as [[Mosleh2024-op]] and [[Brady2026-ln]], and to broader diagnoses of affective and behavioral polarization in [[Knupfer2025-vt]] and [[Hartmann2025-px]]. Its concern that ideological sorting suppresses cross-cutting interaction echoes findings on exposure and platform dynamics in [[Bakshy2015-rn]] and [[Esau2025-tf]]. Most other papers in this topic cluster focus on hybrid-media mechanisms of polarization rather than interventions, so the connection there is thematic adjacency rather than direct intellectual lineage.
